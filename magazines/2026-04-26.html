01 — Research Insights of the Day

Testing Theories of Political Persuasion Using AI

Kobi Hackenburg, Matt Tappin, Matz et al. · Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS)

Political microtargeting — the practice of tailoring messages to individuals based on psychological profiles and behavioral data — is widely assumed to be a powerful tool for campaigns. This study tested that assumption rigorously using GPT-4 to generate both personalized and generic persuasive messages, then measuring their effects on voter attitudes across multiple experiments. The headline result upends a core assumption of modern digital campaigning: customized, psychologically profiled messages were not statistically more persuasive than simple, well-crafted generic arguments. The researchers found that if microtargeting provides any advantage at all, it is extremely small. The persuasive power of large language models, they conclude, resides primarily in their ability to produce compelling generic text — not in their capacity to tailor messages to individuals. A companion experiment showed that even when participants moderated their policy opinions after exposure to tailored arguments, this shift rarely translated into increased respect for opposing viewpoints.

Practitioner Implication

Campaigns investing heavily in AI-powered microtargeting may be misallocating resources. The evidence suggests that a single, well-written generic message performs as well as an expensive personalized pipeline — a finding that should redirect budgets toward message quality over audience segmentation.

PNAS, 2025 · PNAS Nexus, 2024 · PsyPost, March 2026

Trump’s Overall Approval Rating — Lowest of Either Term

President Trump’s approval has plunged to the lowest point of his two terms in office, weighed down by surging gas prices, the Iran conflict, and persistent inflation. A Verasight/G. Elliott Morris survey pegged his approval at 35 percent, with his net rating on prices and inflation falling to −46 — the worst monthly reading of 2026, down from −31 in January, −35 in February, and −40 in March. A CNBC survey confirmed his economic approval at a career-low 31 percent, and Fox News’ latest poll shows Democrats leading Republicans on the economy by four points — the first time the GOP has trailed on its strongest issue since 2010. Nearly two-thirds of Americans say gas prices, now averaging over $4 per gallon nationally, are a problem for them. A Reuters/Ipsos poll found 78 percent of voters call gas prices a “very big concern,” and 77 percent blame Trump directly. With eight of nine quality polls in the past month placing Trump in the 30s, the president enters midterm season in historically dangerous territory for his party.

04 — Global Elections

Gaza Votes for the First Time in 20 Years

Palestinians went to the polls on April 25 in municipal elections that included, for the first time since 2006, a city in Gaza. In Deir al-Balah, in central Gaza, voters cast ballots under extraordinary circumstances — marking the first election of any kind in the territory since Hamas took control nearly two decades ago. The inclusion of a Gaza municipality was a significant step for the Palestinian Authority, which has sought to reassert its claim over the strip after being driven out by Hamas in 2007. Officials framed the vote as progress toward political reunification between the West Bank and Gaza. Diplomats noted that transparent local elections could serve as a step toward rebuilding institutions and restoring public trust — both key components of the U.S.-brokered ceasefire framework. However, turnout in Deir al-Balah reached only 22.7 percent, compared with 53.4 percent in the West Bank, reflecting the deep challenges Gaza faces. Al Jazeera’s analysis cautioned that “elections without sovereignty” risk legitimizing occupation rather than advancing self-determination. In the absence of national elections for nearly two decades, these local polls have become a barometer of political sentiment, testing the resilience of Palestinian institutions under extraordinary pressure.

Al Jazeera · CNN · PBS · Euronews · April 25, 2026

05 — Global Elections

Gujarat: 42 Million Voters Shape India’s Grassroots Battleground

Polling stations opened at 7 a.m. across Gujarat on April 26 for the state’s largest local body elections in recent history. Over 4.18 crore (42 million) eligible voters are casting ballots across nearly 50,000 polling stations to elect 9,992 representatives — spanning 15 municipal corporations, 84 municipalities, 34 district panchayats, and 260 taluka panchayats. More than 26,000 candidates are in the running across all categories. The Gujarat State Election Commission has introduced a new multi-EVM system and, for the first time, implemented OBC (Other Backward Classes) reservations for seven district panchayat president posts. Results are expected on April 28. The elections are viewed as a critical barometer ahead of Gujarat’s assembly elections scheduled for late 2027, testing the ground-level strength of the Bharatiya Janata Party against the Indian National Congress and the Aam Aadmi Party. For the BJP, which has dominated Gujarat politics for decades, the local body elections offer an early read on whether national headwinds — including inflation and unemployment concerns — are penetrating its home turf.

Zee News · Sunday Guardian Live · DeshGujarat · Gujarat SEC · April 26, 2026

06 — AI in Politics

Deepfakes at Industrial Scale: The First Synthetic Midterm

Ongoing: AI Campaigns

The 2026 midterms are the first U.S. election cycle in which political deepfakes are being deployed at industrial scale. The American Prospect reports that the National Republican Senatorial Committee released an 85-second deepfake video of Texas Senate candidate James Talarico, in which an AI-fabricated version of the Democrat appears to proudly read excerpts from years-old social media posts — and makes self-praising comments there is no evidence Talarico actually made. The ad is only the latest in a series of AI-generated creations from the national GOP campaign apparatus. A Reuters review found that at least 15 campaign ads featuring AI-generated content have run since November. Europol projects that as much as 90 percent of online content could be synthetically generated by 2026 — a threshold researchers say has already been crossed in some campaign contexts. The Foundation for Defense of Democracies documented a division of labor within what it calls the “authoritarian axis”: Iran produces the deepfake content, while Russia and China handle amplification. At home, the WEF’s 2026 Global Risks Report placed mis- and disinformation among the top short-term global risks. AI experts interviewed by the Prospect say it is more important than ever for voters to maintain good digital hygiene, especially when governments and tech companies fall short.

American Prospect · CNN · Reuters · WEF Global Risks Report 2026 · April 17, 2026

07 — Political Campaigning

AI Industry Pours $140 Million into 2026 Races — Without Mentioning AI

Compiled from NBC News, Quartz, CNBC, Political Wire

Leading the Future, a super PAC backed by Andreessen Horowitz, OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman, Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, SV Angel founder Ron Conway, and AI search company Perplexity, announced it raised $15 million in the first quarter of 2026, bringing its total haul for the cycle to $140 million. The group is pouring money into the earliest primaries — Texas and North Carolina — seeking to shape how AI models and companies are regulated nationwide. But there is one thing conspicuously absent from its ads: any reference to artificial intelligence. Instead, the super PAC leans into red-meat or progressive messaging on hot-button issues unrelated to its core policy mission. On the other side, former U.S. representatives have launched rival super PACs to support candidates who favor stronger AI regulation, setting up a bitter face-off. The spending war underscores a paradox: the AI industry considers regulation an existential question but calculates that talking about AI in campaign ads is a losing strategy with voters. NBC News reports that this pattern — AI money flooding elections while the ads avoid the subject entirely — is now the defining financial dynamic of the 2026 midterms. The practical effect is that billions of dollars in AI-related spending will shape congressional races without voters ever knowing what the money is really about. Campaign finance experts warn that the disconnect between donors’ motivations and the content of their ads erodes the transparency that disclosure laws were designed to provide.

NBC News · CNBC · Quartz · Political Wire · February–April 2026

08 — Political Technology

Wisconsin’s $84,000 Chatbot Experiment: 21,000 Voter Conversations

Ongoing: AI Campaigns

In the run-up to Wisconsin’s Supreme Court election, the advocacy group Defend Our Courts partnered with Convos, an AI texting platform, to deploy an AI chatbot that reached 1.1 million voters via text message. Over 10,000 responded, and the system held 21,316 meaningful conversations — all for approximately $84,000. The chatbot was designed to help voters “make an informed decision” about the race, in which liberal Justice-elect Chris Taylor won a decade-long seat, giving liberals a 5–2 majority. Unlike general-purpose chatbots, Convos operates on a closed-system model: campaigns load candidate bios, platforms, and event information into the system, and the AI responds only from that curated data set. Starter texts disclosed that recipients were interacting with an AI agent, and the system honored opt-outs. Convos co-founder Tom Carroll described the virtual assistant as “a modern type of grassroots organizing” at a scale that was previously impossible. Critics counter that even a transparent AI intermediary is a “slippery slope” — a concern amplified by the difficulty of responding to real-time campaign developments within a pre-loaded system.

Wisconsin Public Radio · Wisconsin Law Journal · Urban Milwaukee · Campaigns & Elections · April 13–16, 2026

Ongoing: Midterm Headwinds

Presidents whose approval sits below 45 percent entering their first midterm elections have consistently seen their party lose significant ground in Congress — and the House Republican majority, already at 216–213 with at least 36 incumbents not seeking re-election, provides almost no margin before control changes hands.

10 — Politics & Society

Pope Leo Condemns Death Penalty Hours After U.S. Approves Firing Squads

The Trump administration announced on Friday that it will authorize firing squads as a federally permitted method of execution, deepening its push to revive the death penalty. Hours later, Pope Leo XIV issued a prerecorded video message to DePaul University in Chicago — marking the 15th anniversary of Illinois’s abolition of capital punishment — reiterating the Catholic Church’s teaching that the death penalty is “inadmissible” and that every human life, from conception until natural death, is sacred. The timing underscored a sharp divide between the administration and Catholic leadership. Only three prisoners remain on federal death row after former President Biden commuted the sentences of 37 condemned inmates to life in prison during his final days in office: Dylann Roof, the Charleston church shooter; Dzhokhar Tsarnaev, the Boston Marathon bomber; and Robert Bowers, the Pittsburgh synagogue gunman. The juxtaposition of the policy announcement and the papal statement drew immediate commentary from lawmakers on both sides, with progressives citing the pope’s words as a moral rebuke and conservatives defending the administration’s move as long-overdue justice for victims of mass violence.

NPR · Newsweek · KPBS · April 25, 2026

11 — Social Media Trends

WEF 2026: Disinformation Ranks as World’s Second-Biggest Short-Term Risk

The World Economic Forum’s Global Risks Report 2026, released earlier this year, placed misinformation and disinformation as the second most severe short-term global risk — behind only geoeconomic confrontation. The ranking is notable for its persistence: disinformation appears in the top tier across all time horizons the WEF measures, making it one of the most enduring threats identified. The report highlights how AI systems and opportunistic influencers actively manipulate content to provoke strong emotional reactions, using behavioral and psychological profiling to tailor messages that target specific groups. Those susceptible to emotional manipulation can be identified through microtargeting that uses self-reported online data to reveal personality type. The Blackbird.AI analysis of the WEF report notes that narrative attacks — coordinated campaigns to shape public perception through false or misleading stories — have become an increasingly critical global threat. Researchers tracking synthetic content observed approximately 500,000 deepfakes circulating online in 2023; by 2025 that number reached roughly 8 million, a 1,500 percent increase in two years. For campaign professionals, the WEF’s assessment reinforces what practitioners already sense: the information environment in which elections are contested has fundamentally changed, and the tools to detect manipulation are not keeping pace with the tools to create it.

13 — Split Graph

Who Should Regulate AI — the States or Washington?

The Progressive Case: States Must Fill the Federal Void

Progressive advocates argue that the federal government has proven unable — or unwilling — to regulate AI with the speed the technology demands. Writing in Progressive.org, Meghna Parikh makes the case that Democrats should be actively resisting Big Tech rather than courting its donations. Over 600 state AI bills have been introduced in 2026 alone, with Washington and Utah leading the way on chatbot safety, transparency, and digital replica protections. Supporters of state-level action point to the private right of action in Washington’s new chatbot safety law as a model: it empowers citizens rather than relying on under-resourced federal agencies. The ACLU and consumer groups argue that federal preemption would strip communities of their most effective tool for holding AI companies accountable. State legislatures, they contend, are closer to the voters and faster to act.

Progressive.org · ACLU · Transparency Coalition · April 2026

The Conservative Case: Federal Standards to Prevent a Patchwork

The White House’s National Policy Framework recommends federal preemption of state AI laws that “impose undue burdens,” arguing that a patchwork of 50 different regulatory regimes would cripple American competitiveness in the global AI race. Pro-innovation conservatives warn that aggressive state regulation risks ceding AI leadership to China. The Hill reported that a coalition including Andreessen Horowitz and industry allies lobbied to include a state AI regulation ban in the reconciliation bill — a provision that was eventually removed after backlash from both progressive Democrats and hard-line conservatives. A new conservative group, reported by Axios in March, is pushing for “common-sense” AI guardrails focused on child safety, but insists these should be set at the federal level to avoid compliance chaos for businesses operating across state lines.

The Hill · Axios · Roll Call · White House · March–April 2026

14 — Political Tech Application

$ caucus_ai --track --models=gpt,grok,gemini

What it does: Caucus AI is a monitoring platform that tracks what the major large language models — ChatGPT, Grok, and Gemini — tell voters when they ask about candidates and elections. The tool stores every chatbot response, logs which sources each model cites, and surfaces patterns in how LLMs frame political information across all 50 states.

Who is using it: Built by the two chief analytics officers from Kamala Harris’s 2024 presidential campaign, with support from the Analyst Institute. The platform is designed for campaigns, journalists, and researchers who need to understand the information environment voters encounter when they ask chatbots about politics.

Reported outcomes: In a documented March 2026 experiment, the team created Wikipedia pages for two real candidates — Republican Gregg Hull (New Mexico governor) and Democrat Hallie Shoffner (Arkansas Senate). In the case of OpenAI, information posted online became something a chatbot told voters in just twelve minutes. The research revealed a clear sourcing preference across models for neutral platforms like Wikipedia, Ballotpedia, and the AP. ChatGPT frequently cites its media partner Axios but almost never cites NPR. This is the first election cycle in which the answers LLMs give voters will be of significant importance — and Caucus AI is the first systematic attempt to monitor them.

> takeaway: If a Wikipedia edit becomes a chatbot talking point in 12 minutes, the information supply chain for elections has fundamentally changed. Campaigns that understand what LLMs are saying about their candidates — and which sources feed those answers — hold a structural advantage in 2026.
