Attitude Manipulation and Voting Intentions

Drews, L. & Krause, W. · Political Behavior · Springer · 2025

A representative survey experiment in Germany (N = 3,004) reveals that persuasion on policy attitudes does not reliably translate into changes in voting intention — even among weakly partisan voters. Published in Springer’s Political Behavior, the study manipulated respondents’ issue attitudes by informing them of a party’s position and then measured downstream effects on vote choice. The key finding: citizens adjusted their attitudes to resemble their preferred party’s position, but even respondents with weak partisan attachments did not adjust their voting intentions. The result contradicts the common assumption that swing voters are more susceptible to persuasive messaging campaigns. In multiparty systems, at least, attitudinal shifts appear to be absorbed by partisan identity rather than channeled into vote switching.

Practitioner implication: Campaign persuasion budgets may be misallocated if they assume attitude change equals vote change. The study suggests investing in mobilisation of sympathetic non-voters may yield higher returns than persuasion-focused messaging aimed at converting opponents.

Source: Springer · Political Behavior · 2025

Project Freedom: US Navy Guides Ships Through Hormuz as Iran Opens Fire

The US military operation dubbed ‘Project Freedom’ launched Monday morning in the Strait of Hormuz, deploying guided-missile destroyers, more than 100 aircraft, and 15,000 service members to guide trapped merchant vessels through the Iranian-blockaded waterway. Two American-flagged ships successfully transited the strait within hours — but the operation immediately drew fire. US and Iranian forces exchanged shots, with Tehran’s military chief warning that ‘any foreign armed force will be attacked if they intend to enter the Strait of Hormuz.’ The Pentagon insists Project Freedom is not a formal military escort but an information-sharing operation: rather than accompany commercial vessels, the Navy provides navigational data to help ships avoid Iranian naval mines. The distinction matters legally — and politically. For midterm strategists, the escalation resets the campaign conversation: every day the Hormuz crisis dominates cable news is a day Republicans cannot pivot to the economy, and every Iranian provocation reminds voters that the war Trump promised would be ‘quick and decisive’ is now in its tenth week.

Sources: Axios · CNN · Time · The Times of Israel

Trump’s Retribution Machine Faces Its First Ballot-Box Test in Indiana

Today is the day. Seven of the eight Republican state senators who defied Trump’s push to redraw Indiana’s congressional map face Trump-endorsed primary challengers, backed by more than $8 million in television advertising from Trump-aligned groups. The stakes extend well beyond Indianapolis: if Trump’s retribution campaign succeeds, it will signal to every Republican state legislator in America that crossing the president on redistricting — or anything else — carries a career-ending price. If the incumbents survive, it will be the first concrete evidence that Trump’s endorsement power has limits in low-turnout state primaries, the very terrain where his influence was assumed to be strongest. NPR reports that Trump allies have also poured millions into digital and mail operations, making Indiana’s state senate races among the most expensive in the country. The results will arrive tonight alongside Ohio’s closely watched primaries, where former senator Sherrod Brown’s comeback bid has turned a once-safe Republican Senate seat into a toss-up.

Modi Conquers West Bengal — India’s Left Loses Its Last Bastion

Results declared yesterday confirm a political earthquake across five Indian states. The BJP swept West Bengal with more than 200 of 294 assembly seats, ending Mamata Banerjee’s fifteen-year reign and delivering the party its most consequential state-level victory since 2014. Banerjee herself lost her own Bhabanipur seat to BJP’s Suvendu Adhikari. In Kerala, the Congress-led UDF toppled the Communist-led LDF, ending fifty years of unbroken left-front governance — meaning for the first time since the 1970s, no Indian state is governed by a left or communist party. In Tamil Nadu, the upstart Tamilaga Vettri Kazhagam stormed to 109 seats, displacing the DMK. The BJP also scored a hat-trick in Assam and took Puducherry. For global campaign professionals, the results illustrate two forces at work simultaneously: the power of a well-funded, personality-driven national machine (BJP spent an estimated $300 million across five states), and the limits of regional incumbency when anti-incumbency sentiment reaches critical mass.

Sources: Al Jazeera · NPR · Outlook India · Republic World

AI Data Centers Become the ‘Third Rail’ of Pennsylvania Politics

A 21.7 percent jump in Pennsylvania electricity rates in 2025 has turned AI data centres into a potent campaign issue in three toss-up House districts. CNBC reports that Democratic challengers in the 7th, 8th, and 10th Districts are hammering Republican incumbents for failing to protect residents from the grid strain and rate hikes caused by hyperscale data-centre construction. Governor Josh Shapiro, once an enthusiastic booster, has reversed course and now proposes requiring companies to fund their own energy infrastructure. State legislators are debating a three-year moratorium on facilities exceeding 5,000 servers. Nationally, opponents delayed or blocked 48 data-centre projects in 2025, affecting $156 billion in potential investment. The backlash scrambles traditional political lines: environmentalists, farmers, and conservative ratepayers find themselves on the same side, while tech lobbyists and labour unions that benefit from construction jobs push back. For campaign operatives, Pennsylvania is the canary in the coal mine — if data centres cost the GOP three House seats, the AI industry’s political calculus changes overnight.

Sources: CNBC · Lawfare · City & State Pennsylvania · Axios

GovTech · Ongoing: Voter Roll Crisis

Trump and GOP Push Voter Roll Purges Up to Election Day

The Justice Department has launched a sprawling campaign to obtain voter registration files from nearly every state and cross-reference them against the federal SAVE immigration database to identify suspected non-citizens. As of this week, the DOJ has sued 30 states and the District of Columbia to compel compliance. Twelve states — including Ohio, Texas, and Indiana — have voluntarily handed over their data. The ACLU has filed suit to block what it calls a ‘national voter surveillance-and-purge database,’ warning that SAVE is riddled with false positives that disproportionately flag naturalised citizens, military families, and voters with common surnames. The legal battle centres on the National Voter Registration Act’s 90-day ‘quiet period,’ which prohibits systematic voter removal programmes within three months of an election. For decades, that provision was understood to bar mass purges close to Election Day. But the Trump administration argues the ban does not apply to removals targeting non-citizens, who — it contends — should never have been registered in the first place. A federal appeals court rejected a similar argument from Virginia in 2024, but the Supreme Court intervened with an emergency order allowing that state to restart its purge programme just days before the election. That precedent now looms over the 2026 midterms: if courts side with the administration, states could conduct purges as late as October. Higher Ground Labs warns that the data already shared with DHS has been transferred for immigration screening, raising privacy concerns that extend well beyond election administration. For campaign data teams, the operational question is urgent: voter-contact universes built on current registration files may be obsolete by autumn.

Sources: CNN · ACLU · Higher Ground Labs · NPR Illinois

AI Industry Floods Midterms with $140 Million — Without Ever Mentioning AI

Two warring factions of the AI industry are pouring unprecedented sums into the 2026 midterms — and there is one thing conspicuously absent from their advertising: any mention of artificial intelligence. NBC News reports that ‘Leading the Future,’ the pro-deregulation super PAC backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman, Marc Andreessen, and Ben Horowitz, has raised $140 million for the cycle. Its ads focus on immigration enforcement, healthcare, and Trump’s leadership — never AI policy. On the opposite side, ‘Public First Action,’ which received $20 million from Anthropic, backs candidates who favour AI guardrails, but its ads likewise avoid the subject, focusing instead on affordability and veterans’ services. The strategy is borrowed from the crypto industry’s successful 2024 playbook: spend heavily on culturally resonant issues to elect sympathetic legislators, then lobby them on your core regulatory agenda after they take office. For campaign professionals, the AI PAC war is reshaping the financial landscape of competitive races. In at least four primaries so far, AI-backed spending has exceeded the candidates’ own fundraising — raising questions about whether Silicon Valley is buying legislative outcomes on a regulatory question most voters have never heard of.

Sources: NBC News · CNBC · Popular Information · ABC News

The Council on Foreign Relations notes that the redistricting battle has become ‘the single most important structural factor’ in determining whether Democrats can win back the House. For strategists, the lesson is clear: in 2026, the map is the message.

Trump Imposes Most Sweeping Cuba Sanctions in Decades

A May 1 executive order has established an entirely new sanctions architecture targeting Cuba, going further than any U.S. administration since the original embargo. The order authorises blocking sanctions against any foreign person operating in Cuba’s energy, defence, mining, or financial sectors — meaning non-American companies that maintained carefully segregated Cuba operations are no longer protected. Bloomberg reports that the Treasury Department can now designate entire economic sectors at will, and the order threatens foreign financial institutions’ access to U.S. markets if they continue working with Cuban government entities. Havana has denounced the measures as ‘collective punishment.’ A former Treasury OFAC investigator told CBS News that the order represents ‘the most significant expansion of Cuba sanctions for non-American companies since the embargo began.’ For Latin America-focused campaign strategists, the move is transparently aimed at Florida’s Cuban-American electorate ahead of November — but it also complicates U.S. relations across the hemisphere at a moment when Colombia’s leftist presidential frontrunner has vowed to deepen ties with Havana.

Sources: Bloomberg · White House · Al Jazeera · CBS News

Should AI Industry Money Decide Who Regulates AI?

“Letting the Regulated Buy Their Regulators Is a Democratic Failure”

Critics argue that $140 million from AI companies flooding midterm races represents regulatory capture in real time. Public Citizen and the Brennan Center warn that AI PACs are deliberately avoiding AI in their ads to obscure their true agenda — electing legislators who will block safety regulations. Anthropic’s $20 million donation to Public First Action, while smaller, comes from a company that has publicly called for AI guardrails, suggesting at least one faction of the industry recognises the need for oversight. Campaign-finance scholars note that when an industry outspends candidates in their own races, the resulting legislators owe their seats to corporate benefactors, not constituents.

Sources: Public Citizen · Brennan Center · NBC News

“Innovation Requires a Seat at the Table”

Supporters counter that AI companies have every right — and a responsibility — to participate in democratic elections. Leading the Future argues that premature regulation risks ceding American AI leadership to China, and that electing technologically literate legislators benefits everyone. The Chamber of Commerce and tech trade associations point out that pharmaceutical, energy, and financial companies have long supported candidates aligned with their policy views. Marc Andreessen has called AI regulation ‘the most important policy fight of the decade’ and argues that aggressive state-level laws threaten to fragment the market and drive innovation offshore.

Political Tech Application

SAVE — Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements

The Department of Justice is deploying the federal SAVE database — originally designed to verify immigration status for benefits eligibility — as the backbone of a nationwide voter roll review. SAVE cross-references voter registration files against DHS immigration records to flag suspected non-citizens. The DOJ has demanded voter data from 30 states and D.C., and at least 12 states have complied voluntarily. The tool has been used to generate match lists that states are then pressured to act on, potentially removing flagged voters from the rolls.

But SAVE was never built for this purpose. False-positive rates are significant: naturalised citizens, military families, and individuals with common surnames are routinely misidentified. The ACLU’s lawsuit argues that using SAVE for voter purges violates the NVRA and the Fourteenth Amendment. Higher Ground Labs reports that voter data already shared with the DOJ has been transferred to DHS for immigration screening — a use that was never disclosed to the states that handed it over.

> takeaway: SAVE turns a benefits-verification tool into a voter-suppression vector. Campaign data teams should monitor state compliance lists and prepare voter-protection protocols — any contact universe built on current rolls may be unreliable by October.

Sources: CNN · ACLU · Higher Ground Labs · NPR Illinois
