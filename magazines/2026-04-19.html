Research Insights of the Day
Overestimating the Social Costs of Political Belief Change
Northwestern Institute for Policy Research · Journal of Personality and Social Psychology · 2026

Partisans consistently overestimate the social punishment they will face for changing their minds on polarizing issues. Across five main studies and five supplemental experiments (N = 4,535), researchers found an average weighted effect size of d = .87 — a large gap between the backlash people expect and the backlash they actually receive. In one behavioral study, participants predicted their partner would abandon them 18.7 percent of the time after a belief shift; the actual abandonment rate was just 7.9 percent.

The inflated fear appears to stem from a belief that dissent signals disloyalty to one's political group — a signal that turns out to be far less damaging than anticipated. Critically, the researchers tested an intervention: participants who reflected on their history of group loyalty before disclosing dissent anticipated less rejection and were less likely to self-censor. The effect held across genuine belief shifts, not just hypothetical ones.

Practitioner implication: Campaigns that normalize belief flexibility — by affirming voters' existing loyalties before asking them to consider new positions — may unlock a larger persuadable electorate than conventional targeting models assume.
Source: PsyPost / Northwestern IPR Working Paper WP-24-25 / Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 2026

AI in Politics
Anthropic's Mythos Brings Amodei to the West Wing

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei met White House chief of staff Susie Wiles and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent on Friday in what the administration called “productive and constructive” discussions — the most significant thaw since the Pentagon declared the AI company a supply-chain risk in March.

The meeting centered on Mythos, Anthropic's newest model with advanced cybersecurity capabilities that multiple government agencies want access to. The Pentagon blacklisted Anthropic after the company refused to grant unrestricted military access to its models for “all lawful purposes,” including autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. A federal judge issued a preliminary injunction blocking the ban in late March, but an appeals court reversed the stay in early April, leaving the dispute unresolved. Ongoing: the Anthropic-Pentagon standoff has been a recurring thread; Friday's White House meeting represents a new diplomatic channel outside the courtroom.

The Shadow Docket Feud: Three Justices Break the Silence

In a rare breach of the Supreme Court's internal decorum, Justices Ketanji Brown Jackson, Sonia Sotomayor, and Clarence Thomas all issued public critiques of the court's direction within the same week. The central flashpoint: the emergency docket.

Jackson accused her conservative colleagues of allowing the Trump administration to “push forward controversial policies without proper legal scrutiny” through the emergency docket — the stream of unsigned, often unexplained orders the court issues outside its normal briefing and argument schedule. Sotomayor, in a speech at the University of Kansas, took aim at a Kavanaugh concurrence in an immigration case, then issued a rare public apology on April 15, calling her remarks “hurtful” and “inappropriate.” Meanwhile, a Fourth Circuit judge warned that treating emergency orders as binding precedent would “weaken the public's confidence in the integrity of our judicial system's commitment to deliberation.”

Source: CNN, Axios, Legal News Feed · April 15–18, 2026

05 · Global PoliticsApril 19, 2026
Global Politics · Polling
Netanyahu Torched U.S. Support for a Generation

Sixty percent of Americans now view Israel unfavorably, according to a Pew Research Center survey fielded about a month into the U.S.- and Israeli-led war in Iran. The collapse is sharpest among younger voters: Israel's favorability has dropped 22 percentage points among both younger Republicans and younger Democrats since 2022.

The political downstream is already visible. Among Republicans under 45, 61 percent now say they would prefer a candidate who would reduce support for Israel — a generational rupture within the party's most reliable pro-Israel bloc. Lawmakers who entered Congress as staunch Israel supporters are becoming vocal critics, and 77 percent of Americans call the Iran military action “personally important,” compared with 53 percent who say the same about the Israel-Hamas conflict. Ongoing: the Iran conflict's domestic political fallout has appeared in multiple recent issues; this is the first major post-invasion Pew benchmark.

Source: Pew Research Center, April 7, 2026 / Axios, April 18, 2026

Brazil: Flavio Bolsonaro Closes a 12-Point Gap

Senator Flavio Bolsonaro, running in place of his barred father, has pulled even with President Lula in the most dramatic consolidation of the 2026 global election cycle. A Datafolha poll released April 11 shows the pair deadlocked in a hypothetical runoff — Bolsonaro 46 percent, Lula 45 percent — after trailing by twelve points as recently as December.

Lula, seeking an unprecedented fourth term at 80, faces a 51 percent disapproval rating. In the first-round field, he still leads with 45.9 percent to Flavio's 40.1, but the trajectory favors the opposition. The first round is October 4; a runoff, if needed, falls on October 25. Ongoing: Brazil's tightening race was noted in previous issues; this is the first poll to show a statistical tie in the runoff scenario.

07 · GovTechApril 19, 2026
GovTech
Pennsylvania Deploys GenAI Across 35 State Agencies

Following a one-year pilot, the Shapiro administration has expanded generative AI tools to more than 3,000 state employees across 35 agencies — one of the largest statewide government AI rollouts in the country. The numbers tell a clear story of bureaucratic acceleration: during the pilot, 175 employees across 14 agencies reported saving an average of 95 minutes per day on tasks like drafting communications, summarizing materials, conducting research, and brainstorming.

Specific deployments are already reshaping how Pennsylvania interacts with its residents. The Department of Human Services uses AI to scan uploaded documents, flagging image quality or relevance issues before a caseworker ever sees them — nearly 400,000 documents have been processed since October. The Board of Pardons is using AI to transcribe handwritten clemency applications into digital systems, removing a bottleneck that once delayed cases by weeks.

Governor Shapiro's FY 2027 budget proposal includes AI consumer protection requirements and a research partnership with the University of Pennsylvania to advise on responsible use across state government. The state is also hiring its first chief data and AI officer. Pennsylvania now ranks in the top three for government AI readiness nationwide, according to a recent assessment, and its approach — piloting with real metrics before scaling — is being studied by other states wrestling with the gap between AI hype and operational deployment.

Source: GovTech, StateScoop, PublicSource, Commonwealth of Pennsylvania · April 2026

AI-Powered TV Ad Production Hits Scale for the Midterms

The 2026 midterms are the first election cycle in which AI-powered television ad production is available at scale. AI production platforms have collapsed the cost and timeline of creating TV spots, allowing campaigns to iterate messaging mid-cycle and respond to breaking news in hours rather than weeks. Political ad spending is projected to hit a new record, according to OpenSecrets, and the technology is shifting who can afford television.

The implications run deeper than speed. Down-ballot candidates who could never afford broadcast production can now compete on the same visual plane as Senate races. Combined with connected TV targeting — where political data vendors now achieve 50–70 percent voter file match rates — the result is a fundamentally different advertising ecosystem than 2024. Meta's AI election integrity machine adds another layer: advertisers creating or editing political content with AI must now disclose it, with labels auto-applied in Meta's Ad Library.

10 · National SecurityApril 19, 2026
National Security · Legislation
Section 702: Twenty Republicans Derail Surveillance Renewal

A bloc of twenty House Republicans broke ranks to torpedo a longer-term reauthorization of Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, forcing a two-week stopgap extension that the Senate passed by voice vote on Friday. The provision, which allows warrantless surveillance of foreign targets' communications — including when they correspond with Americans — has become a flashpoint for an unusual left-right coalition of civil libertarians and populist-right lawmakers skeptical of intelligence agencies.

The rebellion signals growing Republican resistance to the national security establishment, even as the Trump administration relies heavily on Section 702 intelligence for the Iran conflict. Privacy advocates argue the program's “incidental collection” of Americans' communications amounts to backdoor surveillance; intelligence officials counter that the tool is essential to counterterrorism and cybersecurity. The next vote is expected before April 30.

Connected TV Reaches the Campaign Mainstream

Political data vendors have pushed voter file matching on connected TV platforms to 50–70 percent accuracy — meaning campaigns can now deliver targeted ads directly to streaming devices for up to 70,000 identified voters from a list of 100,000. The breakthrough is reshaping media buying as more American households cut the cord and campaigns follow voters onto streaming platforms.

The shift makes CTV a genuine persuasion channel, not just a reach play. Campaigns can micro-target by voter file attributes, test creative in real time, and measure downstream effects on turnout and donation behavior. Combined with AI-powered ad production, the 2026 midterms represent the first cycle where a state legislative candidate could, in theory, produce and distribute a TV-quality spot to identified voters in a single day.

Trump Signs Psychedelics Executive Order for Veterans

President Trump signed an executive order on Friday directing federal agencies to expedite research into — and access to — psychedelic compounds used outside the United States to treat post-traumatic stress disorder. The order focuses on psilocybin and MDMA-assisted therapies that have shown promise in clinical trials but remain Schedule I substances under federal law.

The move carries unusual bipartisan valence. Veterans' mental health commands broad sympathy across the political spectrum, and psychedelic-assisted therapy has attracted advocates from libertarian-right veterans' groups to progressive mental health organizations. The executive order does not reschedule any substance but directs the FDA and VA to fast-track clinical pathways — a pragmatic framing that sidesteps the culture-war baggage of broader drug policy reform. For campaign strategists, the order is a case study in finding issue space that splits the other side's coalition.

Source: Axios, NPR · April 18, 2026

The Federal AI Preemption Fight
Democrats: The GUARDRAILS Act

Congressional Democrats introduced the GUARDRAILS Act to repeal the Trump administration's executive order and block efforts to impose a moratorium on state-level AI regulation. The bill would strengthen oversight mechanisms, address workforce disruption, and establish safeguards against harmful or deceptive AI deployment. Supporters argue that states should remain free to protect their residents and that a federal floor, not a federal ceiling, is the appropriate framework for AI governance.

Source: TechPolicy.Press, National Law Review, Lawfare · 2026
White House: Industry-Friendly Framework

The White House's March 2026 AI policy framework asks Congress to preempt “cumbersome” state AI laws, avoid creating a new federal AI regulator, and rely on sector-specific rules and industry-led standards. House Speaker Mike Johnson and Senator Ted Cruz have signaled support. Advocates contend that a patchwork of state regulations will stifle innovation, burden startups, and drive AI development offshore — and that existing agencies already have the tools to address sector-specific harms.

14 · Political Tech ApplicationApril 19, 2026
Political Tech Application
Quiller AI

What it does: Quiller is an AI copilot built specifically for Democratic campaigns and progressive nonprofits. It generates first drafts of fundraising emails, supporter communications, op-eds, and volunteer scripts — with guardrails that keep a human in the loop before any content reaches a voter.

Who is using it: Over 100 Democratic organizations have adopted the platform as of early 2026, spanning congressional campaigns, state parties, and advocacy groups. The tool was featured in Campaigns & Elections as part of the new wave of AI-powered fundraising infrastructure.

Reported outcomes: In a documented case study, a mayoral campaign using Quiller achieved approximately 40% email open rates and raised $22.22 per minute spent on the program — up from $8.33 per minute before adoption. The campaign cited the ability to test multiple message variants rapidly as the key efficiency gain.

takeaway: Quiller represents the professionalization of AI-assisted fundraising on the Democratic side — a tool designed not to replace strategists but to remove the first-draft bottleneck, in a cycle where the GOP's broader AI adoption advantage (64% daily use vs. 49% for Democrats) makes efficiency gains existential for smaller campaigns.
Source: Quiller.ai / Campaigns & Elections / Medium Case Study / AAPC Survey · 2026
