SPREAD 01 / RESEARCH INSIGHTS
Political Psychology

Truth-Seeking vs. Balance: The Credibility Dilemma in Correcting Political Misinformation

Hwayong Shin · Washington University in St. Louis

Journalists who correct misinformation from one political party more often than the other face a structural credibility trap, according to a preregistered experimental study published this year in Political Communication. Shin finds that asymmetric corrections—even when factually warranted—undermine perceived credibility among audience segments that believe misinformation is equally distributed across parties. Two experiments demonstrate that individuals who attribute disinformation to both parties discount a source that mostly corrects one side, regardless of whether the corrections favor or oppose their own partisan identity. The result is a perception gap between journalists, who see asymmetry in misinformation output, and audiences, who frequently do not.

Practitioner Implication

Campaign communicators exploiting the "both sides" perception should expect that fact-checkers will increasingly struggle to maintain credibility when correcting one party disproportionately—making source diversification and bipartisan framing essential for opposition research that relies on third-party validation.

Source: Political Communication, Vol. 43, February 2026. DOI: 10.1080/10584609.2026.2627253

Virginia Votes Today on a $100 Million Redistricting Battle That Could Reshape the House Map

Polls open this morning on a statewide referendum that asks whether Virginia's legislature should temporarily reclaim the power to draw congressional districts from its bipartisan redistricting commission. If the “yes” vote prevails, Democrats—who control the state legislature and governor's mansion—could redraw a map favoring their party in 10 of 11 congressional seats, up from the 6 they won in 2024. The stakes have attracted staggering sums: Virginians for Fair Elections, the pro-referendum campaign, has raised over $64 million, mostly from 501(c)(4) groups that do not disclose donors. The anti-referendum Justice for Democracy PAC has spent nearly $10 million, itself funded by a single dark-money entity. Combined spending is approaching $100 million—for a single off-cycle referendum. The vote is part of a nationwide redistricting arms race triggered when Trump encouraged Republican-led states to redraw their maps last year. NPR reports that contradictory mailers, conflicting TV ads, and vague ballot language have left Virginia voters “confused and misled,” raising questions about whether the democratic process can survive when the rules of representation themselves become a campaign product.

Sources: NPR, April 20, 2026; NBC News; VPM; Virginia Mercury; WTOP; Ballotpedia

Hungary's Magyar Unveils Cabinet to Lead an EU Reset After 16 Years of Orban

Prime Minister-designate Peter Magyar presented his cabinet team on Sunday, signaling a definitive break with the Orban era and an accelerated push to unlock roughly 18 billion euros in frozen EU funds. The new government—expected to take power in the first week of May—will prioritize anti-corruption reforms, restoring judicial independence, and reviving media freedom to meet European Commission conditions. Magyar has pledged “constructive cooperation” with Brussels while pursuing a pragmatic foreign policy, though his stance on Ukraine remains cautious: he opposes fast-tracking Kyiv's EU accession, arguing it is “completely out of the question” to admit a country at war. On energy, he plans to check existing contracts for corruption and gradually diversify away from Russian supplies by the mid-2030s. His two-thirds supermajority—138 of 199 seats—gives him the legislative power to amend the constitution, an option Orban used extensively to entrench his own rule.

Sources: Bloomberg, April 20, 2026; Euronews, April 15; Carnegie Endowment; Al Jazeera

Mejia's Progressive Win in NJ-11 Cuts the GOP House Majority to a Single Vote

Democrat Analilia Mejia won the special election to fill Governor Mikie Sherrill's vacated House seat in New Jersey's 11th District, defeating Republican council member Joe Hathaway and narrowing the GOP's margin to 217–214 with one independent caucusing Republican. Speaker Mike Johnson can now afford exactly one defection on party-line votes. Mejia, a union organizer and daughter of Colombian and Dominican immigrants, ran on an affordability platform—child care funding, higher taxes on top earners—and drew endorsements from Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, Elizabeth Warren, and Bernie Sanders, for whom she served as 2020 national political director. NBC's Steve Kornacki noted that traditionally Republican suburban precincts swung decisively toward Mejia, a sign that the anti-Trump suburban realignment observed in 2025 gubernatorial races continues to deepen.

Sources: CNN, April 16, 2026; NBC News; Gothamist; 19th News

$200 Million and Counting: AI Super PACs Are Reshaping the Midterm Battlefield

The AI industry's political spending machine has reached a scale that rivals Big Tech's 2022 crypto push. Leading the Future, backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman, Andreessen Horowitz, and Palantir's founders, announced a $140 million war chest after raising $15 million in Q1 2026 alone. Its mission: elect candidates from either party who support a single federal AI framework instead of the state-by-state patchwork now taking shape. On the other side, Public First has pledged $50 million to back candidates who favor stronger regulation. The combined $200 million dwarfs traditional tech lobbying and is already flowing into Texas congressional primaries and swing-state Senate races. The emerging dynamic is not left vs. right but innovators vs. regulators—a fault line that cuts across both parties and is rewriting the playbook for how technology industries buy influence in Washington.

Sources: CNBC, April 15, 2026; NOTUS; Blaze Media; NBC News; Common Dreams

Lebanon Goes Mobile-First for Its May Election—and Confronts a Deepfake Reckoning

When Lebanese voters head to the polls between May 1 and May 10 for the first parliamentary election since 2022, they will encounter a campaign landscape that has been almost entirely relocated to the smartphone screen. Street posters and banners—once the dominant visual grammar of Lebanese elections—have given way to social media reels, WhatsApp stories, and closed messaging groups where candidates court supporters in real time.

The shift is partly structural: Lebanon's economic crisis has made traditional print and billboard advertising prohibitively expensive for all but the wealthiest blocs. But it is also strategic. A new online platform launched by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs has streamlined diaspora voter registration, aiming to boost participation among the estimated 600,000 Lebanese abroad. Campaigns are using targeted digital ads in diaspora hubs from Paris to São Paulo, where competitive turnout could tip marginal seats.

The mobile-first pivot has not come without risk. The Maharat Foundation, a Beirut-based media watchdog, has warned of growing challenges from AI-generated disinformation and deepfakes, calling for urgent legal frameworks to protect information integrity during the electoral period. Speakers at a recent Maharat forum noted that Lebanon lacks any binding regulation on synthetic media in elections—a gap that places it alongside the United States in having no comprehensive federal-level deepfake law, despite facing many of the same threats.

For campaign technologists watching from outside, Lebanon's May election is a live case study in what happens when a country leapfrogs traditional campaign infrastructure and goes directly to mobile—and whether civil society can keep pace with the disinformation risks that come with it.

Sources: Nascode, 2026; Wikipedia, 2026 Lebanese general election; Maharat Foundation; NowLebanon

EU Deepfake Rules Take Force in 103 Days—and the Code of Practice Still Isn't Final

On August 2, 2026, the EU AI Act's Article 50 transparency obligations become legally enforceable across all member states. Deployers of AI systems that generate synthetic audio, video, or images—including political ads—will be required to label content as AI-generated and disclose synthetic interactions to users. Non-compliance carries fines of up to 35 million euros or 7% of global annual turnover. But the voluntary Code of Practice on marking and labeling AI-generated content, published in first draft last December, is still being finalized and is not expected until June. That leaves a narrow window for platforms and campaign vendors to implement technical watermarking and disclosure standards before the law bites. For American observers, the contrast with the U.S. regulatory landscape is stark: 31 states have passed deepfake election laws, but there is no federal statute and the Trump administration has signaled opposition to any AI-specific regulation.

Sources: European Commission; TechPolicy.Press; Brennan Center for Justice; identifAI; Columbia CJEL

The Algorithm Effect: One Week of Feed Reranking Moves Voters as Much as Three Years of Real Life

A landmark study published in Science recruited 1,256 U.S. participants and used large language models to rerank their X (formerly Twitter) feeds in real time during the 2024 presidential campaign. Users who received reduced exposure to content expressing partisan animosity felt 2.11 degrees warmer toward the opposing party on a 100-point scale after just one week. Those with increased exposure cooled by 2.48 degrees. The shift is equivalent to what longitudinal survey data shows occurring over roughly three years of organic attitude change. Critically, 74% of participants reported noticing no impact on their experience—algorithmic effects operated entirely below conscious awareness. The finding has immediate implications for platform governance ahead of the 2026 midterms: if a week of feed tuning can move the needle this much, the cumulative effect of 18 months of campaign-season algorithms could be transformative.

Sources: Science, November 2025 (DOI: 10.1126/science.adu5584); Northeastern University; TechPolicy.Press; The Daily UW

31 States Have Deepfake Election Laws. There Are Still Zero Federal Rules.

The 2026 midterms are the first U.S. electoral cycle in which political deepfakes are being deployed at industrial scale, and the regulatory response remains a patchwork of largely untested state statutes. At least 15 campaign ads featuring AI-generated content have aired since November, including an NRSC video that used deepfake technology to show a Texas Democratic Senate candidate “reading” his own social media posts, disclosed only with a small watermark. The Brennan Center for Justice warns that the absence of a federal framework means enforcement depends entirely on state attorneys general, many of whom lack the technical capacity or political will to act. The Trump administration has taken a hard line against any AI-specific regulation, and Congressional prospects for action are dim. For campaigns, the practical result is a green light: if you can label it, you can run it—and even the labeling requirements vary wildly by state.

Sources: NBC News; Brennan Center for Justice; Detroit News, March 28, 2026; American Prospect, April 17, 2026

SPREAD 14 / POLITICAL TECH APPLICATION
> Aaru

What it does: Aaru replicates entire voter districts as populations of AI agents, each one synthesized from census data, voter files, and behavioral profiles. These synthetic electorates can be polled in under 90 seconds at less than one-tenth the cost of traditional surveys. Campaign operatives load polling data and news events into the platform, then query the AI population on candidate favorability, message resonance, or issue salience—generating results that would take a conventional pollster weeks and tens of thousands of dollars.

Who is using it: Republican campaigns and the NRSC have adopted Aaru alongside MiroFish, a complementary platform that forecasts public opinion using simulated people and can test ad creative by predicting real voter responses. According to Axios, GOP operatives are using these tools to identify new small donors, pre-test attack ad effectiveness, and model voter behavior in small-universe races like congressional primaries where traditional polling is prohibitively expensive.

Reported outcome: Aaru's synthetic polls of approximately 5,000 AI respondents have achieved accuracy competitive with conventional surveys in early 2026 primary races, though the platform notably mispredicted the 2024 presidential outcome. The cost reduction—estimated at 90% or more compared to traditional phone/online polls—is enabling campaigns to poll far more frequently and in far smaller races than was previously viable.

> takeaway: AI-simulated electorates are making micro-polling economically viable for down-ballot races, but the technology's predictive reliability remains unproven at scale. The party that masters synthetic polling first gains a structural information advantage—and right now, that party is the GOP.

Sources: Axios, April 14, 2026; Semafor, September 2024; Ordinary Times, April 7, 2026; aaru.com
