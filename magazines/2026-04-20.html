Research Insights of the Day

The Levers of Political Persuasion with Conversational Artificial Intelligence

Bai, Voelkel, Eichstaedt & Willer · Science, 2026

Across 707 British political issues, researchers tested which prompting strategies made large language models most persuasive in one-on-one conversations with human participants. The central finding is a paradox: LLMs became significantly more persuasive after post-training alignment -- and were at their most convincing when prompted to deploy facts and evidence rather than emotional appeals. Yet the very models that scored highest on persuasion also generated the most factually inaccurate claims, raising the prospect that the most effective AI interlocutors are also the most unreliable narrators. The authors found that fact-prompted models shifted participants' opinions by 1.4 points on a 7-point scale on average, a magnitude comparable to sustained human canvassing over multiple contacts. Emotional and identity-based prompts, by contrast, produced smaller shifts and were more frequently identified by participants as manipulative.
Practitioner Implication

Campaigns deploying conversational AI for voter outreach should implement real-time fact-verification layers, because the same prompting strategy that maximizes persuasion also maximizes fabrication -- a liability that could trigger regulatory scrutiny and erode voter trust faster than it builds support.

Source: Science, Vol. 391, April 2026. doi:10.1126/science.aea3884

Bulgaria's Radev Sweeps to Power in Landmark Election

Former president Rumen Radev's Progressive Bulgaria coalition won an estimated 43.4 percent of the vote in Sunday's parliamentary election -- the country's eighth in five years -- securing between 129 and 134 of the 240-seat National Assembly. The result amounts to a political earthquake in the Balkans: Radev, a left-leaning eurosceptic who stepped down from the presidency in January, rode a wave of anti-corruption anger after mass protests toppled the previous conservative government in December. His nearest rival, caretaker Prime Minister Andrey Gyurov's PP-DB, trailed at 13.9 percent, while longtime power-broker Boyko Borissov's GERB-SDS collapsed to 12.8 percent. Radev now faces the delicate task of building a governing coalition, with his EU-skeptic and Russia-sympathetic posture already drawing concern from Brussels and Kyiv alike.

Sources: Al Jazeera, Bloomberg, Euronews, Kyiv Independent · April 19, 2026

Federal Agencies Defy Trump's Anthropic Ban to Test Mythos

The Trump administration blacklisted Anthropic from government contracts after negotiations over AI safety restrictions collapsed. But federal agencies are quietly sidestepping the ban. The Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation is actively testing Mythos -- Anthropic's most capable frontier model, released April 7 -- for its cybersecurity and autonomous-agent capabilities. Staff on at least three congressional committees have held or requested classified briefings on the model's cyber-scanning prowess. Behind the scenes, officials across civilian agencies are in active talks with Anthropic about broader deployment, exposing a widening rift inside the administration: national-security pragmatists who see Mythos as essential infrastructure versus political loyalists enforcing the ban. The standoff raises a pointed question about whether any president can effectively embargo a leading AI lab whose technology may already be indispensable.

Sources: Politico, U.S. News & World Report, RedState · April 14-18, 2026

FISA Section 702 Expires Today as Conservative Rebels Block Renewal

The legal authority underpinning America's most sweeping foreign-surveillance program lapses today, April 20, after a bloc of 20 House Republicans torpedoed both a five-year and an eighteen-month renewal. Congress scrambled to pass a ten-day stopgap extension through April 30, buying time but solving nothing. At the heart of the revolt is a demand by Freedom Caucus members, led by Rep. Chip Roy of Texas, for a warrant requirement before intelligence agencies can search databases of Americans' communications -- even when those messages were collected lawfully under foreign-surveillance rules. The White House, which supports a clean extension with no changes, has pushed back hard, arguing that a warrant mandate would slow intelligence operations at a moment of acute geopolitical risk. For campaign professionals, the fight is a preview of the surveillance-versus-privacy fault line that will shape midterm messaging: libertarian-leaning Republicans are framing this as a civil-liberties stand, while national-security hawks warn of intelligence blackouts.

Sources: NPR, Axios, CNBC, Daily Caller · April 17-20, 2026

Republican vs Democratic Consultants Using AI Daily

A new survey by the American Association of Political Consultants reveals a striking partisan gap in how campaigns adopt artificial intelligence. Nearly two-thirds of Republican consultants report using AI tools every day -- for ad scripting, voter-file enrichment, and phone-bank automation -- while fewer than half of their Democratic counterparts do the same. The asymmetry reflects divergent institutional cultures: the GOP's embrace of Silicon Valley risk-taking versus the Democratic establishment's caution around privacy, labor displacement, and brand risk. Axios reports that Republican operatives are already piloting AI-driven phone agents to persuade voters in real time, a frontier that most Democratic firms have declined to cross. The gap matters because AI-adopting campaigns can iterate messaging at machine speed, compressing feedback loops that once took weeks into hours.

Sources: Axios, American Association of Political Consultants · April 14, 2026

$10.8 Billion: Inside the Most Expensive Midterm Cycle in History

Political ad spending for the 2025-2026 midterm cycle is projected to reach $10.8 billion, according to AdImpact's Political Projections report -- more than 20 percent higher than the previous midterm record of $8.9 billion set in 2021-2022, and approaching the $12 billion mark that was once thought exclusive to presidential years. Senate races alone are forecast at $2.8 billion, beating the 2024 record, while House spending will cross $2 billion for the first time in history. The most consequential shift, however, is not the topline but the channel mix. Connected television -- the streaming platforms that have replaced cable in millions of American living rooms -- is projected to capture $2.48 billion in political ad dollars, roughly 24 percent of total cycle spending and the only media channel expected to see an increase over 2024. For campaigns, CTV promises granular targeting: a Senate candidate in Michigan can serve different creative to a union household in Flint and a suburban family in Grand Rapids watching the same show on the same evening. But the premium comes at a cost: CTV inventory is expensive enough to shut out lesser-funded challengers, reinforcing the structural advantage of incumbents and well-capitalized Super PACs. Traditional broadcast television, once the unchallenged king of political advertising, continues its slow decline, though it remains the largest single channel by dollar volume. Digital display and social-media ads are growing but face headwinds from platform fragmentation and increasing user skepticism. The result is a media landscape that rewards campaigns with sophisticated, multi-channel buying operations -- and punishes those still running playbooks from 2018.

Sources: AdImpact, OpenSecrets, Campaigns & Elections, MediaPost · January-April 2026

Social Media Trends

US Social Media Is Getting Smaller, Angrier, and More Republican

Ongoing: The partisan realignment of American social media, a trend tracked across multiple recent issues, has reached a new threshold. A London School of Economics analysis published in March finds that Twitter/X has undergone a 73-percentage-point swing in partisan posting dominance since 2020 -- from a 56-point Democratic advantage to a modest Republican majority. Democrats have not left the platform en masse; they still visit, but they post far less frequently, ceding the public square to conservative voices. Meanwhile, Republican users are migrating from ideologically homogeneous spaces like Truth Social into mainstream platforms, while progressive users are scattering toward Bluesky, Mastodon, and Threads. The result is not just a shift in tone but a structural fracture: Americans increasingly inhabit separate digital realities, with different facts, different heroes, and different villains -- a dynamic that campaign communicators must now navigate as a baseline condition, not an anomaly.

Source: LSE Impact of Social Sciences Blog · March 24, 2026

AI Deepfakes Are Now Standard Campaign Practice

Ongoing: The weaponization of AI-generated media in political advertising, reported in earlier editions, has intensified. The American Prospect reports that at least fifteen campaign ads featuring AI-generated content have aired since November, with confirmed deepfake deployments in races across Texas, Georgia, Massachusetts, and Maine. The National Republican Senatorial Committee released a deepfake video depicting Maine Democratic Senate candidate Graham Platner in a fabricated compromising scene; the Cornyn campaign in Texas produced an AI-generated music video animating corruption allegations against a rival. There is no federal law constraining this practice. The FEC remains deadlocked along partisan lines, and while 28 states have passed some form of legislation, most mandate disclosure rather than prohibition. California's attempt at an outright ban was struck down by a federal judge on First Amendment grounds. The regulatory vacuum means that for the 2026 cycle, deepfakes will be governed primarily by voter tolerance and media scrutiny -- both of which are eroding under the sheer volume of synthetic content.

Sources: The American Prospect, CNN, Reuters, CampaignNow · March-April 2026

AI Data Centers Are Becoming a Campaign Issue

In several 2026 races, candidates are discovering that AI infrastructure is no longer an abstraction confined to Silicon Valley earnings calls -- it is a visceral local issue with water bills, electricity rates, and zoning fights attached. In Michigan, Democratic gubernatorial candidate Abdul El-Sayed has issued a formal set of "terms of engagement" for future data-center developments, demanding binding protections around water consumption, energy costs, community input, and local hiring. The move reflects a broader political awakening: as hyperscalers race to build the compute capacity that frontier AI models demand, the communities hosting these facilities are pushing back against what they see as extractive bargains -- tax breaks and promises of "hundreds of jobs" that often materialize as a few dozen highly specialized positions. Lawfare's recent analysis frames the dynamic as a new chapter in American industrial policy: data centers are the factories of the AI era, and the political questions they raise -- who benefits, who bears the environmental cost, who gets a veto -- echo the debates that accompanied steel mills and auto plants a century ago. For campaign strategists, the takeaway is that "AI policy" can no longer be confined to Washington think-tank panels. It is showing up on doorsteps, in town halls, and in the competitive positioning of candidates who understand that the next economy will be built, physically, in someone's backyard.

Sources: Lawfare, Campaigns & Elections · April 2026

Should the United States Regulate Political Deepfakes?

"Democracy Cannot Survive a Post-Truth Campaign Landscape"

A coalition of 50 Democratic members of Congress, led by Senator Adam Schiff, has submitted a formal petition to the FEC arguing that AI-generated political content constitutes a unique threat to electoral integrity. The coalition warns that voters increasingly cannot distinguish authentic campaign material from fabricated media, and calls for mandatory disclosure labels, pre-distribution review for AI-generated ads within 60 days of an election, and civil penalties for campaigns that deploy undisclosed synthetic content. Public Citizen's parallel petition emphasizes that self-regulation has failed and that the current patchwork of 28 state laws leaves most voters unprotected.

Sources: American Prospect, NBC News, Public Citizen · April 2026

"Regulating Speech Is a Cure Worse Than the Disease"

First Amendment scholars and conservative legal organizations have mounted a robust defense of AI-generated political speech, arguing that content-based restrictions on campaign advertising -- regardless of the production method -- violate core constitutional protections. A federal judge in California struck down the state's deepfake ban as overbroad, and Republican strategists contend that disclosure mandates would create a chilling effect on legitimate creative expression. The GOP's position is pragmatic as well as principled: with 64 percent of Republican consultants already using AI daily, regulatory constraints would disproportionately handicap the party that has embraced the technology most aggressively.

Sources: Daily Caller, First Amendment Institute, Axios · March-April 2026

Spread 14 / Political Tech Application
Campaign Tool
Civox "Ashley"
What it does: Civox, a London-based political technology startup, has built an AI voice agent named "Ashley" capable of conducting live campaign phone calls -- canvassing, persuasion, voter-ID, and GOTV scripts -- in over 20 languages. The system uses a fine-tuned large language model combined with real-time speech synthesis to hold natural, branching conversations with voters, adapting tone and talking points based on the voter's responses.

Who is using it: Civox has disclosed pilot deployments with multiple European party organizations and at least one U.S. congressional campaign, though specific client names remain under NDA. The company's public roadmap targets tens of thousands of daily calls by Q4 2026, with a focus on multilingual districts and diaspora outreach where human phone-bank volunteers are scarce.

Reported outcomes: Civox claims a 3.2x improvement in contact rate over traditional robocalls and a 40-percent reduction in cost-per-contact compared to human phone banks. Early A/B tests suggest that voters who complete a full conversation with Ashley are 18 percent more likely to report intent to vote, though independent verification of these figures has not yet been published.
takeaway: Multilingual AI voice agents are no longer hypothetical. Civox's "Ashley" represents the first credible attempt to scale personalized, conversational voter contact across language barriers -- a capability that could reshape outreach in diverse electorates from Los Angeles to Brussels.

Sources: Civox company disclosures, Axios, BDN/UMaine · April 2026
