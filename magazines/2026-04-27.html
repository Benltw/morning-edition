01 — Research Insights of the Day

Persuading Voters Using Human–Artificial Intelligence Dialogues

Bai, Voelkel, Eichstaedt, Willer et al. · Nature · December 4, 2025

Can a chatbot change how you vote? Researchers at Cornell and Stanford ran large-scale experiments across the 2024 U.S. presidential election, the 2025 Canadian federal election, and the 2025 Polish presidential election, randomly assigning participants to converse with AI models that advocated for one of the top two candidates. The headline finding is stark: chatbots powered by large language models moved opposition voters’ candidate preferences by 10 percentage points or more — an effect roughly four times larger than traditional video advertisements tested during the 2016 and 2020 cycles. The most persuasion-optimized model shifted opposition voters by a striking 25 percentage points. But the study surfaces a troubling trade-off: the more persuasive a model was, the less accurate the information it provided. When pushed to generate dense factual claims, the chatbots eventually ran out of real evidence and began fabricating. A companion paper in Science examining 77,000 participants and 19 LLMs confirmed that “information density” — the sheer volume of factual-sounding claims — drives persuasion regardless of whether the facts are accurate.

Practitioner Implication

AI-powered voter outreach is now demonstrably more persuasive than traditional advertising — but persuasion and accuracy are inversely correlated. Campaigns deploying chatbot canvassing must decide where on that trade-off they are willing to sit, and regulators must reckon with the reality that the most effective AI persuasion tools are also the least truthful.

Nature, December 2025 · Science, December 2025 · Cornell Chronicle · Scientific American

03 — Global Elections

Fatah Sweeps Palestinian Elections as Gaza Returns to the Ballot

Ongoing: Palestinian Elections

Loyalists of President Mahmoud Abbas have swept Palestinian municipal elections held on April 25, including — for the first time in nearly two decades — a city in the besieged Gaza Strip. Fatah announced victories in the majority of local councils, including Jenin, a northern West Bank city where the Palestinian Authority had previously been accused of losing control to Hamas-allied factions. In Gaza’s Deir al-Balah, the Fatah-backed Nahdat list secured six of 15 contested seats, while the Hamas-aligned Deir el-Balah Brings Us Together list won only two. Turnout diverged sharply: 56 percent in the West Bank but just 23 percent in Gaza, reflecting the deep challenges the territory faces after nearly two years of Israeli bombardment. In multiple West Bank cities, including Nablus and Ramallah, only one list was submitted, meaning candidates won automatically without a vote. Fatah framed the results as a “qualitative shift in the path of Palestinian democracy.” Critics, including the Palestinian National Initiative, boycotted the vote, arguing there was no genuine democratic competition. The Palestinian Authority has not held a presidential election in 21 years. NPR reports that Palestinian officials hailed the elections as a success, but analysts caution that elections without sovereignty risk legitimizing occupation rather than advancing self-determination.

Al Jazeera · NPR · CBC News · The Jerusalem Post · April 26–27, 2026

04 — Global Elections

Hungary’s Lesson: Disinformation Is Not a Political Magic Trick

Ongoing: EU / Hungary

Peter Magyar’s Tisza party swept to a two-thirds majority of 138 seats in Hungary’s 199-seat parliament on April 12, ousting Viktor Orbán after 16 years — despite one of the most sophisticated disinformation campaigns in European history. Investigators identified 17 high-reach TikTok channels launched in March 2026 alone, all using AI-generated personas to spread coordinated anti-Tisza messages. An AI-fabricated video of a Hungarian soldier killed in Ukraine became a global example of synthetic propaganda. Fourteen Fidesz candidates ran 162 political ads on Facebook in January, of which Meta caught only 19. Pro-Kremlin networks, including the Storm-1516 operation, published fake Euronews articles claiming Magyar had insulted Donald Trump. Yet the disinformation machine failed. TechPolicy.Press argues that hyperpolarization, paradoxically, made it easier for the opposition to unite: moderates grew disillusioned with propaganda and rallied behind Magyar, who walked hundreds of kilometers across rural Hungary and mobilized younger voters through social media at a scale the opposition had never managed. CNN reports that the incoming government now faces the challenge of dismantling Orbán’s “propaganda machine” — a state media apparatus built over a decade. Brussels Signal notes the election was procedurally clean, but the campaign was not.

TechPolicy.Press · Euronews · CNN · Brussels Signal · France 24 · April 13–22, 2026

05 — AI in Politics

68%
of Pennsylvania Voters Oppose AI Data Centers in Their Community

A Quinnipiac survey found that 68 percent of Pennsylvania voters would oppose the construction of an AI data center in their community — including 53 percent of Republicans. The backlash is now an electoral force: in the swing 7th, 8th, and 10th Districts, all rated toss-ups by the Cook Report, first-term Republican incumbents Ryan Mackenzie and Rob Bresnahan face rising fury over energy costs that have jumped 21.7 percent since 2025. In the 7th District, Democratic candidate Carol Obando-Derstine, a former energy engineer, is attacking Mackenzie for failing to address the energy and water impacts of a proposed data center. Pennsylvania lawmakers are mulling a three-year moratorium on “hyperscale” data centers exceeding 5,000 servers. CNBC reports that the data center issue now ranks alongside inflation and gas prices as a top-tier voter concern in the state. The opposition crosses traditional political lines: environmentalists, farmers worried about lost farmland, and conservative voters furious about rising utility costs have formed an unlikely coalition. Lawfare notes that AI data centers are reshaping the political map in ways that neither party anticipated.

CNBC · Quinnipiac · WESA · Spotlight PA · Lawfare · April 20–24, 2026

The MAGA Crack-Up: Right-Wing Media Breaks With Trump Over Iran

Ongoing: Midterm Headwinds

The most consequential fracture in the MAGA coalition since January 2021 is now in the open. Tucker Carlson told The Wall Street Journal he feels “betrayed” by Trump’s war in Iran, accusing the president of being “captured by neoconservatives and Israel” and abandoning the “America First” premise of his campaigns. 07 — AI in Politics

Congress Introduces Bipartisan Act to Mandate AI Content Labels

Compiled from Congress.gov, Nextgov/FCW, FCC, Perkins Coie

On April 24, Congresswoman Valerie Foushee (NC-04), Congressman Don Beyer (VA-08), and Congressman James Moylan (GU-AL) introduced the bipartisan Protecting Consumers from Deceptive AI Act, which would direct NIST to develop technical standards for identifying content created by generative artificial intelligence — including digital watermarking and fingerprinting for AI-generated audio and visual content. The bill would also require NIST to assist content providers with labeling content that has been modified through AI. It arrives as AI-generated political ads proliferate without disclosure. In Louisiana, House Bill 459 advanced out of committee on April 1, requiring political ads to disclose AI use, with a $10,000 fine for non-compliance. The FCC has separately proposed rules requiring broadcasters to disclose AI-generated content in political advertising. The Foushee-Beyer bill has drawn endorsements from Adobe, the Authors Guild, IEEE-USA, and the Society of Composers and Lyricists. Congresswoman Foushee framed the legislation as a direct response to deepfakes and synthetic media that pose major risks to consumers, elections, and public trust. The mandatory disclosure requirement would apply not only to official candidate campaigns but also to political action committees and other third-party entities engaged in electioneering. Campaign finance experts note that the bill, if passed, would represent the first federal statute specifically targeting AI-generated content in elections — filling a gap that over 600 state-level bills have tried to address in 2026 alone. Critics from the tech industry argue the labeling mandate could stifle innovation and impose technically unworkable requirements on platforms that host user-generated content. Supporters counter that without federal action, voters will face an election environment in which synthetic content is indistinguishable from reality.

Congress.gov · Nextgov/FCW · FCC · Perkins Coie · Transparency Coalition · April 24, 2026

08 — Political Technology

Russia Wages Digital War on Armenia Ahead of June Elections

Russia has launched an unusually early and comprehensive disinformation campaign targeting Armenia’s June 2026 parliamentary elections, in what analysts describe as a direct attempt to punish Yerevan for its pivot toward the West. The campaign is attributed to Storm-1516 and the Foundation to Battle Injustice — Russian information warfare groups previously active in the United States, Germany, France, and Moldova. The operations employ the Matryoshka strategy: a layered network of bots, trolls, and anonymous media resources using short vertical videos styled to resemble content from respected Western outlets. More than 100 Facebook pages promoting manipulative content and at least 17 YouTube channels have been identified by the Union of Informed Citizens, a respected Armenian civil society group. AI-generated photos, audio, and deepfakes are appearing in Armenia’s information space for the first time, with fabricated allegations of corruption and sexual crimes targeting Prime Minister Pashinyan and his government. Grey Dynamics reports that the campaign mirrors Russia’s playbook from Moldova’s 2024 elections, updated with more sophisticated synthetic media. The Kremlin-linked network Matryoshka responded to a bipartisan U.S. Senate letter by flooding platforms with a fresh wave of fakes about the upcoming elections.

EVN Report · CIVILNET · Grey Dynamics · MassisPost · OC Media · April 2026

09 — Social Media Trends

A Populist Backlash Over AI Is Brewing Across America

Time reports that if 2026 proves to be the year of AI takeoff, concerns about its effects on the economy, politics, and human relationships could coalesce into a potent populist political force. The backlash is already materializing around data center construction, which has become a particularly charged issue in swing states hosting the largest data center markets. In Virginia, electricity costs figured prominently in last November’s gubernatorial election, where typical data center facilities consume as much electricity as 100,000 households. In Arizona, local opposition has already blocked several major projects. An unlikely coalition has emerged: Senator Bernie Sanders and Florida Governor Ron DeSantis are both leading opponents of data center expansion. Republican senators Josh Hawley, Katie Britt, and Marsha Blackburn are demanding tougher AI regulation — notably at odds with their own president. MIT Technology Review analyzes why opinion on AI is so divided, finding that the politics of artificial intelligence do not map onto the traditional left-right spectrum and are spawning unexpected coalitions. As companies deploy AI more aggressively and workforce disruptions ripple across industries, job elimination may well become the next political lightning rod. The AI backlash is reshaping the electoral map in ways neither party has fully grasped.

Time · MIT Technology Review · Townhall · NPR · April 2026

10 — Political Campaigning

GOP Campaigns Deploy AI Voter Simulations While Democrats Hang Back

Axios reports a striking partisan divide in AI adoption on the campaign trail. Republican operatives are deploying a suite of AI tools that would have been science fiction two cycles ago. Aaru, a voter simulation platform, can replicate pools of voters with AI agents that can then be polled on their views — operatives load polling data and the system designs what a universe of voters in a particular area looks like. MiroFish forecasts public opinion using simulated people, allowing strategists to predict how voters might respond to events before they happen. EyesOver scans millions of social media sites per day to identify real-time trends in public sentiment. GOP strategists say AI agents that interact with voters by phone to persuade them to back Republican candidates are “on the horizon.” By contrast, Democratic operatives — many wary of privacy risks and concerned about AI displacing their jobs — have been much slower to adopt the technology, with the party not using AI in ads with few exceptions. The asymmetry raises a question that will define the 2026 cycle: is the GOP building a structural advantage in campaign technology, or is it exposing itself to a backlash if voters learn they are being canvassed by machines?

Axios · CampaignNow · American Prospect · April 14, 2026

11 — GovTech

U.S. Senators Demand Tech Platforms Shield Armenian Democracy

Senators Jeanne Shaheen, the ranking member of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee, and Thom Tillis (R-NC) have sent bipartisan letters to Meta and Alphabet urging them to devote sufficient resources to preventing Russian disinformation ahead of Armenia’s June 2026 parliamentary elections. The letters cite independent research documenting more than 100 Facebook pages with manipulative content and at least 17 YouTube channels promoting disinformation about the upcoming vote. The senators note that the campaign is directed by a Russian-Armenian oligarch previously listed by the first Trump administration as a “significant senior foreign political figure.” Independent experts assess that Russia is updating its playbook from Moldova to influence Armenia’s democratic process. The Kremlin-linked Matryoshka network responded to the Senate letters by flooding platforms with a fresh wave of fakes. The senators wrote: “While the United States must remain impartial in its views about the results of Armenia’s democratic process, we cannot turn a blind eye when we see a foreign power attempt to impose its will on the Armenian people.” The intervention is notable for its bipartisan character at a time when foreign election interference has become a deeply partisan issue in Washington.

13 — Split Graph

Should Big Tech Pay for the Infrastructure AI Demands?

The Progressive Case: Communities Bear the Cost, Tech Reaps the Profit

Progressive critics argue that AI data centers extract enormous public resources — energy, water, land — while returning minimal benefit to the communities that host them. In Pennsylvania, electric rates jumped 21.7 percent in 2025, a burden that falls disproportionately on working families. Senator Bernie Sanders has called for tech companies to pay their “fair share” of utility costs rather than shifting them onto ratepayers. Environmental groups point to the carbon footprint of hyperscale facilities, which consume as much electricity as 100,000 homes. In Arizona, community opposition has already blocked several major projects. The ACLU and local organizers argue that without binding community-benefit agreements, data center expansion amounts to a corporate subsidy underwritten by public infrastructure.

Time · NPR · CNBC · April 2026

The Conservative Case: AI Infrastructure Is a National Security Imperative

Pro-growth conservatives counter that data center construction creates high-paying jobs, drives tax revenue, and is essential to maintaining American dominance in the global AI race. Ben Shapiro, writing in Townhall, warns that the populist backlash risks “handing AI leadership to China” by blocking the physical infrastructure that makes domestic innovation possible. The White House’s National Policy Framework positions data center buildout as a pillar of national competitiveness. Industry advocates argue that moratoria like the one Pennsylvania is considering would drive investment to less-regulated states or overseas, costing communities the very economic benefits they claim to want. Governor DeSantis’s opposition, they note, is an outlier in a party that broadly supports infrastructure investment as an engine of growth.

Townhall · White House · Brookings · April 2026

14 — Political Tech Application

$ aaru --simulate --district=PA-07 --sample=10000

What it does: Aaru is an AI-powered voter simulation platform that replicates pools of voters with AI agents. Operatives load polling data, demographic information, and issue profiles into the system, and the AI constructs a synthetic electorate — a digital twin of the voters in a given district — that can then be polled on their views, tested with messaging, and stress-tested against hypothetical events. The platform uses large language models to generate voter personas that behave statistically like real respondents.

Who is using it: Republican campaigns and strategists. Axios reports that GOP operatives who have used Aaru say it does a strong job of modeling what a universe of voters in a particular area looks like. The platform is part of a broader suite of AI tools being deployed by the right, alongside MiroFish (which forecasts opinion shifts using simulated people) and EyesOver (which scans millions of social media posts daily for real-time sentiment analysis).

Reported outcomes: Strategists report that Aaru’s simulated voter panels produce responses that closely track real polling results, at a fraction of the cost and turnaround time. One operative described it as “like having a perpetual focus group running in the background.” The platform allows campaigns to A/B-test messaging before spending on ads, identify persuadable voter segments that traditional polling misses, and model the impact of news events on voter sentiment before they happen. Democrats have not adopted comparable tools at scale, creating what Axios calls a growing “AI gap” in campaign technology between the two parties.

> takeaway: When a campaign can poll 10,000 synthetic voters in minutes instead of spending weeks and six figures on traditional surveys, the economics of political intelligence change fundamentally. The question is whether simulated voters produce insights that hold up on Election Day — or whether campaigns are optimizing for a digital fiction.

Axios · CampaignNow · American Prospect · April 14, 2026
