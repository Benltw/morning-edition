01 · Research Insights of the Day

"Political Practitioners Poorly Predict Which Messages Persuade the Public"

Alexander Coppock, Seth J. Hill & Lynn Vavreck

Campaign professionals — the consultants, pollsters, and strategists who earn their living crafting political messages — perform barely better than chance when asked to predict which arguments will actually move voters. That is the central finding of a large-scale study that asked hundreds of political practitioners and ordinary citizens to forecast the persuasive effects of dozens of real campaign messages tested in randomized experiments. The practitioners, despite years of experience and access to proprietary data, showed no meaningful advantage over laypeople in identifying which messages would shift attitudes. The results challenge a foundational assumption of the campaign consulting industry: that expertise translates into reliable judgment about what works. The authors find that common theories about what makes advertising persuasive — emotional appeals, personal narratives, fear-based framing — have limited and context-dependent power to predict persuasiveness. Instead, the variation in ad effects appears to be driven by factors that neither experts nor theory consistently capture, suggesting that the persuasion landscape is more stochastic than the industry acknowledges.

Practitioner Implication

If expert intuition is unreliable, the case for rigorous experimental testing of campaign messages before deployment becomes not just useful but essential — gut instinct is a poor substitute for randomized trials.

PNAS, 2025 · doi:10.1073/pnas.2400076121

03 · Global Elections

India Votes: 90 Million Go to the Polls in Tamil Nadu and West Bengal

India's democratic machinery roared to life this morning as more than 90 million eligible voters across 386 constituencies began casting ballots in two of the country's most politically consequential state elections. Tamil Nadu voted in a single phase across all 234 assembly seats, with the ruling DMK-led alliance of Chief Minister M.K. Stalin facing its most formidable challenge yet: not only the traditional AIADMK-NDA opposition but also the electoral debut of actor-turned-politician Vijay's Tamilaga Vetri Kazhagam party, which has electrified younger voters. In West Bengal, half of 294 seats went to polls today in the first phase of a contest that pits Mamata Banerjee's Trinamool Congress against a resurgent BJP making its deepest push into the state's interior. BJP and TMC workers clashed in Siliguri, and reports of a crude bomb explosion surfaced in Murshidabad, underscoring the stakes. By 11 a.m., turnout stood at 37.56% in Tamil Nadu and 41.11% in West Bengal. Counting is scheduled for May 4, and the results will ripple well beyond state borders — shaping alliance calculations, BJP's southern expansion strategy, and the national narrative six months before the U.S. midterms remind us that 2026 is a global election year.

Three House Resignations in Seven Days Upend Majority Math

The U.S. House of Representatives lost three members in a single week — an extraordinary cluster of departures that has scrambled the chamber's already razor-thin majority and injected fresh uncertainty into midterm planning. On April 13, Republican Tony Gonzales of Texas and Democrat Eric Swalwell of California both announced they would resign rather than face near-certain expulsion votes. Gonzales had admitted to an affair with a staffer who later died by suicide; Swalwell faced allegations of sexual assault from multiple former staff members, which he denied. On April 21, Democrat Sheila Cherfilus-McCormick of Florida resigned minutes before the House Ethics Committee was set to recommend her punishment for 25 ethics violations tied to the alleged theft of $5 million in pandemic relief funds. All three seats now require special elections, creating simultaneous vacancies in Texas's 23rd, California's 14th, and Florida's 20th congressional districts. For campaign strategists, the immediate question is margin: with the House majority already narrow, three open seats create both opportunity and vulnerability, and the special election calendar will consume resources that both parties had earmarked for November.

05 · Election Security

ACLU Sues to Block the DOJ's First National Voter Database

For the first time in American history, the Department of Justice has demanded confidential voter registration data from all 50 states and the District of Columbia — and is compiling that data into a single federal record system. The ACLU and Common Cause filed suit this week to block what they call an unconstitutional "surveil-and-purge database," arguing it violates the National Voter Registration Act and the Constitution's delegation of election administration to the states. The DOJ has already sued 30 states to force compliance; at least 12 — including Texas, Ohio, Indiana, and Arkansas — have voluntarily handed over files containing Social Security numbers and driver's license data. Meanwhile, the administration has quietly retooled the SAVE (Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements) system, linking immigration records to Social Security and passport databases to flag potential noncitizens on voter rolls. According to DHS, SAVE has labeled 21,000 registered voters as "non-citizens" — but experts warn that the underlying SSA data is notoriously unreliable and was never designed for citizenship determinations. Judges in five states — Michigan, Oregon, California, Massachusetts, and Rhode Island — have already dismissed the DOJ's lawsuits. The legal battle will shape who is on the rolls when voters head to the polls in November.

ACLU, CNN, NPR, Votebeat, Campaign Legal Center, Stateline · March–April 2026

Newsom Signs First-of-Its-Kind AI Executive Order, Defying Trump

Governor Gavin Newsom signed executive order N-5-26 on March 30, making California the first state to require entities seeking government contracts to disclose their AI usage and policies for preventing the generation of child sexual abuse material, civil rights violations, and discrimination. The order also mandates watermarking of all AI-generated imagery and video produced by state officials, creates an independent review process for federal supply-chain designations of AI companies, and directs agencies to develop AI applications that expand public access to government services. The move is a direct challenge to the Trump administration's national AI framework, released March 20, which recommends Congress preempt state AI laws to establish a "single, minimally burdensome national standard." Newsom's office framed the contrast explicitly: "Unlike the Trump administration, California remains committed to ensuring that AI solutions cannot be misused by bad actors." The order follows a federal judge's temporary block of the Defense Department's supply-chain risk designation of Anthropic, the San Francisco AI company — a ruling Newsom's order effectively renders moot at the state level. For campaign professionals tracking the AI regulation terrain, California's order establishes the first concrete procurement standard that other blue states can replicate.

CalMatters, Washington Post, Axios, Governor of California · March 30–April 3, 2026

07 · AI in Politics / Global

AI's Silent Coup: How Algorithms Infiltrated British Governance

New Statesman · April 2026

Text composed by a large language model has made its way into an Act of Parliament. That sentence, buried in a New Statesman investigation published this month, captures the essence of a sovereignty question Britain has yet to answer: what happens when the foundational infrastructure of governance — drafting legislation, analyzing spending, writing ministerial speeches — is quietly handed to AI systems built in the United States and China? The magazine's reporting documents a cascade of integration points. During the 2025 Spending Review, AI analyzed departmental funding bids, influencing billions in government allocation decisions on housing, healthcare, and defense. The Bank of England uses machine learning for interest rate decisions. Members of Parliament have used chatbots to compose speeches for the House of Commons — identified, the magazine notes, by distinctive phrases that reveal their non-human origin. AI models can now read government data releases, draft media responses, and circulate press lines before traditional party structures have even scheduled a meeting to discuss them. "Large language models are built to produce answers that will be accepted by users — not to calculate, but to convince," the investigation warns. The core concern is not that AI is being used, but that it is being used without democratic deliberation about who controls the systems, where the data flows, and what accountability structures exist when algorithmic recommendations become government policy. Britain, the magazine argues, has handed over political power without ever really deciding to — and without fully understanding to whom.

Five Hours a Day: Heavy Social Media Use Linked to Support for Political Violence

Americans who spend at least five hours a day on social media are nearly three times more likely to say it is sometimes acceptable for people to use violence to achieve a political goal — 22% versus 8% among lighter users. They are also less likely to believe democracy is the best form of government, more likely to see facts as subjective (16% vs. 9%), and less open to political compromise. Those are the findings of a nationally representative study of more than 20,000 Americans conducted by Gallup and the Charles F. Kettering Foundation, released in early April. The paradox at the heart of the data is that heavy users also report feeling more politically heard and more connected to their communities — the same platforms that appear to erode democratic commitment simultaneously make users feel more democratically engaged. Researchers caution that the study does not establish causation: it remains unclear whether social media drives these attitudes or whether people with these attitudes are drawn to heavy social media use. But for campaign strategists navigating a cycle in which voter mobilization increasingly runs through algorithmic feeds, the data raises an uncomfortable question: does the infrastructure of modern political engagement also undermine the democratic norms it depends on?

Washington Post, Gallup, Axios, Kettering Foundation · April 2, 2026

09 · Political Technology

GOP Goes All-In on AI While Democrats Play Catch-Up

A partisan AI gap has opened in American campaign politics — and it runs in the opposite direction from what most observers expected. According to a survey by the American Association of Political Consultants, 64% of Republican consultants now use AI daily in their campaign work, compared to 49% of Democrats. Republican campaigns are deploying AI for ad generation, voter modeling, opposition research, and fundraising copy at a pace that has caught Democratic operatives off guard. The asymmetry extends beyond tools to posture: Democratic campaigns have largely avoided using AI in advertisements, with few exceptions, while Republican committees have embraced AI-generated content — including the National Republican Senatorial Committee's deepfake of Texas Democrat James Talarico. The gap reflects a deeper strategic divergence. GOP operatives view AI as a force multiplier that compensates for Democrats' traditional advantage in grassroots organizing; Democratic strategists worry that aggressive adoption will invite regulatory backlash and erode voter trust. The National Democratic Training Committee has released an AI playbook, but adoption remains cautious. For the party that once held the edge in digital innovation — from the Obama 2008 data operation to ActBlue's fundraising revolution — the lag is a strategic liability that grows more costly with every news cycle.

From Boxing Deepfakes to Senate Races: AI Campaign Ads Go Mainstream

AI-generated political advertising has crossed the threshold from novelty to standard practice. In New Jersey's governor's race, Democrat Josh Gottheimer launched his first television ad — roughly 80% AI-generated — depicting himself as a boxer sparring with President Trump in a ring. The ad carries a small "AI Generated Imagery" disclaimer, but does not distinguish which frames are synthetic and which are real. It is backed by a multimillion-dollar buy. Meanwhile, the National Republican Senatorial Committee's deepfake of Texas Democrat James Talarico, released in March, remains the cycle's most discussed example of weaponized synthetic media — a full video of a candidate making fabricated statements, with only a watermark to signal its origins. At the local level, a candidate in Shelby County, Tennessee is running her campaign almost entirely on AI-generated literature and imagery. At least 15 campaign ads featuring AI-generated content have aired since November 2025, and the tools that produce them — from consumer-grade video platforms to purpose-built campaign software — are now accessible to any operation with a subscription and a prompt. The regulatory landscape remains a patchwork: 31 states have some form of deepfake election law, but federal rules remain nonexistent.

NBC News, Daily Signal, CNN, ActionNews5 · March–April 2026

11 · Political Technology

Higher Ground Labs Sounds the Alarm on the Voter Roll Tech Crisis

The progressive venture fund Higher Ground Labs — which has deployed more than $50 million in political technology investments since 2017 — has published its starkest warning yet about what it calls the "voter roll crisis" facing the 2026 midterms. The report documents how the Trump administration's campaign to centralize voter data has exposed a fundamental vulnerability in democratic infrastructure: most campaign and election technology was designed to operate in a cooperative federal-state environment and is not resilient to adversarial federal pressure. Higher Ground Labs recommends that campaigns and civic organizations immediately invest in encrypted communications and distributed storage architectures that prevent comprehensive data seizures, build open-source alternatives for campaign infrastructure, conduct data security audits with strict access controls and retention policies, and develop vendor accountability systems ensuring technology providers resist voluntary federal data requests. The report frames the threat not as a future possibility but as a present emergency: with the DOJ actively suing states for voter files and the SAVE system already flagging thousands of legitimate voters, the organizations that serve voters need infrastructure that functions under conditions of institutional conflict — not just institutional cooperation.

Higher Ground Labs, 2026 Series: Election Disruption and Political Tech · April 2026

$2.8 Million and Counting: AI Super PACs Target Texas Congressional Races

The AI super PAC arms race has found its primary battlefield: Texas. At least seven candidates running for Congress in the state have collectively received more than $2.8 million in support from AI-linked super PACs, making it the most AI-funded state on the midterm map. The money flows from both sides of the regulatory debate. Leading the Future — the pro-AI super PAC network backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale — is supporting candidates who oppose restrictive AI regulation. On the other side, Anthropic-backed Public First Action is channeling resources toward candidates who favor guardrails. Two former U.S. Representatives have launched new super PACs explicitly to counter the pro-industry money. The result is a set of Texas races where AI policy is no longer a second-tier issue but a primary line of attack and defense — a preview of what the national landscape will look like by fall. For campaign professionals, Texas is the proving ground for whether AI industry money can buy policy outcomes at the congressional level, or whether voters will push back against the perception that their representatives are being purchased by the companies building the tools that shape their information environment.

13 · Split Graph

Should the Federal Government Preempt State AI Laws?

The Case Against Preemption

States have been the primary laboratories of AI governance, with 31 states enacting deepfake election laws and California pioneering procurement standards that address bias, civil rights, and child safety. The Trump administration's national AI framework — which calls on Congress to preempt state laws that "impose undue burdens" — would strip states of their authority to protect their own citizens. Rep. Don Beyer and four Democratic co-sponsors introduced the GUARDRAILS Act on March 20 to repeal the executive order and block the preemption moratorium.

Proponents argue that the same AI companies funding preemption through $140 million in super PAC spending have a structural conflict of interest, and that a weak federal standard would create not uniformity but a regulatory vacuum.

Sources: GUARDRAILS Act text, March 20, 2026; CalMatters, April 2, 2026; TNW, April 2026

The Case for a Federal Standard

A patchwork of 50 different state AI laws creates compliance chaos that disproportionately harms startups and small campaigns that cannot afford to navigate conflicting requirements. The White House framework argues that a single federal standard — administered through existing regulatory agencies with subject-matter expertise — would provide clarity without creating a new bureaucracy. Industry-led standards, the administration contends, are more agile than legislation and keep the U.S. competitive with China.

Supporters point to the Executive Order's carve-outs for child safety, data center permitting, and state procurement as evidence of a balanced approach. The goal, proponents say, is not deregulation but standardization.

Sources: White House National AI Policy Framework, March 20, 2026; Gibson Dunn analysis, March 2026; Holland & Knight, March 2026
