03 · Political TechnologyDateline: Maine
Caution · AI Synthetic Media
03
The Platner Ad

A deepfake of the Democratic nominee for Maine Senate is now on the air — with a disclosure tag, per state law.

National Republicans synthesized an image and voice of Graham Platner, rendered shirtless, reading back old Reddit posts from 10–15 years ago. The spot carries the small-print "deepfake content" disclosure that Governor Janet Mills signed into Maine law last month. A disclosure, not a guardrail.

The National Republican Senatorial Committee's spot is the second confirmed deepfake this cycle aimed at a sitting Democratic Senate candidate, after an 85-second fabrication of Texas nominee James Talarico in March that a forensics expert said he could not distinguish from authentic footage. A Reuters review has now logged at least five confirmed deepfake incidents across Texas, Georgia, Maine and Massachusetts.

"We are in an era where AI is going to be abused," Platner said of the ad. Twenty-eight U.S. states now require some kind of AI disclosure in political advertising; federal law still requires nothing at all.

Nearly half of voters told pollsters this cycle that deepfakes had "some influence" on their election decisions — even as most said they distrust synthetic media on principle.
04 · GovTech · Election SecurityDateline: Washington
FY27 BUDGET · 07 APRIL 2026
04

The White House proposes to cut $707 million from CISA — and to zero out its election-security program entirely.

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency's FY27 request would fold the agency from roughly $2.9 billion to $2.4 billion, eliminate dedicated election-security advisors in all fifty states, and end federal support for the Elections Infrastructure ISAC seven months before the midterms.

In addition to the top-line cut, the proposal would end CISA's information-sharing agreements with secretaries of state and county clerks, effectively privatizing the threat-intel pipeline that was stood up in the wake of 2016. Maine has already refused roughly $130,000 in federal cybersecurity support rather than accept new grant conditions that bar participation in the EI-ISAC.

The cuts coincide with the firing of General Timothy Haugh as head of U.S. Cyber Command and the National Security Agency. State election officials are, in the words of Arizona Secretary of State Adrian Fontes, "effectively flying blind," scrambling to replace federal services with a patchwork of phone calls and email lists as the 2026 primary season ramps.

"The rollback will have serious consequences." Al Schmidt · Pennsylvania Secretary of State

06 · Political CampaigningDateline: Austin · Houston
Filed · Texas 2026 Primary · Pro-AI spend tops $2.8M in 7 House races

The AI industry has opened a new front — and it is paying for congressional primaries.

06
"Leading the Future," a pro-AI super PAC backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, has committed more than $100 million to 2026 federal races. Anthropic has separately pledged $20 million to candidates who favor guardrails. The opening salvos are already in Texas mailboxes.

Seven candidates for Congress in Texas alone have collectively received $2.8 million in support from AI-aligned super PACs in the run-up to the state's primaries, according to FEC filings compiled by Houston Public Media and the NBC News politics desk. The ads, notably, are almost never about AI itself: they run on crime, the border, energy and schools.

That is by design. Strategists behind "Leading the Future" have told donors the goal is not to make the 2026 midterms a referendum on artificial intelligence, but to quietly seat a bipartisan cohort of members who will block state-level regulation and keep a federal preemption bill alive in the next Congress.

Opposing the effort: an equally well-funded cluster of civil-society groups warning that the money is an attempt to buy permissive rules for an industry that, a year ago, was publicly asking Washington to slow it down. Anthropic's pledge sits awkwardly in the middle — supporting what its leadership describes as candidates willing to vote for guardrails.

The practical effect, in any case, is that 2026 is the first American election cycle with a named, self-identified AI lobby running television. It will not be the last.

08 · GovTech · Political TechnologyWisconsin Law Journal · 13 April 2026
Case Study · The Supreme Court Chatbot
viii

The Wisconsin chatbot that texted 1.1 million voters — and what it settled about the 2026 playbook.

In a state Supreme Court race, a campaign deployed an AI-powered text agent that opened conversations with more than 1.1 million Wisconsin voters, answering questions about polling locations, candidate positions and ballot mechanics at a scale no field program could have reached by hand. The Wisconsin Law Journal reported the deployment this week as the first large, civil-court-level test of conversational AI in an American judicial election.

What makes the Wisconsin case instructive is not the novelty — similar bots have been tested in primaries elsewhere — but the response cycle. Voters replied. Many asked basic civic questions. A meaningful slice, surveyed afterward, said they had never had a two-way exchange with a campaign before, and rated the bot more useful than a mailer. Opposition counsel raised disclosure concerns; the campaign pointed to its first-message identification as an AI assistant.

The academic reading: the barrier to entry for personalized voter contact has collapsed from a thirty-staffer call center to a configuration file. The political reading, which operators in both parties are now quietly circulating, is that the deliverability of an AI text conversation is the new unit of campaign work — and that the advantage accrues to the team willing to say, on the record, that it used one.

09 · GovTechDateline: The White House
The Paper of RecordFriday, April 17, 2026 · Section C

A $2.7 trillion wager, and the question of who holds the ledger.

The White House is now framing U.S. AI policy as a headline investment number. What goes into that number, and what it obscures, matters for every downstream question about federal technology in 2026.
Analysis · Staff Report

The administration's current talking point is that it has attracted "north of $2.7 trillion" in tech and AI investment, including a $90 billion package of AI and energy commitments in Pennsylvania. That figure rolls together multi-year corporate capex announcements, data-center memoranda of understanding, and grid-upgrade pledges tied to foreign sovereign funds. It is, by construction, an aspirational running total rather than an audited flow. Useful for a press conference; harder to reconcile against a budget.

Against the same backdrop, federal agencies are accelerating concrete AI use-cases — GAO has flagged procurement bottlenecks, but documented active deployments in benefits processing, veterans' services, FAA operations and IRS correspondence review. The Office of Management and Budget's inventory of agency AI systems grew again this quarter. California, from the other direction, has just ordered its General Services and Technology departments to rewrite state GenAI procurement rules to force vendors to disclose their guardrail policies.

Two different ledgers are being kept, in other words. One, in the public narrative, is an investment total that doubles as a political asset. The other, in the agency trenches, is an operational inventory of live systems — most of which depend on federal standards work at NIST and threat-intel at CISA, both of which are facing cuts in the same FY27 budget document that celebrates the investment headline.

The gap between the two ledgers is where the 2026 GovTech story actually lives. Reconciling them — deciding which number is the one Congress, governors and procurement officers should steer by — is the assignment now falling to state CIOs and inspectors general, not to the podium.

From the feed to the pod: 2026 is the first cycle where "influencer" is a campaign job title.

Pew finds 21 percent of U.S. adults, and 37 percent of adults under 30, regularly get news from social-media influencers. Operators are responding the way operators do — with staff, budgets and green-rooms.
Podcasts Long-form YouTube Creator budgets Short-form Reels Substack endorsements Disclosure gaps
Campaigns on both sides are shifting budget out of paid-social ad buys and into creator retainers and long-form podcast and YouTube bookings, according to Katie Harbath's widely circulated "10 Political Digital Trends" outlook and the Reuters Institute's 2026 journalism report. The strategy trades reach for perceived authenticity — and trades one disclosure problem (paid advertising) for another (creator compensation that is, at present, effectively unregulated).
TikTok sits uncomfortably in the mix. A Columbia Business School review finds the platform moves negative emotional affect more than political attitudes, and a recent field experiment on young adults suggests exposure to political influencer content raises anxiety and depression without shifting voter preferences. Operators, reading the same research, have begun sliding TikTok down the priority list — not off it, but below YouTube and podcast pipelines that convert listeners to donors and volunteers more reliably.
