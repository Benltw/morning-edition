TSMC prints money.
AI chips do the rest.

A 58% jump in Q1 net profit, a fourth consecutive record quarter, and full-year 2026 guidance raised above 30% growth — the foundry that makes the world's AI is still bottlenecked by demand it cannot meet.

TSMC reported NT$572.48 billion in net income for the quarter ending March, beating analyst estimates on the back of what executives called "sustained, structural" demand for advanced nodes. Nvidia has already locked up the majority of CoWoS advanced-packaging capacity; two new packaging plants are being raced to production in Taiwan, with two more planned in Arizona before year-end, and a new fab announced for Tainan. Taiwan now supplies more than 90% of leading-edge logic chips, which means every AI breakthrough — and every cloud capex headline — still threads through a single 36,000-square-kilometre island. ASML's results, due next week, will tell us whether the lithography side is keeping pace or quietly pulling the brake lever on the whole stack.

Europe is quietly
drafting a NATO
Plan B.

Senior officials in Berlin, Paris, Warsaw, London and Ottawa are discussing — with unusual candour — what a credible Atlantic alliance looks like if Washington walks. For the first time since 1949, the answer is not "it does not."

The conversation has moved from think-tank salons into ministries. Europe's largest NATO members and Canada are drawing up contingency structures that could function without the American nuclear umbrella and without US strategic enablers — satellite ISR, air-to-air refuelling, long-range munitions — that currently backstop almost every European operation. Sergey Lavrov's visit to Beijing this week, where he and his Chinese counterpart jointly celebrated the "failure" of Western hegemony, sharpened the planning. The awkward truth: without the United States, Europe has the factories but not the glue. Building the glue — shared command, integrated air defence, and European long-range strike — is the silent project of 2026, and it is already reshaping defence budgets from Warsaw to The Hague.

Berlin's recovery story has stalled again.

The government's 2026 outlook has been cut from 1.0% to 0.5%, 2027 from 1.3% to 0.9%, and inflation revised up to 2.7% — all of it triggered by the Iran energy shock crashing into a still-weak industrial base.

Five leading institutes now see 2026 growth at just 0.6%. Industrial production slipped 0.3% in February, before the oil spike. The Bundesbank still officially expects a gradual recovery; the ifo index does not agree, falling to 86.4 in March. The political pressure is real: Chancellor Merz recorded the lowest approval rating of any elected leader in a 24-country survey this week, with 76% disapproval. The coalition's answer — €500 billion in infrastructure-and-climate borrowing plus a loosened debt brake to fund defence — is the biggest German fiscal bet since reunification. Whether it works depends almost entirely on whether industrial demand returns before the political patience does.

The Fed-cut trade
is dead.

Oil-driven inflation from the Iran war has forced traders to unwind the most crowded bet of 2026 — expected Federal Reserve easing — and sent short-maturity yields vaulting higher. The US 10-year is back to 4.34%.

Central-bank communications this week turned unmistakably hawkish. The Bank of England, the ECB, and the Fed all independently warned that near-term inflation risks have shifted up. Market pricing has now fully erased the expectation of further Fed easing in 2026, after markets entered the year pricing in at least two cuts. Treasury market liquidity has held up better than feared through the tariff litigation and Supreme Court ruling, but emerging-market resilience is the more striking story: Liberty Street's latest work shows EM currencies absorbing shocks with a fraction of the volatility of a decade ago. The bond-market equilibrium of the late 2010s is over; what replaces it is, for now, being priced day by day.

Germany · Europe · China

The Merz moment,
and a fracturing Europe.

Friedrich Merz has the most unpopular government on earth — and arguably the most consequential European agenda since Kohl. Both can be true.

Five months into Merz's chancellorship, the numbers look bleak. Insa has him at 19% approval, 76% disapproval — the lowest of any elected leader in a 24-country survey published this week. The economy is flat; the Iran war has reignited the energy-cost debate; industrial production is slipping again; and the AfD continues to poll well above its February result. And yet, under the surface, the Merz–SPD coalition is doing real things: a €500 billion infrastructure and climate fund, a reform of the debt brake to finance defence, a planned 25% cut in bureaucracy costs worth an estimated €16 billion, and — most importantly — an informal but tightening leadership role in the post-American NATO conversation. The EU's fight with China over industrial overcapacity, from electric vehicles to mature-node semiconductors to wind-turbine components, will be largely fought from Berlin, because Berlin still has the factories and the balance sheet. Merz's unpopularity is not evidence of failure; it is the reliable cost of governing Germany through a structural adjustment. Helmut Schmidt had it. Gerhard Schröder had it. The question for 2026 is whether the coalition has the discipline to spend political capital it does not feel it has — on an industrial strategy, a European security architecture, and an AI-and-data policy that, almost by accident, Berlin is now positioned to lead.
