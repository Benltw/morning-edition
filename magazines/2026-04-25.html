01 — Research Insights of the Day

Persuading Voters Using Human–Artificial Intelligence Dialogues

Samuele Marro, Filippo Sala, Yara Kyrychenko, Max Bartolo, Cesare Fracassi · University of Oxford · University College London

Conversational AI chatbots can shift voter attitudes roughly four times more effectively than conventional political advertisements. In pre-registered experiments spanning three national elections — the 2024 U.S. presidential race, the 2025 Canadian federal election, and the 2025 Polish presidential election — researchers had participants engage in dialogues with large language models that advocated for one of the two leading candidates. Trump supporters who chatted with a model favoring Harris moved 3.9 points toward her on a 100-point scale, approximately quadrupling the measured persuasive effect of campaign ads from the 2016 and 2020 cycles. A companion study published in Science by a multi-university team (N = 77,000 participants, 91,000 dialogues) found that targeted post-training — including supervised fine-tuning and reward modeling — can boost a model’s persuasiveness by up to 51 percent, but with a troubling tradeoff: the more persuasive the model, the less accurate its information tended to be.

Practitioner Implication

The persuasion-accuracy tradeoff means campaigns face an incentive to optimize chatbots for attitude change at the expense of truthfulness — regulators and platform designers should treat conversational AI as a distinct category of political communication, not merely an extension of existing ad rules.

Nature, 2025 · Science, 2025 · MIT Technology Review, December 2025 · Oxford University News, December 2025

03 — Global Elections

Magyar Unveils Expert Cabinet, Races to Unlock Frozen EU Billions

Two weeks after his Tisza party won a two-thirds supermajority in Hungary’s parliament — ending Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule with a record 79.6 percent turnout — Prime Minister-designate Péter Magyar announced his cabinet picks. The slate is heavy on technocrats: separate ministries for health, environmental protection, and education will be restored after being dissolved under Orbán. Magyar told Bloomberg the government’s overriding priority is to unlock 19 billion euros in EU funding frozen over rule-of-law concerns, 10 billion of which expires at the end of August. He has pledged constitutional amendments to introduce a two-term limit for the prime minister and restore checks and balances dismantled over the past decade. The president has assured Magyar his government could take power in the first week of May — an accelerated timeline that underscores the urgency of the democratic transition.

Bloomberg · Washington Post · Euronews · Fortune · April 15–22, 2026

04 — U.S. Politics

DeSantis Goes Dark: Florida Redistricting Session Opens Without Public Maps

Florida’s special legislative session on congressional redistricting convened this week with no proposed maps made public. Legislators slated to vote had not seen the new district lines as of Thursday night, according to multiple reports. Governor Ron DeSantis called the session in January, citing malapportionment, but the process has drawn fire from both parties. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries accused DeSantis of a “brazen power grab,” while some senior Republicans in Washington warned the effort is “fraught with peril.” Florida’s constitution expressly bans drawing districts “with the intent to favor or disfavor a political party,” and any new map is almost certain to trigger litigation. The GOP hopes to pick up two to three seats in the state — critical after Virginia Democrats flipped potentially four seats through their own redistricting referendum this week. Former Attorney General Eric Holder urged Floridians to tell the legislature its effort is “not acceptable.”

CNN · Axios · The Hill · WFSU · Florida Phoenix · April 21–24, 2026

Ongoing: Data Center Backlash

A Quinnipiac survey found that 68 percent of Pennsylvania voters would oppose the construction of an AI data center in their community — including 53 percent of Republicans. The backlash is now threatening GOP incumbents in eastern Pennsylvania’s competitive swing districts: the 7th, 8th, and 10th districts are all listed as “toss-ups” by the Cook Report, and analysts say incumbents will bear the brunt of voter anger over rising energy prices and environmental damage linked to data center construction. An unlikely coalition has formed — environmental groups alongside populist Trump supporters — united by resentment of large-scale industrial AI infrastructure. Governor Josh Shapiro has dialed down his initial enthusiasm and now proposes guidelines requiring companies to fund their own energy infrastructure and meet environmental standards. State Senator Katie Muth has introduced a three-year moratorium on hyperscale data centers, backed by a Republican co-sponsor.

06 — GovTech

Washington Signs Nation’s First Chatbot Safety Law with Private Right of Action

Ongoing: AI Regulation

Governor Bob Ferguson signed House Bill 2225 into law, making Washington the first state to regulate AI companion chatbots with a private right of action. The Conversational AI Safety Act requires companies to tell users upfront that they are talking to a robot, with reminders every three hours — and every hour for minors. If an operator knows the user is under 18, it must implement measures to prevent the chatbot from generating explicit sexual content or using manipulative engagement techniques. Operators must also maintain and publicly disclose protocols to detect and respond to users expressing suicidal ideation, including publishing the number of crisis referral notifications issued. The law is notable for its enforcement mechanism: unlike most state privacy laws, it grants individuals a private right of action, allowing citizens to sue companies directly. The chatbot safety rules take effect in January 2027, with AI-generated media labeling requirements following in February 2027.

Transparency Coalition · Hunton Andrews Kurth · Axios Seattle · KUOW · March–April 2026

07 — AI in Politics

Yale: AI Chatbots Shift Political Opinions Even Without Trying

A Yale University study published in March 2026 found that AI chatbots can influence users’ political opinions even when they are not designed or prompted to do so. The research demonstrates what the team calls “hidden bias” — subtle patterns in training data and model behavior that steer users toward particular political positions through ordinary, apparently neutral conversations. The finding complicates the regulatory picture significantly: if persuasion requires no intent, then disclosure rules that target only deliberately persuasive AI may miss the larger phenomenon. The study builds on the Nature and Science papers showing intentional persuasion is powerful, adding a second vector of concern — that the default behavior of widely deployed chatbots may be shifting the political landscape in ways neither their creators nor their users fully understand.

Yale News · March 3, 2026

08 — GovTech

600+ State AI Bills Flood the Regulatory Gap

Compiled from Transparency Coalition, Cooley, Kiteworks, Global Policy Watch

State lawmakers have introduced over 600 AI bills with requirements for private entities in the 2026 legislative sessions so far — a number that would have been unthinkable even two years ago. Six weeks into the legislative season, 78 chatbot-specific bills are alive in 27 states. Washington and Utah have moved fastest: Governor Ferguson signed two AI bills in a single ceremony, while Governor Spencer Cox signed all nine AI bills that reached his desk, including eight in the past two weeks. The enacted laws cluster around four themes: companion chatbot safety, AI transparency and watermarking, digital replicas and synthetic content, and the use of AI by mental health providers and health insurers. Cooley’s state-by-state tracker counts an additional 27 bills that have passed both chambers and await gubernatorial signatures. The surge comes as the White House’s National Policy Framework recommends federal preemption of state AI laws that “impose undue burdens” — a collision course between a federal government that wants to slow regulation down and states racing to fill the void. The practical effect for political campaigns and tech companies is a patchwork of compliance obligations that varies by jurisdiction, creating pressure for either a federal floor or a set of industry-wide standards that could satisfy multiple state regimes at once.

Transparency Coalition · Cooley · Global Policy Watch · Kiteworks · April 24, 2026

09 — Political Campaigning

Conservative-Progressive Alliance Pledges Eight Figures for AI Guardrails

A coalition that includes the Family Policy Alliance, the National Center on Sexual Exploitation, the Heritage Foundation, and the American Principles Project has launched a campaign to block what it calls tech companies’ effort to “give AI companies regulatory and legal amnesty.” The group plans to spend at least eight figures in 2026 to support its advocacy. The coalition’s existence complicates the narrative that AI regulation splits cleanly along partisan lines. On the left, a growing number of Democratic candidates are making AI policy a centerpiece of their 2026 campaigns. On the right, a new conservative group reported by Axios is pushing for tougher AI rules, particularly around child safety and chatbot interactions. The shared target: AI companies that have lobbied aggressively against state-level bills. The alliance’s formation signals that AI governance is becoming a rare issue where left and right populism can converge, even as their underlying motives differ sharply.

The Hill · Axios · Roll Call · March–April 2026

10 — Political Technology

Maine Campaigns Deploy AI Games and Automated Outreach to Win Voters

Voters in Maine should be on the lookout for AI-generated photos, videos, games, and automated outreach as November approaches. A Republican gubernatorial candidate has created an AI-generated online game where voters engage directly with a political platform — a novel use of interactive AI that goes beyond the usual ad-and-email playbook. Campaign managers in both parties are embracing the technology’s creative potential for voter engagement, with AI tools handling labor-intensive research so human staff can focus on face-to-face organizing. The University of Maine’s Mark Brewer notes the technology could reshape politics the way social media did two decades ago — for better or worse. Maine has responded with LD1690, a bill requiring disclosure when images, audio, or video used in political communications have been materially manipulated by AI. Campaign experts warn that the rapidly changing AI landscape may leave voters unclear about what is real and what is not.

12 — Global Elections

Can Orbanism Survive Without Orban?

Chatham House and Foreign Policy have both published analyses asking whether Hungary’s illiberal institutional architecture can outlast the man who built it. Orbán spent 16 years stacking courts, centralizing media ownership, rewriting the constitution to entrench his party’s advantages, and building a patronage network that funneled EU funds to political allies. Magyar’s two-thirds supermajority gives him the constitutional power to dismantle these structures — but the practical challenge is enormous. Judges appointed for life, media conglomerates with deep state ties, and a civil service shaped by a generation of Fidesz loyalism will not yield to a parliamentary vote alone. Bloomberg’s editorial board calls the transition “both dangerous and necessary,” warning that rushing constitutional amendments could replicate the very authoritarian tactics Magyar has promised to end. The 19 billion euros in frozen EU funds add a financial clock: if Magyar cannot demonstrate credible rule-of-law reforms by August, 10 billion of those euros expire permanently.

Chatham House · Foreign Policy · Bloomberg Opinion · Christian Science Monitor · April 17–24, 2026

13 — Split Graph

Should AI Data Centers Get Public Subsidies?

The Case Against: Community Cost, Corporate Profit

Environmental groups and populist organizers argue that AI data centers externalize massive costs — surging energy prices, water consumption, noise pollution, and strained power grids — onto communities that see few local jobs in return. A Quinnipiac poll found 68 percent of Pennsylvania voters oppose data center construction in their communities. State Senator Katie Muth has proposed a three-year moratorium on hyperscale facilities, arguing that taxpayers should not subsidize infrastructure that primarily benefits a handful of trillion-dollar tech companies. The Lawfare Institute notes that data centers are becoming a “kitchen-table issue” in swing districts, with a bipartisan coalition of residents demanding that companies fund their own energy infrastructure.

Quinnipiac · Lawfare · City & State PA · April 2026

The Case For: Strategic Asset, National Security

Proponents frame domestic AI infrastructure as a national security imperative. The refrain that the U.S. is in a race with China over artificial intelligence has become a favorite of both parties, with the administration’s action plan warning that America’s private sector “must be unencumbered by bureaucratic red tape.” Ben Shapiro, writing in the Daily Wire, calls data center opposition a form of populism that risks ceding AI leadership to authoritarian competitors. Supporters cite job creation during construction, expanded tax bases, and the downstream economic activity generated by AI-powered industries. Governor Shapiro’s revised position — supporting data centers with conditions — reflects the middle ground: welcome the investment, but require companies to internalize environmental and energy costs.

WorldNetDaily · Roll Call · CNBC · April 2026

14 — Political Tech Application

$ ai_campaign_game --platform=web

What it does: A Republican gubernatorial candidate in Maine has built an AI-generated online game that lets voters interact directly with the candidate’s political platform. Instead of reading a policy page or watching an ad, voters play through scenarios that illustrate the candidate’s positions on issues like energy, education, and taxes — with AI generating dynamic responses to player choices.

Who is using it: The game is deployed by a GOP primary campaign in Maine’s 2026 gubernatorial race, part of a broader push by the campaign to use AI tools for voter engagement, including AI-generated photos, videos, and automated outreach. Campaign managers say the interactive format reaches voters who would never open a fundraising email or watch a 30-second spot.

Reported outcomes: The campaign reports higher engagement times compared to traditional digital ads, though specific metrics have not been published. The University of Maine’s Mark Brewer notes that interactive AI formats may reshape political communication the way social media did — but warns that the line between engagement and manipulation is not yet well defined.

> takeaway: AI campaign games represent a new frontier beyond ads and emails. When voters play a candidate’s platform instead of reading it, the persuasion mechanism shifts from information delivery to experiential engagement — raising questions about disclosure, consent, and whether existing campaign-ad regulations apply at all.

Bangor Daily News · UMaine News · April 13, 2026
