Research Insights of the Day

Unravelling the Link Between Non-Voting and Political Knowledge: An Exploratory Study

Soares, F. G. et al. · Electoral Studies / ScienceDirect · April 2026

Conventional wisdom assumes that non-voters are simply uninformed, but this paper challenges that premise. Using survey data from over 6,000 respondents across five European democracies, the authors find that a significant subset of habitual non-voters score at or above the median on measures of political knowledge — identifying institutions, policy positions, and electoral rules correctly. What differentiates this cohort is not ignorance but alienation: they report high political knowledge coupled with low political efficacy, a combination the authors term "informed disengagement." The study also finds that informed non-voters are disproportionately young, urban, and likely to consume political news through social media rather than broadcast television.

Practitioner implication: Campaigns targeting low-turnout demographics should not default to "awareness" messaging. For informed non-voters, persuasion depends on restoring a sense of efficacy — demonstrating that participation yields tangible, visible outcomes.

Soares, F. G. et al. (2026). "Unravelling the link between non-voting and political knowledge." Electoral Studies / ScienceDirect. Published online April 2026.

Comey Indicted Again — This Time Over Seashells on a Beach

A North Carolina grand jury has indicted former FBI Director James Comey for a second time, charging him with threatening the life of the president. The basis: an Instagram photograph posted last May showing seashells arranged to spell "86 47" — old slang for "get rid of" paired with Trump's presidential number. Comey captioned it "Cool shell formation on my beach walk" and later deleted it, saying he did not realize some people associated the numbers with violence. Legal scholars broadly view the charge as a stretch under First Amendment precedent, which requires proof that a statement constitutes a "true threat" rather than protected political expression. The indictment is part of a broader wave of prosecutions against Trump's perceived adversaries — including former New York AG Letitia James and ex-National Security Adviser John Bolton — that the Washington Post reports is becoming a political headache for the GOP heading into the midterms. Republican strategists privately worry the retribution cases distract from economic messaging and energize Democratic turnout.

NPR, CNN, Axios, Washington Post · April 28-29, 2026

Virginia Redistricting Backfires: GOP's Mid-Decade Gamble Goes South

Ongoing: Redistricting. When President Trump urged Republican-led states to redraw congressional maps ahead of the midterms, the strategy was supposed to pad the GOP's razor-thin House majority. In Virginia, it has done the opposite. Voters approved a new map on Tuesday that could leave Republicans with just one congressional seat, down from five. The Virginia Supreme Court is now hearing a GOP challenge to invalidate the voter-approved plan, but the damage is done politically. As Rep. Steve Womack (R-Ark.) put it: "We have to understand that for every action, there are second- and third-order effects that we could live to regret." Axios reports the overall mid-decade redistricting battle has produced roughly nine new Republican seats nationwide — and ten new Democratic ones. The net result: a wash, if not a slight Democratic advantage, achieved at enormous political cost.

Axios, NBC News, PBS, Brookings, Democracy Docket · April 23-29, 2026

AI Super PAC War Chest

Silicon Valley's Biggest Midterm Bet Yet

Ongoing: AI + money in politics. The AI industry has assembled at least $70 million in super PAC funds to shape the 2026 midterms, and Texas has become ground zero. More than $2.8 million has already flowed into Texas congressional races alone, with two rival factions leading the charge. Leading the Future Network, backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, raised over $50 million in its first year and backs candidates favoring minimal regulation. On the other side, Public First Action — which received a self-reported $20 million from Anthropic — favors stronger safety guardrails. NBC News reports that AI-funded ads are flooding the midterms on issues ranging from immigration to healthcare, often without mentioning AI at all. The result is an election cycle where the technology industry's policy preferences are being laundered through kitchen-table issue advertising.

Meta Rolls Out AI-Powered Election Integrity Plan for 2026

Meta has unveiled a comprehensive election security framework for the midterms, anchored by AI detection systems and the C2PA content-provenance standard to automatically label digitally altered content across Facebook, Instagram, and Threads. The plan includes blocking new political ads during the final week before Election Day, requiring advertisers to complete an authorization process with "paid for by" disclaimers, and storing all political ads in a publicly searchable Ad Library for seven years. Meta's Election Operations Center — which has removed more than 200 coordinated disinformation networks since 2017 — will monitor threats in real time. The company will also deploy Community Notes, its crowd-sourced fact-checking system, to allow users to add context to posts by politicians and public figures. Multilingual voter-information prompts will appear at the top of feeds in the weeks before the election. Meta says it has invested over $30 billion in safety infrastructure over the past decade.

Meta Newsroom, Social Media Today, TechBuzz · February-April 2026

Maine's Deepfake Senate Ad Crosses a New Line

Ongoing: Deepfakes in campaigns. The National Republican Senatorial Committee has released what may be the first political ad to deploy AI-generated video and voice of a real candidate to discredit him. The ad shows an AI-synthesized, shirtless Graham Platner — the Democratic frontrunner for Maine's U.S. Senate seat — reading controversial Reddit posts he made a decade ago. While the ad includes a small "dramatic reenactment" disclaimer in the corner, University of Southern Maine political scientist Ron Schmidt warns that "isn't necessarily the message people will take away from it." Platner, who leads Gov. Janet Mills in both polling and fundraising for the Democratic primary, called the ad a sign that "AI is going to be abused and weaponized in political campaigns." Mills, who signed Maine's deepfake disclosure law last month, called the ad "absolutely wrong." Twenty-six states now regulate political deepfakes, but federal legislation remains stalled.

Bangor Daily News, WGME, Maine Public, American Prospect · April 16-29, 2026

The GUARDRAILS Act: Congress Fights Over Who Gets to Regulate AI

On March 20 the White House released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence — a sweeping set of legislative recommendations that would establish a single, nationally unified approach to AI governance by preempting state-level regulation. The framework explicitly recommends against creating any new federal rulemaking body, calling instead for AI to be governed through existing agencies with subject-matter expertise and industry-led standards. The same day, Rep. Don Beyer (D-VA) and four co-sponsors introduced the GUARDRAILS Act, which would repeal the framework and block the administration's effort to impose a moratorium on state AI laws.

The bill now has 34 House cosponsors and a Senate companion led by Sen. Brian Schatz (D-HI). On the other side, Rep. Baumgartner's American AI Leadership and Uniformity Act would write federal preemption into statute. Meanwhile, states keep legislating: 25 new AI laws have been enacted in 2026 so far, with another 27 bills having passed both chambers. The result is a policymaking landscape where federal and state actors are moving at cross-purposes, with no resolution likely before the midterms.

Congress.gov, Beyer.house.gov, Holland & Knight, Ropes & Gray, Cooley, Plural Policy · March-April 2026

Antigua and Barbuda Votes Today in a Razor-Thin General Election

Voters in Antigua and Barbuda head to the polls today to elect members of the House of Representatives across 17 single-member constituencies. The ruling Antigua and Barbuda Labour Party (ABLP) holds a slim majority after the 2023 elections and faces a strong challenge from the United Progressive Party (UPP). Thirty-seven candidates have been nominated — 17 from the ABLP, 16 from the UPP, three independents, and one from the Barbuda People's Movement. The BPM's single seat in Barbuda could prove decisive in a close result. International observers from the OAS and the Commonwealth have deployed 17 experts and observers from eleven countries. The election is being watched as a test of incumbency in the Eastern Caribbean, where cost-of-living pressures and post-pandemic fiscal strain have eroded governing-party advantages across the region.

Newsom Sets California's Own AI Harm Standards for State Contracts

Governor Gavin Newsom has signed an executive order directing California to independently review federal supply-chain risk designations and set its own AI harm standards for state contracts. The order followed a dispute between Anthropic and the Defense Department over contract terms that barred the military from using Anthropic's systems for domestic mass surveillance and fully autonomous weaponry. Under the new rules, California will develop contract standards addressing AI's capacity to generate child sexual abuse material, violate civil liberties, and infringe upon protections against unlawful discrimination, detention, and surveillance — while simultaneously encouraging state employees to accelerate their adoption of vetted generative AI tools. Newsom's office framed the order as a direct rebuke of the Trump administration: "Unlike the Trump administration, California remains committed to ensuring that AI solutions cannot be misused by bad actors." The order positions California as the de facto regulatory counterweight to Washington on AI procurement.

Washington Post, CalMatters, Palo Alto Online · April 2, 2026

Should the Federal Government Preempt State AI Laws?
The Progressive Case

States Must Fill the Federal Void

The GUARDRAILS Act, introduced by Rep. Don Beyer and Sen. Brian Schatz with 34 House cosponsors, would repeal the White House AI framework and block federal preemption of state AI laws. Proponents argue that state legislatures have been the only functioning arena for AI governance — with 25 new laws enacted in 2026 alone — and that a federal "framework" without any new rulemaking body is a framework in name only. They contend that preemption would strip states of their ability to protect residents from bias, surveillance, and discrimination while offering no enforceable federal standard in return.

Rep. Beyer's office, Sen. Schatz press release, Plural Policy · March-April 2026

The Conservative Case

A Patchwork Regime Undermines American Competitiveness

The White House framework and Rep. Baumgartner's American AI Leadership and Uniformity Act argue that a single national standard — governed through existing agencies and industry-led norms — is essential for American competitiveness. Supporters contend that 25 different state laws create an unworkable compliance burden for startups and established firms alike, fragmenting innovation and driving investment overseas. They argue that AI governance should follow a light-touch, pro-innovation model and that Congressional preemption is the fastest path to regulatory clarity.

White House National Policy Framework, Holland & Knight, Ropes & Gray, Biometric Update · March-April 2026

Political Tech Application

Quiller AI — Now Part of Grassroots Analytics

Quiller is an AI copilot built exclusively for Democratic and progressive campaigns. Founded by strategist Mike Nellis (CEO of Authentic Campaigns), the tool generates first-draft fundraising emails, SMS blasts, press releases, op-eds, and digital ad copy — trained on thousands of hand-tagged pieces of progressive content. Campaigns customize Quiller with their own talking points, writing samples, and candidate voice profiles. In early 2026, Grassroots Analytics acquired Quiller to merge its generative AI capabilities with Grassroots's voter-data segmentation infrastructure, creating what the company calls "an all-in-one AI fundraising tool." The combined platform reportedly produces drafts that are "70 percent of the way done," letting campaign staff focus on strategy and personalization rather than first-draft composition. The American Association of Political Consultants reports that a majority of its member firms now use AI regularly, with more than 40 percent saying it will "fundamentally transform" their profession.

For progressive campaigns, the Quiller-Grassroots merger signals a shift from AI as novelty to AI as infrastructure — the question is no longer whether to use it, but how deeply to integrate it into the fundraising stack.

Grassroots Analytics, The Hill, Medium/Quiller, AAPC Survey, Time · 2024-2026
