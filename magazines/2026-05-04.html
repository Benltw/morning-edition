Research Insights of the Day

Disconnect Between the Public Face and the Voting Behavior of Political Representatives

Andersen, C. I. & Coscia, M. · Applied Network Science · 2026

Politicians are more different in parliament than they appear on the campaign trail, according to a network-analysis study published in Springer’s Applied Network Science. The researchers placed Danish legislators in two distinct spaces — one defined by their electoral campaign promises, another by their actual votes in the Folketing — and measured the gap between the two. They found clear left-right divides in both spaces, but also a larger-than-expected disconnect: the aggregate voting behaviour of politicians does not match how they present themselves on the salient issues discussed during the election campaign. The finding holds even after controlling for coalition dynamics and whip pressure, suggesting that the gap is structural rather than incidental.

Practitioner implication: Campaign opposition researchers should build “promise-vs-vote” matrices for incumbents. The structural disconnect identified here is not a bug of one party — it is a feature of parliamentary systems, and it gives challengers a data-driven attack surface that voters find intuitively compelling.

Source: Springer · Applied Network Science · 2026

34%

Trump Approval Craters to Record Low of Either Term

A Pew Research Center survey of 5,103 adults conducted April 20–26 puts President Trump’s job approval at 34 percent — the lowest of both his first and second terms. A parallel Washington Post / ABC / Ipsos poll pegs disapproval at 62 percent, also a personal record. The erosion is no longer confined to Democrats: Pew finds notable slippage among Republicans themselves, with confidence in Trump’s ability to use military force wisely falling from 46 percent last summer to 38 percent today, and confidence in his economic stewardship dropping from 53 percent after his 2024 re-election to 42 percent now. The Iran war, $4.30-a-gallon petrol, and persistent inflation are the dominant drivers. Six months before November, the numbers place the Republican House majority in serious jeopardy — historically, presidents below 40 percent approval lose an average of 37 House seats at the midterms.

Sources: Pew Research Center · Washington Post · Brookings

Ohio and Indiana Primaries: What to Watch Tomorrow

Voters in Ohio and Indiana head to the polls on May 5 in the first major primary day of the 2026 midterm cycle. In Ohio, the marquee race is the Democratic Senate primary, where former senator Sherrod Brown’s comeback bid has turned a once-safe Republican seat into a toss-up. The gubernatorial primaries on both the Republican and Democratic tickets are fiercely contested, with 549 local ballot issues — most of them tax levies — also on the ballot. Statewide races for attorney general, secretary of state, auditor, and treasurer round out what the Ohio Capital Journal calls the most consequential primary in a decade. Early voting closed this weekend; polls open Tuesday at 6:30 a.m. and close at 7:30 p.m. The results will offer the first hard data on whether Trump’s cratering approval is translating into down-ballot Republican weakness in a critical swing state.

Sources: Ohio Capital Journal · NPR

Political Campaigning · Ongoing: Iran War

Iran Blockade Costs Tehran $4.8 Billion as Peace Talks Stall

The Pentagon estimates that the U.S. naval blockade in the Gulf of Oman has denied Iran nearly $4.8 billion in oil revenue since it began on April 13. More than 40 vessels carrying oil and other contraband have been redirected, and 31 tankers laden with 53 million barrels of Iranian crude are now “stuck in the Gulf,” according to Defence Department officials. Tehran may be running out of storage capacity, adding urgency to what Washington calls its most significant leverage tool in the conflict. Yet peace talks continue to stop and start without resolution, and Trump told reporters last week he was “not satisfied” with Iran’s latest proposal. For midterm strategists, the blockade is a double-edged sword: it demonstrates military resolve but keeps crude near $105 a barrel, feeding the petrol-price anger that is dragging Republican numbers down nationwide.

Sources: Axios · ChinaPulse · Newsmax

28 States Now Regulate Deepfake Political Ads — Courts Push Back

As of January 2026, twenty-eight states have enacted laws specifically addressing deepfakes in political communications, up from just three states in 2023. Most focus on disclosure requirements rather than outright bans: campaigns must label AI-generated content with clear disclaimers, though the specifics of what that statement must say vary wildly by jurisdiction. More than half of these laws apply only within a defined window before an election — typically 60 or 90 days — and include exceptions for content carrying a disclosure statement. Minnesota went furthest, making it illegal to use AI to portray a candidate doing something they did not actually do unless the candidate consents. But the courts are pushing back. A federal judge struck down California’s sweeping law as overbroad, holding it could be more narrowly tailored to target only false speech that causes “legally cognizable harms like voter interference, coercion, or intimidation.” X (formerly Twitter) has challenged Minnesota’s law, and early rulings suggest judicial scepticism toward broad deepfake prohibitions. The result is a regulatory patchwork that campaign lawyers describe as “a compliance nightmare” for national ad buys. Meanwhile, deepfake attack ads proliferate: the Detroit News documented multiple AI-generated videos deployed in 2026 midterm campaigns, and CNN reported on a Republican deepfake of Texas Democrat James Talarico that went viral before any disclosure was added.

Sources: MultiState · Public Citizen · Detroit News · CNN

Colombia’s Left Leads Polls Four Weeks Before Presidential Vote

With Colombia’s May 31 presidential election four weeks away, left-wing senator Iván Cepeda has consolidated his lead at 44.3 percent in the latest Invamer poll, more than doubling the support of his nearest rival, far-right lawyer Abelardo de la Espriella at 21.5 percent. Centre-right senator Paloma Valencia sits third at 19.8 percent. Incumbent President Gustavo Petro is constitutionally barred from a consecutive second term, and Cepeda — who won the Historic Pact primary in October 2025 — is running as his ideological successor. The race is unfolding against a backdrop of rising political violence, economic difficulty, and tense relations with the United States after the Trump administration imposed trade tariffs that Bogotá has vowed to contest at the WTO. For campaign professionals, Colombia is a test case for whether Latin America’s left-populist wave can survive the cost-of-living backlash that has unseated incumbents elsewhere in the region.

Sources: AS/COA · The City Paper Bogotá · Al Jazeera

AI in Politics

Trump’s AI Executive Order Threatens to Sue States Over Regulation

President Trump’s December executive order, “Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence,” has created a new front in the AI-politics war: a Department of Justice AI Litigation Task Force empowered to sue states whose AI laws the Attorney General deems unconstitutional. Since January 10, the task force has been preparing challenges grounded in the Dormant Commerce Clause, arguing that state-level AI regulations place an undue burden on interstate commerce. The order goes further: it directs the Secretary of Commerce to make states with “onerous AI laws” ineligible for broadband deployment funding under the BEAD programme, and instructs the FTC to classify state-mandated bias mitigation as a per se deceptive trade practice. Red states have largely welcomed the pre-emption; blue states including California, New York, and Illinois have vowed to fight. NPR and legal scholars note the executive order is “almost certain to be challenged in court” — the president cannot restrict state regulation without an act of Congress. For campaign operatives deploying AI tools across state lines, the resulting uncertainty is itself the problem.

Sources: NPR · Paul Hastings · Governing

Rise of AI Berlin Opens Tuesday — A Decade of European AI Sovereignty

The tenth edition of the Rise of AI Conference convenes in Berlin on May 6, marking a decade of Europe’s flagship AI policy summit. Roughly 300 hand-picked decision-makers from business, government, research, and investment will gather across two stages — Meta Stage and Applied AI Stage — for a programme centred on Europe’s technological sovereignty. Keynote speakers include Dr. Volker Wissing, Germany’s Federal Minister for Digital, Transport, and Justice, and Professor Jürgen Schmidhuber, the LSTM pioneer. Core tracks cover trusted AI infrastructure, the EU AI Act’s real-world implementation, sustainable AI, and Europe’s competitive positioning against U.S. and Chinese ecosystems. The same week, the European AI and Cloud Summit in Cologne will draw 3,000+ attendees to navigate EU AI Act compliance, and the Data Innovation Summit in Stockholm expands to three days with 3,500 participants. For campaign professionals, these events shape the regulatory pipeline that will govern AI use in European elections for years to come.

Social Media Trends

TikTok’s Post-Divestiture Privacy Overhaul Sparks User Backlash

Four months after TikTok’s U.S. operations were divested into the American-controlled TikTok USDS on January 22, the platform’s new terms of service are provoking a backlash that could reshape political advertising on the app. The biggest change: geolocation is now explicitly classified as “sensitive data” in the 2026 privacy policy, a shift CBS News reports has alarmed both users and campaign data teams. TikTok has also introduced mandatory labelling for AI-generated content and prohibited “misleading AI content that could spread misinformation” entirely — a stricter regime than any U.S. social platform has attempted. Political advertisers must now use built-in disclosure tools for any branded content, and the UK’s Online Safety Bill has imposed additional age-verification and content-moderation requirements. For midterm campaigns that had been banking on TikTok as a low-cost youth-outreach channel, the new rules add friction at precisely the moment when the platform’s 170 million U.S. users are most politically engaged.

Sources: CBS News · Center for American Progress · Dark Room Agency

AI-Driven Campaign Fundraising Hits Half a Billion This Cycle

More than $500 million has been raised through AI-driven methods in the 2026 midterm cycle, according to reporting by OpenTools and Campaigns & Elections. The figure captures a range of techniques: machine-learning-optimised donation pages that personalise ask amounts in real time, AI-generated direct-mail copy that A/B tests thousands of variants overnight, predictive models that identify lapsed donors most likely to reactivate, and chatbot-assisted small-dollar solicitation. Anthropic announced a $20 million donation to Public First Action, a new super PAC supporting candidates who favour AI guardrails. On the Republican side, Campaign Nucleus has integrated the EyesOver sentiment-analysis engine, which scans millions of social media posts daily to feed real-time public-opinion data directly into campaign execution workflows. The AI fundraising arms race is widening the gap between well-resourced campaigns that can afford these tools and smaller races that cannot — a structural inequality that campaign-finance reformers warn could entrench incumbency advantages for a generation.

Sources: OpenTools · Campaign Nucleus · Axios

Should the Federal Government Pre-empt State AI Laws?

Yes — A National Framework Prevents a Patchwork

Proponents argue that a unified federal approach to AI regulation is essential for innovation. Trump’s executive order contends that state-by-state rules create an “undue burden on interstate commerce” and that conflicting compliance requirements stifle startups and raise costs for consumers. The Chamber of Commerce and major tech trade associations have backed federal pre-emption, arguing that a single set of rules would give companies the certainty they need to invest in AI development on U.S. soil. Supporters also note that the Dormant Commerce Clause already limits state authority over inherently national markets.

Sources: Paul Hastings · Alston & Bird

No — States Are the Laboratories of Democracy

Critics counter that federal pre-emption without a corresponding federal AI law leaves a regulatory vacuum. NPR reports that legal scholars say the president “cannot restrict state regulation in this way without Congress passing a law.” California, New York, and Illinois have vowed to defend their statutes. The ACLU and Public Citizen argue that state laws are filling a gap left by federal inaction and that threatening states with loss of broadband funding amounts to coercion. They point to the 28 state deepfake laws as proof that bottom-up regulation can respond faster to emerging threats than Congress can.

Sources: NPR · Governing

Political Tech Application

Aaru

Aaru simulates entire voter populations using AI agents to predict election outcomes. Founded by Cam Fink and Ned Koh, the seven-person startup uses census data to replicate voter districts, creating thousands of AI agents programmed to think like the voters they model. Its Dynamo product allows agents to continuously receive and process media information, simulating how real voters update their views over time.

In the 2024 presidential election, Aaru’s 5,000-agent poll predicted results within 371 votes of the actual outcome. Polls that traditionally cost tens of thousands of dollars and take weeks can be conducted in 30 seconds to 1.5 minutes at less than one-tenth the cost. The company closed a $50 million Series A at a $1 billion valuation in late 2025. Republican campaigns are the earliest adopters, using Aaru to test messaging and model turnout scenarios for the 2026 midterms.

> takeaway: Aaru collapses the cost and time of polling by orders of magnitude. If its accuracy holds through a midterm cycle, traditional survey firms face an existential challenge — and campaigns gain a real-time feedback loop that was previously impossible.

Sources: Semafor · Ordinary Times · Aaru
