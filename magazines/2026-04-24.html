01 — Research Insights of the Day

Overestimating the Social Costs of Political Belief Change

Trevor Spelman, Nour Kteily, Eli Finkel · Kellogg School of Management, Northwestern University

Partisans consistently overestimate how harshly their peers will judge them for changing their minds on polarizing issues. Across five main studies and five supplemental experiments (N = 4,535), Spelman and colleagues found that people predicted their political dissent would be viewed as a massive betrayal of group loyalty — yet the actual reactors did not view belief change as a severe indicator of disloyalty. The weighted effect size (d = .87) was large and robust across surveys, behavioral outcomes, live participant interactions, and coded open-ended responses. The inflated expectations were associated with a greater likelihood of self-censoring dissenting views, depriving public discourse of the very ideological diversity that might reduce polarization.

Practitioner Implication

Campaign strategists who rely on base-mobilization messaging may be reinforcing a perception gap that keeps persuadable partisans silent — signaling permission to evolve could unlock a larger movable middle than conventional polling suggests.

Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 2026 · Northwestern IPR Working Paper WP-24-25

03 — Global Politics

EU Unlocks $106 Billion for Ukraine After Hungary Lifts Its Veto

The European Union on Thursday finalized a 90-billion-euro ($106 billion) loan package for Ukraine — the largest single financial commitment in the bloc’s history — after Hungary dropped its months-long veto. The breakthrough came when oil deliveries resumed through a key pipeline to Hungary and Slovakia, resolving a dispute that had infuriated the other 24 member states. Of the total, 45 billion euros will be disbursed in 2026 alone: 8.35 billion through macro-financial assistance, another 8.35 billion via the Ukraine Facility, and 28.3 billion to support Ukraine’s defence-industrial base. Hungarian PM Viktor Orban had reneged on a December deal, blocking the package over a pipeline dispute and drawing sharp diplomatic criticism. The loans, which require no repayment until 2033, are expected to begin flowing in the coming weeks.

European Council · ABC News · European Parliament · April 23–24, 2026

04 — U.S. Politics

Senate Rams Through $70B Immigration Budget in Predawn Vote-a-Rama

In a 50–48 party-line vote cast in the predawn hours of Wednesday, the Senate adopted a GOP budget resolution authorizing up to $70 billion in new immigration enforcement spending through the end of President Trump’s term. The measure, which bypasses the need for Democratic votes through the reconciliation process, funds ICE and parts of Customs and Border Protection for 3.5 years. The resolution now moves to the House, which must pass its own version before committees can draft the actual legislation. Trump has set a June 1 deadline. The move comes after most of DHS has been unfunded for over nine weeks, with Democrats insisting that ICE and Border Patrol be subject to warrant requirements before entering private homes — a demand that intensified after two U.S. citizens were fatally shot by immigration agents in Minneapolis.

NPR · CNN · NBC News · CNBC · April 23, 2026

05 — Geopolitics & Campaigns

Trump Orders Navy to “Shoot and Kill” Iranian Boats Mining the Strait of Hormuz

Ongoing: Iran War & Ceasefire

President Trump posted on Truth Social that the U.S. Navy will “shoot and kill” any Iranian vessel laying mines in the Strait of Hormuz, a dramatic escalation that could jeopardize the fragile ceasefire now in its seventh week. The order comes after Iran’s Revolutionary Guard Navy seized two container ships and reimposed “strict control” over the strait, reversing its declaration a day earlier that the vital shipping lane was fully open. Vessel tracking data shows an almost total standstill — just one ship transited in the last 12 hours. The head of the International Energy Agency called it “the biggest crisis in history,” noting that roughly 20 percent of the world’s oil and gas flowed through Hormuz before the war. Trump denied that midterm considerations are driving his timeline, telling reporters: “Don’t rush me.”

Al Jazeera · CNN · NBC News · CNBC · April 22–24, 2026

Republican Party Cash Reserves for the 2026 Midterms

Democratic candidates are outraising Republicans in nearly every marquee Senate and House contest — but at the national party level, the picture reverses sharply. The RNC, NRCC, NRSC, and allied super PACs Congressional Leadership Fund and Senate Leadership Fund, plus Trump’s MAGA Inc., hold roughly $850 million in combined cash on hand — nearly double what the DNC, DCCC, DSCC, House Majority PAC, and Senate Majority PAC have banked. The contrast poses a strategic puzzle: grassroots energy flows to individual Democratic campaigns, while institutional Republican infrastructure commands a massive financial overhang that can be deployed in the final months before November.

07 — AI in Politics

Connecticut Passes the Most Ambitious State AI Bill Yet

Compiled from CT Mirror, AI Commission, Yankee Institute

Connecticut’s state Senate voted 32–4 on Tuesday to pass Senate Bill 5, a sweeping piece of AI legislation that may become the most comprehensive state-level AI regulatory framework in the country. The bill regulates developers of “frontier” AI models, establishes a state AI “sandbox” for companies to test technologies under regulatory oversight, and introduces new rules around youth social media use and AI chatbot safety. AI chatbot companies would be required to make “reasonable efforts” to detect suicidal ideation or self-harm language and respond with resources for help. Employers using AI for hiring and employment decisions must notify affected workers and ensure the tools do not discriminate. The bill also expands AI workforce development, promoting the Connecticut AI Academy among baby bond recipients and unemployed workers. The measure now moves to the House, which last year declined to take up a similar proposal. The bill arrives as 19 new AI laws have been signed across the country in just six weeks, a pace that underscores the growing urgency of state-level action amid a federal vacuum. Governor Spencer Cox of Utah alone has signed nine AI bills in 2026, including eight in the past two weeks. Connecticut’s sandbox provision is notable: it creates a regulatory safe harbor for AI experimentation that could attract startups while maintaining oversight — a model other states may replicate.

CT Mirror · AI Commission · Yankee Institute · Plural Policy · April 21–22, 2026

08 — AI in Politics

USC Study: AI Agents Can Run Propaganda Campaigns Without Human Direction

A team at USC’s Information Sciences Institute has demonstrated that even simple AI agents can autonomously coordinate, amplify each other, and push shared narratives online — without any human controlling the operation after the initial goal is set. The study, accepted at The Web Conference 2026, placed 50 AI agents in a simulated social-media environment modeled on X: 10 designated as influence operators, 40 as ordinary users. The agents wrote their own posts, learned what worked, copied teammates’ successful approaches, and echoed each other’s content — producing what the researchers call “emergent coordinated behaviors” indistinguishable from a human-directed influence operation. The threat extends beyond elections to public health, immigration, and economic policy, with adversarial attacks potentially sowing division and eroding trust in institutions at a pace and scale no human team could match.

USC Viterbi School of Engineering · The Web Conference 2026 · March 2026

19 New AI Laws in Six Weeks: The State-Level Flood Reshaping U.S. Tech Policy

Ongoing: AI Regulation

Since mid-March 2026, the number of AI laws signed into law across the United States has surged from 6 to 25, with another 27 bills having passed both legislative chambers and awaiting gubernatorial signatures. Utah’s Governor Spencer Cox has been the most prolific signer, putting his name to nine AI bills this year alone — including eight in the past two weeks. Nebraska signed the Conversational AI Safety Act. Maine prohibited unlicensed AI therapy. Idaho created a framework for generative AI in K-12 education. The wave comes as the White House’s National Policy Framework recommends federal preemption of state AI laws that “impose undue burdens” — setting up a collision between states racing to regulate and a federal government that wants to slow them down.

10 — Political Campaigning

Talarico Shatters Senate Fundraising Records with a $27 Million Quarter

Texas state Rep. James Talarico raised $27 million in the first three months of 2026 — the largest sum ever recorded by a Senate candidate in any state during a first quarter of an election year. His campaign reports that 97 percent of contributions were $100 or less, drawn from more than 540,000 individual donors across 246 of Texas’s 254 counties. The haul brings his total since entering the race last September to over $40 million, giving him a cash-on-hand edge of $9.9 million over incumbent John Cornyn’s $8.2 million and far ahead of GOP primary challenger Ken Paxton’s $2.6 million. Talarico more than doubled the first-quarter totals of recent high-profile Democratic Senate candidates including former Ohio Sen. Sherrod Brown and former North Carolina Gov. Roy Cooper.

Texas Tribune · Axios Austin · CNN · April 15, 2026

11 — Political Technology

Team Mirai: The Japanese Party Using AI to Strengthen Democracy, Not Undermine It

While most of the world debates how to defend democracy from AI, Japan’s Team Mirai is building tools to make it work better. Founded by AI engineer Takahiro Anno, the party — whose name translates to “Team Future” — won 11 seats in the 2026 general election on a platform of “digital democracy.” Its open-source tool Mirai Marumie visualizes political funding flows in near-real-time using Sankey diagrams. Another tool, Mirai Diet, uses AI to translate complex legislative language into plain Japanese, letting citizens follow parliamentary deliberations without a law degree. Anno describes the party as “basic infrastructure for Japanese democracy that serves the broader polity rather than one faction.” Harvard’s Berkman Klein Center and security researcher Bruce Schneier have both highlighted Team Mirai as a rare case of tech-augmented participatory politics that prioritizes inclusion over disruption.

12 — Political Psychology

Even Intentionally-Used AI Erodes Democratic Institutions, Scholars Warn

In a new essay from Harvard’s Berkman Klein Center, legal scholars Woodrow Hartzog and Jessica Silbey argue that the democratic threat from AI does not come only from deepfakes and disinformation — it comes from the routine, intentional deployment of AI in governance and political operations. Their argument: AI systems that automate decision-making in areas like criminal justice, welfare, and voter eligibility quietly redistribute power from accountable human officials to opaque algorithmic systems, weakening the deliberative fabric of democratic institutions. Separately, Berkman Klein fellows Nathan Sanders and Bruce Schneier have urged political leaders to take firmer stances on AI governance in the wake of the Trump administration’s executive order limiting states’ ability to regulate AI — a position they argue creates a dangerous regulatory void at a moment when the technology is advancing faster than oversight can keep pace.

Berkman Klein Center, Harvard University · March 2026

The Case for Government Guardrails

A growing number of Democrats are making AI regulation a centerpiece of their 2026 campaigns, arguing that self-regulation has failed and that federal standards are needed to prevent AI-driven disinformation, discriminatory targeting, and job displacement. Anthropic announced a $20 million donation to Public First Action to support candidates who favor AI guardrails. Dario Amodei projected that AI could disrupt up to 50 percent of entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years, lending urgency to calls for worker protections and transparency mandates.

Axios · Quartz · February–April 2026

The White House’s National Policy Framework recommends against creating any new federal rulemaking body, calling instead for AI to be governed through existing regulators and industry-led standards. Yet a new conservative coalition, the Alliance for a Better Future, is pushing for “common-sense” guardrails — especially around child safety and AI chatbot interactions — while maintaining a “pro-innovation” stance. Leading the Future, a pro-AI super PAC backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Andreessen Horowitz, has pledged to spend over $100 million in 2026 federal races to support candidates who oppose heavy-handed regulation.

14 — Political Tech Application

$ quiller 2.0

What it does: Quiller is an AI copilot for political fundraising that generates first-draft emails, SMS messages, press releases, op-eds, and digital ad copy in under 20 seconds. The tool is trained exclusively on progressive and Democratic campaign data, producing copy that is roughly “70 percent of the way done” before a human editor adds nuance, candidate-specific references, and current events.

Who is using it: Quiller was recently acquired by D.C.-based data firm Grassroots Analytics, which is integrating the tool into its voter segmentation infrastructure to create an all-in-one AI fundraising platform. Since 2023, Quiller has been used by progressive campaigns, nonprofits, and movement builders across federal and state races.

Reported outcomes: Grassroots Analytics reports that the integration allows campaigns to pair AI-generated messaging with granular donor data segmentation — targeting the right ask to the right voter at the right time. The combined platform is positioned to lower barriers to grassroots fundraising in the 2026 cycle.

> takeaway: The Quiller acquisition signals that AI campaign tools are consolidating fast. The race is no longer about whether campaigns use AI for fundraising — it is about who owns the data pipeline underneath.

Grassroots Analytics · Campaigns & Elections · The Hill · April 2026
