01 — Research Insights of the Day

Testing Theories of Political Persuasion Using AI

Hackenburg, Tappin, Teal, Hewitt, Barkley, Willer et al. · PNAS · May 2025

Decades of political communication theory have assumed that personalization is the key to persuasion — that tailored messages, crafted to match an individual’s values, identity, and psychological profile, should outperform generic appeals. A landmark study in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, leveraging generative AI to test these assumptions at unprecedented scale, finds the opposite. Researchers used large language models to generate political messages ranging from simple one-line arguments to deeply personalized multi-turn conversations calibrated to each participant’s moral foundations, personality traits, and demographic profile. The result: highly customized messages performed no better than a single basic argument. A brief, fact-based claim moved opinions just as effectively as an elaborate, psychologically targeted dialogue. The finding challenges the theoretical foundations of micro-targeting, which has driven billions of dollars in campaign spending since 2012. PsyPost reported in March 2026 that the study “upends” major theories of political persuasion by showing that the marginal return on personalization is effectively zero once a competent factual argument is on the table.

Practitioner Implication

The expensive infrastructure of psychographic micro-targeting may be solving a problem that does not exist. Campaigns investing in AI-powered personalization should reckon with the possibility that a well-chosen factual argument, delivered at scale, is equally persuasive — and far cheaper to produce.

PNAS, May 2025 · PsyPost, March 2026 · Stanford Social Neuroscience Lab

03 — Global Elections

Colombia’s Three-Way Presidential Race Enters Its Final Five Weeks

Colombia votes on May 31 in a presidential election that has crystallized into a three-way contest with no clear frontrunner. On the left, Senator Iván Cepeda of the governing Historic Pact carries President Gustavo Petro’s banner after winning the coalition’s primary in October 2025. Cepeda leads in most polls and appears to benefit from Petro’s rising approval, now at 42 percent. On the center-right, Democratic Center Senator Paloma Valencia — a protégée of former President Álvaro Uribe — has doubled her polling numbers since January, making the race tighter than anyone predicted. And from outside the traditional party system, lawyer Abelardo de la Espriella has surged as what Americas Quarterly calls “Colombia’s Bukele” — a hard-right outsider running on law-and-order populism and smart use of digital campaigning. If no candidate clears 50 percent on May 31, a runoff follows on June 21, with inauguration on July 28. AS/COA reports the election will test whether Latin America’s recent rightward shift continues or whether Petro’s left-populist project can survive its founder’s departure. The outcome will shape Colombia’s peace process, its relationship with Venezuela, and its stance on drug policy for the next four years.

04 — Political Campaigning

$10.8B
Projected Political Ad Spending for the 2026 Midterms

The 2026 midterm elections are on track to become the most expensive midterm cycle in American history. AdImpact’s Political Projections Report forecasts $10.8 billion in total political advertising, a 20-percent-plus increase over the $8.9 billion spent in the 2022 cycle — and within striking distance of the $11.2 billion spent during the 2024 presidential election. Senate spending alone is projected at $2.8 billion, breaking the record set during the 2024 cycle. House spending will cross the $2 billion mark for the first time. The fastest-growing channel is connected television, projected to reach $2.5 billion over the cycle and gaining market share even as linear TV, radio, and digital display plateau. OpenSecrets reports that the spending surge is driven by three forces: the narrowest congressional margins in modern history, the explosion of AI-driven ad production that lowers the cost of generating creative content, and a Supreme Court landscape that has all but eliminated restraints on independent expenditure groups. Assembly’s Election Outlook finds that the sheer volume of advertising may be approaching a saturation point in key swing districts, where voters in competitive House races could see more than 10,000 political ads between now and November.

AdImpact · OpenSecrets · CNBC · Axios · Campaigns & Elections · Assembly · January–April 2026

05 — GovTech

Utah’s “Pro-Human AI” Governor Defies the White House

Ongoing: AI Regulation

Republican Governor Spencer Cox of Utah has emerged as the most prominent state-level antagonist to the Trump administration’s AI agenda — from within the president’s own party. After the White House sent a memo calling a Utah child-safety AI bill “an unfixable bill that goes against the Administration’s AI agenda,” Cox pushed back publicly, telling KUER that “regulation should be left to the states.” He then signed all nine AI-related bills that Utah’s legislature passed during one of the nation’s shortest sessions, covering AI in schools, deepfake protections, health insurance AI use, and age verification for harmful digital content. StateScoop reports Cox has announced a “pro-human AI” plan that explicitly rejects the Silicon Valley orthodoxy of minimal regulation while also refusing the White House’s preferred model of broad federal preemption. Nextgov/FCW notes the confrontation has national implications: if a Republican governor in one of the country’s most conservative states insists on regulating AI over White House objections, the administration’s preemption strategy faces a credibility problem within its own coalition. Cox’s stance has drawn quiet support from governors in both parties who view state-level AI regulation as a core governance prerogative.

KUER · StateScoop · Nextgov/FCW · Mintz · Transparency Coalition · February–April 2026

06 — Social Media Trends

The Influencer Primary: Campaigns Chase Creator Credibility

A fifth of American adults now get their news regularly from political influencers, and campaigns for the 2026 midterms are restructuring their outreach strategies around that reality. City & State Pennsylvania reports that Governor Josh Shapiro’s reelection campaign reserved a dedicated credentialed section for content creators at his campaign announcement event — placing influencers on equal footing with traditional press. The move reflects a broader shift: new firms specializing in creator outreach and strategy have emerged to replicate the influencer partnerships that proved effective in 2025 state and local races. For midterm candidates who struggle to break through in a $10.8 billion ad environment, micro and nano-influencers — those with fewer than 100,000 followers — offer targeted reach at a fraction of the cost of television. But the strategy carries risks. The Center for Democracy and Technology warns that political influencers fall through the cracks of platform advertising policies: there are no consistent disclosure requirements, no transparency about payments, and no reliable enforcement when rules are broken. The University at Buffalo’s misinformation research lab predicts that the blurred line between organic creator content and paid political messaging will be one of the defining information-integrity challenges of the 2026 cycle.

City & State PA · CDT · University at Buffalo · Anchor Change · OpenSecrets · January–April 2026

07 — Global Elections

Brazil’s Coin-Flip Election: Lula vs. the Bolsonaro Name

Compiled from AS/COA, ISPI, Wikipedia, Americas Quarterly

Brazil goes to the polls on October 4 in a general election that polls suggest is genuinely too close to call. President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, 80, is seeking an unprecedented fourth term. His campaign can point to slowing inflation, rising employment, and a popular income tax exemption for workers earning less than $930 a month. But Lula faces a headwind that has defined recent Brazilian politics: security. Thirty-eight percent of voters name violent crime as their top concern, a vulnerability the opposition intends to exploit relentlessly. On the right, former President Jair Bolsonaro is ineligible to run after his indictment and imprisonment for plotting a coup. But the Bolsonaro name endures: Senator Flávio Bolsonaro has secured the Liberal Party’s nomination with his father’s endorsement, and the two are polling neck and neck. Two additional right-wing candidates complicate the picture. Former Minas Gerais Governor Romeu Zema of the NOVO party and former Goiás Governor Ronaldo Caiado of the center-right Social Democratic Party are both running, potentially splitting the anti-Lula vote in the first round. AS/COA’s poll tracker shows the race as a statistical tie in head-to-head matchups, with the outcome likely hinging on whether the right consolidates behind a single challenger before October. ISPI warns that the election will be a referendum not just on Lula’s governance but on the resilience of Brazilian democracy itself, coming just three years after the January 8, 2023, insurrection attempt. The campaign is expected to be the most digitally intensive in Brazilian history, with both sides deploying AI-generated content and influencer networks at scale.

AS/COA · ISPI · Americas Quarterly · Colombia One · April 2026

08 — AI in Politics

Nineteen AI Laws in Fourteen Days: The State Legislature Sprint

Ongoing: AI Regulation

American state legislatures are passing AI laws at a pace that has no precedent in technology regulation. Plural Policy reports that 25 AI-specific laws have been enacted in 2026 as of mid-April, with an extraordinary 19 of those signed in a single two-week window. Governor Cox of Utah alone signed nine AI bills. The Cooley law firm’s April 24 tracker documents the breadth of the legislative sprint: bills cover AI in schools, deepfake protections, health insurance automation, age verification, workforce notification requirements, and algorithmic transparency in government services. New York’s RAISE Act, signed in December 2025 and amended on March 27, 2026, is shifting toward a transparency and reporting framework rather than outright prohibitions. The Eversheds Sutherland Global AI Regulatory Update for April 2026 places the American state-level activity in a global context: while the EU focuses on enforcing the AI Act’s approaching deadlines, the United States is producing regulation from the ground up, state by state, without a coordinating federal framework. Georgetown’s CSET observes in its April 2026 mapping that the state-level surge is creating exactly the patchwork of rules that the White House’s preemption strategy was designed to prevent — and that Congress has so far declined to enact.

09 — Political Technology

Connected TV: The Midterms’ $2.5 Billion Battleground

Connected television has quietly become the fastest-growing channel in political advertising, and the 2026 midterms are accelerating its dominance. AdImpact projects $2.5 billion in CTV political ad spending over the cycle, gaining market share even as linear television, radio, and traditional digital display plateau. The shift is structural, not cyclical: CTV allows campaigns to combine the visual impact of television with the targeting precision of digital — serving different ads to different households watching the same program. Basis Technologies reports that the medium is particularly attractive for down-ballot races, where candidates can target persuadable voters in specific precincts without paying for the broader reach of broadcast. The result is a transformation of the political ad market: campaigns that once relied on a handful of 30-second broadcast spots now produce dozens of CTV-specific creatives, many generated with AI tools that lower production costs dramatically. AdWave identifies five trends driving adoption: programmatic buying that lets campaigns adjust spend in real time, frequency capping to avoid voter fatigue, household-level audience matching, cross-device attribution, and the ability to run interactive ad formats that prompt viewers to take immediate action. Inside Radio reports that midterm political ad spending has already topped $2 billion as of April, with CTV accounting for a growing share of every incremental dollar.

AdImpact · Basis Technologies · AdWave · Inside Radio · Campaigns & Elections · 2025–2026

10 — Global Elections

Post-Orbán Hungary: What Magyar’s Victory Means for Europe

Ongoing: EU / Hungary

Two weeks after Péter Magyar’s Tisza party won a two-thirds supermajority with 53.6 percent of the vote, ending Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule, the implications for Europe are coming into sharper focus. PIIE’s analysis argues that the ouster of Orbán removes the EU’s most reliable internal veto on sanctions, foreign policy coordination, and rule-of-law enforcement — a structural shift that could reshape the bloc’s decision-making on everything from Ukraine to enlargement. Magyar has pledged to unlock billions in frozen EU funds that Brussels withheld over democratic backsliding, but first he must navigate a governing apparatus that Fidesz built over a decade: a packed constitutional court, a captured state media empire, and an entrenched bureaucracy loyal to the old regime. Al Jazeera profiles Magyar as a former Fidesz insider who broke with the party in 2024 over corruption, walked hundreds of kilometers across rural Hungary to build grassroots support, and mobilized younger voters through social media at a scale the opposition had never managed. The record 77-percent turnout reflected a country that had decided the election was existential. CNN notes that Magyar’s first fortnight has been consumed by the mechanics of democratic restoration: his government must decide whether to dismantle Orbán’s media conglomerate, reform the judiciary, and rewrite the electoral laws that gave Fidesz a structural advantage — all while managing the expectations of a coalition that spans center-left to center-right.

PIIE · Al Jazeera · CNN · CBS News · Euronews · April 12–28, 2026

The European AI & Cloud Summit convenes in Cologne from May 5–7, expecting over 3,000 attendees focused on enterprise AI and Microsoft Azure. 12 — Political Campaigning

Tech Giants Spend $50 Million Lobbying Congress as AI Bills Pile Up

As state legislatures sprint to pass AI laws and Congress debates whether to preempt them, the seven largest tech and AI companies spent a combined $50 million on federal lobbying in just the first three quarters of 2025 — averaging nearly $400,000 for every day Congress was in session. CalMatters reports that in California alone, Meta, Google, and other AI companies have poured resources into shaping the state’s approach to AI regulation, elections, and content moderation. The lobbying surge coincides with the White House’s National Policy Framework, which recommends federal preemption of state AI laws that “impose undue burdens” — a position that aligns closely with industry preferences. CNN reports that OpenAI has publicly denied spending on super PACs but has not denied lobbying activity. Meanwhile, the Leading the Future super PAC, backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, is spending to elect pro-AI candidates. On the other side, former Representatives Chris Stewart and Brad Carson have created two pro-regulation super PACs to support candidates who favor stricter AI oversight. Built In reports that the lobbying battle is no longer about whether AI will be regulated but about who writes the rules: the federal government (with industry at the table), the states (with consumer advocates leading), or some combination that neither side yet controls.

CalMatters · CNN · CNBC · Built In · OpenSecrets · 2025–2026

13 — Split Graph

Should the Federal Government Preempt State AI Laws?

The Progressive Case: States Must Fill the Federal Void

Democrats in Congress introduced the GUARDRAILS Act on March 20, 2026, which would repeal the Trump administration’s executive order establishing a national AI policy framework and block efforts to impose a moratorium on state-level AI regulation. Rep. Don Beyer framed the bill as a defense of federalism: “States have been the laboratories of democracy on AI while Congress has done nothing.” Progressive advocates point to Utah, New York, Louisiana, and a dozen other states that have enacted substantive AI protections in 2026 alone. The National Governors Association notes that states retain traditional police powers to protect children, prevent fraud, and safeguard consumers. Without federal legislation — which Congress has repeatedly declined to pass — state laws are the only binding rules governing AI in American life.

Rep. Beyer · GUARDRAILS Act · NGA · Governing · March–April 2026

The Conservative Case: A Patchwork Kills Innovation

The White House’s National Policy Framework, released March 20, 2026, recommends that Congress preempt state AI laws that “impose undue burdens,” arguing that a patchwork of 50 different regulatory regimes will stifle innovation and drive AI development offshore. The Framework calls for AI to be governed through existing federal agencies with subject-matter expertise, not through new bureaucracies. Holland & Knight observes that the administration would prohibit states from regulating AI model development, penalizing developers for third-party misuse, or burdening AI activities that would be lawful if performed without AI. Ropes & Gray notes that the preemption framework preserves state authority over zoning, enforcement of general laws, and regulation of state governments’ own AI use. Industry groups argue that national standards are essential for American competitiveness in a global AI race where China and the EU are consolidating their own regulatory regimes.

White House Framework · Holland & Knight · Ropes & Gray · Alvarez & Marsal · March 2026

14 — Political Tech Application

$ nrsc-deepfake --candidate=talarico --duration=60s --format=attack-ad

What it is: The National Republican Senatorial Committee has built what CNN calls the first systematic deepfake ad production pipeline in American campaign history. In March 2026, the NRSC published an AI-generated video of James Talarico, the Democratic Senate nominee in Texas, in which a synthetic version of the candidate appears to speak directly into the camera for more than a minute, reading aloud excerpts from his own old tweets on divisive issues. The video is not labeled as AI-generated.

Who is deploying it: The NRSC has produced multiple deepfake attack ads across competitive Senate races. In the Texas Republican primary, both the Paxton and Cornyn campaigns used AI-generated content: a Paxton ad showed a fake “Cornyn” dancing with a Democratic congresswoman, while a Cornyn ad depicted opponent Wesley Hunt in fabricated scenes. Reuters documented that Republicans are utilizing the technology more frequently than Democrats in the 2026 cycle.

What makes it significant: The Talarico video is the longest and most realistic deepfake of a named political candidate deployed in an official campaign ad to date. It demonstrates that AI-generated candidate impersonation has moved from proof-of-concept to standard opposition-research output. The ad ran without any disclosure that it was synthetically generated, exploiting the absence of federal AI labeling requirements — the very gap that the Protecting Consumers from Deceptive AI Act, introduced four days later, was designed to close.

> takeaway: Deepfake attack ads are no longer a hypothetical threat to elections — they are a production line. The NRSC’s pipeline has normalized synthetic candidate impersonation as a campaign tactic, and the regulatory response remains months behind the technology.

CNN · Reuters · Fakeout · Japan Times · Honolulu Star-Advertiser · March 2026
