Research Insights of the Day

A New Measure of Affective Polarization

James Druckman · S. Iyengar · E. Klar · L. Mason · M. Westwood · American Political Science Review, 2026

Affective polarization — the visceral dislike partisans feel for the opposing side — has been one of the most cited concepts in political science for a decade. Yet its measurement has relied on simple feeling thermometers that collapse a multifaceted phenomenon into a single number. A team led by James Druckman offers a corrective: a nine-item instrument that disaggregates affective polarization into three subdimensions — othering (perceiving the out-party as fundamentally alien), aversion (emotional hostility), and moralization (viewing partisan opponents as morally deficient). Across validation studies, the three-part measure correlates more reliably with support for anti-democratic elite actions and tolerance for political violence than any single-item alternative. The finding that moralization is the subdimension most strongly linked to support for political violence suggests that campaign rhetoric framing opponents as evil — rather than merely wrong — carries measurable democratic risk.

Practitioner Implication

Campaigns that lean on moral condemnation of the opposing party may activate the precise psychological mechanism most dangerous to democratic stability; strategists should consider the downstream cost of moralization frames.

American Political Science Review, 2026 · Cambridge University Press

Political Campaigning

DeSantis Unveils Florida Map That Could Hand the GOP Four More Seats

Florida Governor Ron DeSantis released a proposed congressional map on Sunday that would redraw the state’s 28 House districts to create 24 Republican-leaning seats and just four Democratic seats — up from the current 20–7 split. The map targets four incumbent Democrats: Kathy Castor of Tampa, Darren Soto of Orlando, Lois Frankel and Debbie Wasserman Schultz of South Florida. A special legislative session convened Tuesday to consider the maps, which are widely expected to pass the GOP-dominated legislature. Florida Democratic Party chair Nikki Fried called the proposal “unconstitutional gerrymandering.” Ongoing: redistricting has been a recurring storyline this cycle, with multiple states redrawing lines to maximize partisan advantage ahead of the 2026 midterms.

NPR, The Washington Post, CNN · April 27, 2026

Political Campaigning

Supreme Court Greenlights Texas’s Redrawn Congressional Map

The U.S. Supreme Court on Monday reversed a lower-court ruling that had blocked Texas from using its new congressional map, cementing lines engineered to give Republicans control of 30 of the state’s 38 districts — up from 25 under the 2021 map. The redistricting plan, signed by Governor Greg Abbott in August 2025, had been challenged as racially gerrymandered after a three-judge panel found “substantial evidence” of discrimination. Justices Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson dissented. Attorney General Ken Paxton hailed the ruling as a victory; civil-rights groups including MALDEF warned it could disenfranchise Latino voters across the state. Ongoing: this is the second major redistricting ruling this cycle, following similar fights in Florida, Louisiana, and Ohio.

Texas Tribune, Al Jazeera, SCOTUSblog · April 27, 2026

Diplomatic Moment

King Charles Tells Congress the “Special Relationship” Must Endure

King Charles III became only the second British monarch to address a joint meeting of Congress on Monday, delivering a 20-minute speech that acknowledged recent US-UK friction over the Iran conflict while framing the alliance as “a story of reconciliation, renewal, and remarkable partnership” born from the “bitter divisions of 250 years ago.” The address, timed to the 250th anniversary of American independence, called for generosity of spirit and deeper mutual understanding. Behind the scenes, royal aides told POLITICO that King Charles planned to raise the Iran issue privately with President Trump at a subsequent state dinner. The visit comes as transatlantic relations face their deepest strain in decades, with British lawmakers openly questioning the trajectory of the alliance.

CNN, NBC News, ITV News, CBS News · April 28, 2026

of Pennsylvania voters oppose a data center in their community

A Quinnipiac survey has found that opposition to AI data centers in Pennsylvania crosses party lines, with 53 percent of Republicans joining environmentalists, farmers, and affordability voters in rejecting new facilities. The backlash is especially dangerous for GOP incumbents in competitive districts: Representatives Ryan Mackenzie, Rob Bresnahan, Brian Fitzpatrick, and Scott Perry face constituent fury over a 21.7 percent jump in electricity rates in 2025 — a spike partially attributed to the insatiable energy demands of data centers. State lawmakers are now considering a three-year moratorium on “hyperscale” data centers exceeding 5,000 servers. Ongoing: this cycle has seen data-center opposition grow from a local NIMBY complaint into a potent midterm issue affecting toss-up races in at least a half-dozen states.

Deepfake Ads Are Now Official Campaign Strategy

Ongoing: deepfake deployment in the 2026 cycle · New development: at least five confirmed incidents across three states

When the National Republican Senatorial Committee released an online ad featuring a fabricated version of Democratic candidate James Talarico appearing to speak directly into the camera for more than a minute, it was not an accident, a rogue operative, or a dark-web experiment. It was strategy. The Talarico deepfake — which used AI to put the candidate’s old social-media posts into his own mouth, on screen, in his likeness — represents the clearest evidence yet that synthetic political advertising has moved from the margins to the mainstream of American campaign practice.

At least five confirmed deepfake incidents have appeared in the 2026 midterms, spanning Texas, Georgia, Massachusetts, and Maine, where a separate Republican ad used AI-generated imagery of Democratic Senate candidate Graham Platner. Survey data from the cycle shows nearly 50 percent of voters say deepfakes had “some influence” on their election decisions, even as most claim to distrust the technology — a cognitive dissonance that researchers call the “liar’s dividend,” in which the mere existence of deepfakes erodes trust in all video evidence.

The legal landscape offers little protection. There is no federal regulation governing the use of AI in political advertising, and the existing patchwork of 28 state-level laws remains largely untested in court. California’s 2024 attempt to prohibit deepfake political ads was struck down by a federal judge as a First Amendment violation, setting a chilling precedent for enforcement nationwide. Senator Amy Klobuchar has pressed tech platforms to crack down before the midterms, but compliance remains voluntary.

CNN, The American Prospect, OECD AI Incident Monitor, Bangor Daily News · March–April 2026

GovTech

Alabama Names Its First Chief Artificial Intelligence Officer

Alabama Governor Kay Ivey has appointed Aaron Wright — a 25-year veteran of state and health-care IT — as the state’s inaugural Chief AI Officer, housed within the Alabama Office of Information Technology. Wright previously led the Data Management and Ownership working group in Ivey’s Generative AI Task Force. His mandate: coordinate AI adoption across executive-branch agencies, set guardrails for responsible deployment, and report to the Technology Quality Assurance Board Ivey created in February to oversee emerging technologies. The appointment makes Alabama one of a growing number of states formalizing AI governance roles as the technology spreads through procurement, permitting, and constituent services.

GovTech, StateScoop, Birmingham Free Press · April 28, 2026

Policy Watch

A New Conservative Coalition Demands Tougher AI Rules

A coalition of conservative groups and advocates for stronger children’s online safety and AI regulation launched this month, challenging the assumption that the American right is uniformly hostile to technology regulation. Brad Littlejohn, policy director at American Compass, argues the White House’s light-touch AI framework is a “major political risk” that could alienate social conservatives concerned about technology’s impact on families and working-class populists skeptical of Big Tech. The launch coincides with the administration’s rollout of a new executive order on AI that explicitly seeks to preempt state regulation — a move that has drawn objections from Republican state attorneys general as well as progressive groups, creating an unusual cross-ideological alliance that could reshape the midterm debate over tech governance.

Axios, Time · March–April 2026

Voters Are Using AI as a Political Advisor — and That’s a Problem

A new study from Stanford’s Freeman Spogli Institute finds that a growing share of American voters are turning to large language models — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and others — for guidance on how to vote, which candidates to support, and how to interpret political events. The researchers warn that these models exhibit measurable partisan lean, with both Republicans and Democrats perceiving a left-leaning slant in most commercial LLMs. The concern is not that any single chatbot will swing an election, but that habitual reliance on AI for political judgment erodes the deliberative reasoning that democratic participation requires. A separate Stanford study found that perceived bias in AI models may itself become a politicized issue, with conservative media increasingly framing LLMs as instruments of progressive ideology.

Stanford Freeman Spogli Institute, Stanford Report · 2026

AI in Politics

Trump’s AI Executive Order Splits His Own Party

President Trump’s executive order establishing a federal AI governance framework has exposed a rift within the Republican coalition that party leaders did not anticipate. The order’s most contentious provision — an explicit preemption of state AI regulations — aligns the administration with Silicon Valley accelerationists, defense-tech investors, and large platform companies who argue the United States cannot afford a fragmented state-by-state regime while China races ahead. But it has drawn sharp pushback from Republican state attorneys general, traditional social conservatives, and working-class populists who see it as a giveaway to the same tech companies many Trump voters distrust. The AI regulation fight in the NDAA talks has further complicated the picture, with some GOP lawmakers openly breaking from the White House to support stronger guardrails.

Time, The Hill, Axios · March–April 2026

Political Psychology

Data-Center Protests Scramble Political Lines Across America

The backlash against AI data centers has gone national, and it is scrambling traditional political alignments in ways that neither party fully anticipated. Opponents delayed or blocked 48 data-center projects in 2025, affecting $156 billion in potential investment, according to Data Center Watch. The opposition draws from an unlikely coalition: environmentalists worried about water and energy consumption, farmers fighting the loss of agricultural land, and conservative homeowners furious about rising electricity bills. At least a dozen states have introduced bills to restrict, tax, or impose moratoriums on new hyperscale facilities. The phenomenon has turned infrastructure policy — once a bipartisan safe space — into a volatile midterm issue that defies the usual left-right mapping.

NPR, Axios, CNBC · January–April 2026

Should Federal AI Rules Override the States?

The Progressive Case: States Must Protect What Washington Won’t

With no comprehensive federal AI legislation in sight, states have become the primary laboratory for AI governance — passing 28 laws across jurisdictions covering deepfakes, hiring algorithms, and automated decision-making. Progressive advocates argue that preempting these laws would leave consumers, workers, and voters unprotected while the federal government remains gridlocked. They point to California’s privacy law and Illinois’s biometric-data protections as models that only existed because states acted first.

The ACLU, state-level consumer groups, and several Democratic attorneys general have filed formal objections to the executive order’s preemption clause, arguing it prioritizes corporate convenience over democratic accountability.

ACLU, Brookings Institution, Biometric Update · 2026

The Conservative Case: A Patchwork Regime Undermines Competitiveness

Proponents of federal preemption argue that a fragmented state-by-state regulatory landscape imposes crippling compliance costs on American AI companies at the precise moment they need to outpace Chinese competitors. The Chamber of Commerce and major tech lobbying groups contend that 28 different state AI laws create an unnavigable maze that benefits only lawyers and incumbent platforms with the resources to comply. They cite the European Union’s single-market approach as evidence that regulatory coherence and innovation can coexist.

Several Republican senators and the administration’s own AI policy office have argued that uniform federal standards would actually enable stronger enforcement than the current patchwork allows.

Chamber of Commerce, R Street Institute, The Hill · 2026

Political Tech Application

EyesOver + Campaign Nucleus

What it does: EyesOver is a real-time political intelligence platform that uses AI to monitor social media, news outlets, and polling data, producing daily sentiment tracking, issue-trend identification, and predictive analytics. Its newly announced integration with Campaign Nucleus — a campaign management platform used by Republican and conservative campaigns — connects real-time public-sentiment data directly to campaign execution workflows, allowing operatives to move from insight to voter contact within a single interface.

Who is using it: The EyesOver-Campaign Nucleus stack is being marketed primarily to center-right campaigns for the 2026 midterms. Campaign Nucleus already provides tools for fundraising, email, and volunteer management; the EyesOver layer adds the EyesOver Support Index, which claims to deliver real-time public-support metrics at a fraction of the cost of traditional polling.

Reported outcome: EyesOver claims its AI detects new language patterns emerging in political conversations before they surface in mainstream polls, giving campaigns an early-warning system for shifting voter sentiment. No independent audit of accuracy has been published.

> takeaway: The integration of real-time sentiment AI with campaign execution tools collapses the gap between listening and acting — a structural advantage for campaigns that can afford the stack, and a potential equity concern for those that cannot.

EyesOver, Campaign Nucleus · campaignnucleus.com · 2026
