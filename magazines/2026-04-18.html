No. 01 · Political Technology

The AI money war reaches the midterm ad market.

Leading the Future now sits on more than $51M. A parallel Trump-aligned vehicle promises $100M more. AI has become the best-funded sleeper issue of the 2026 cycle.

Who is writing the cheques. Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz added $25M to Leading the Future this week. The pro-AI super PAC's total haul across entities is now north of $140M for the cycle, per filings reported by Bloomberg and Quartz.

What it is buying. The committee is building air cover for House and Senate members who oppose state-level AI rules and support a pre-emptive federal framework — the same blueprint Trump released as a policy roadmap in March.

The other side. Anthropic sent $20M to Public First Action in February, arguing — unusually for a frontier lab — that regulation is lagging the risk. The AI industry is not one voter.

No. 02 · The machinery

The election-security office is being quietly unfunded — one internship at a time.

CISA has now been operating without appropriations for two months. It cannot legally spend on anything beyond salaries. Non-payroll costs require an Antideficiency Act exception.

The agency has cancelled its CyberCorps Scholarship-for-Service summer class for the second consecutive year — up to one hundred undergraduate and postgraduate cyber interns who would have rotated through federal networks will not, this summer, rotate through anything. Town-hall sessions for the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act have been postponed indefinitely. Staff travel is frozen.

The president's FY27 request, released last week, goes further: it explicitly reduces the budget for the election-security mission, stakeholder engagement, and infrastructure-protection partnerships. Votebeat and the Brennan Center report that state election officials describe the working relationship with CISA as "broken" — and that some now see federal engagement as a threat to their independence rather than a resource.

This is happening seven months before the first Tuesday in November.

No. 05 · Pew Research · Public Opinion

A reform majority now, on both sides of the aisle — and nobody's confident it can happen.

By the numbers · fieldwork March 2026

Seventy-seven percent of Americans now say the political system needs major changes or complete reform, according to Pew's cross-national survey released this week. The share is the highest of any of the twelve wealthy democracies Pew surveyed — despite the United States still having the highest GDP per capita in the set.

Dissatisfaction with how democracy is working rose to 69% in a separate March survey, a seven-point jump from 2025. Democrats remain overwhelmingly dissatisfied — 86% to 13%. Republicans split almost evenly: 51% dissatisfied, 48% satisfied. The partisan split on the mechanics of democracy is now narrower than the split on almost any policy question.

What makes the Pew work distinctive this cycle is the taxonomy. Americans break roughly in thirds: 49% of respondents are what Pew calls "pessimistic reformers" — they think the system needs major change but don't believe the change is coming; 27% are "optimistic reformers" who think it can happen; 23% think the system is fine as is, or needs only minor tweaks.

Put differently: three-quarters of the country wants the system to look different, and most of that three-quarters has given up on it getting there. That is the electorate the midterms are being fought over.

Sources: Pew Research Center, "Americans stand out internationally for their pessimism about the nation's political system" (15 April 2026); "Multiple indicators show a decline in the health of America's democracy" (15 April 2026). Fieldwork conducted March 2026, n≈3,600 US adults.
No. 06 · By the Numbers

Nearly half of voters say a deepfake influenced their decision this cycle — even as most claim to distrust the medium.

Cycle survey data reported across The Japan Times, Honolulu Star-Advertiser and The Express Tribune found that close to half of American voters believe AI-generated content has had some effect on their 2026 election choices. That number is self-reported, which is exactly the point: even voters who swear they don't trust synthetic media are telling pollsters it has reached them.

It sits next to another number. In an AccuPay survey cited by the same reporting, synthetic political content now reaches an estimated two in three voters across digital channels during a competitive race. Reach is no longer the bottleneck. Attribution is.

No. 07 · Global Elections · Budapest dispatch
Budapest · 12 April

After sixteen years: Orbán concedes.

Péter Magyar's Tisza party is projected to take a two-thirds parliamentary majority. The victory speech did not mention Vladimir Putin, Ursula von der Leyen, or Donald Trump.

The campaign that changed Hungary ran on healthcare, not geopolitics.

Magyar, a former Fidesz insider turned insurgent, built a coalition out of small-town dissatisfaction with stagnating wages, deteriorating hospitals, and corruption fatigue. CNN and Al Jazeera both note how little he said, during the campaign, about the EU or the war in Ukraine — the issues that have defined Orbán's image abroad for a decade.

For European capitals, the result removes the most consistent obstacle inside the European Council to coordinated policy on Ukraine, migration, and sanctions. For Washington, it is a setback: President Trump endorsed Orbán during the campaign.

For political-tech watchers, the more interesting finding is that Magyar's insurgent apparatus ran a largely unglamorous, door-to-door, data-led ground game with only modest digital spend — the opposite of the playbook being sold in US consultant decks.

"He has, from the minute he hit the trail, campaigned on kitchen-table issues — Hungary's stagnating economy and its poor health care — and almost nothing else."

The Weekend Read.
Edition of 18 April 2026 · By the desk

The world's democracy scoreboard stabilises. America keeps sliding down it.

The Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index 2025, released this month, shows the first broad global improvement since 2017. The United States is the largest exception in the dataset — and the reasons are domestic, not geopolitical.

The headline

After eight consecutive years of worsening scores, the EIU Democracy Index 2025 registered "one of the biggest increases" in the global index since 2012. The movement is modest — a handful of countries nudging from "flawed" to "full" — but the direction is new. Democracy, measured globally, is not currently getting worse.

The United States is the most-cited exception. Its overall score fell 0.2 points to 7.65, dropping it six places to thirty-fourth in the ranking. It remains where it has sat since 2016: in the "flawed democracies" category, below Chile, Mauritius, and the Czech Republic.

What drove the fall

The EIU attributes the decline to two specific sub-indices: "functioning of government" and "civil liberties." Staff reductions across the federal civil service, politically inflected dismissals, and the stalled appropriations cycle all depress the first. The civil-liberties sub-index picked up reductions in press access, restrictions on federal research data release, and the legal status of certain protest activity.

None of these is a single dramatic event. That is, in the EIU's framing, the point: the index is picking up cumulative wear.

What the public thinks

The EIU's quantitative findings align with Pew's public opinion data from this week. Sixty-nine percent of Americans say they are dissatisfied with how democracy is working. Seventy-seven percent say the political system needs major changes or complete reform — the highest share among any advanced democracy Pew surveyed.

The optimism gap is the unusual finding. Americans are more likely than Europeans to want sweeping change, and less likely than Europeans to believe it will happen. Half of the country is what Pew labels a "pessimistic reformer."

The technology layer

Neither the EIU nor Pew treats artificial intelligence as an index component — yet. The Atlantic Council's geopolitics brief this month argues that the next edition will have to: synthetic media, AI-driven voter simulation, and automated content pipelines affect at least three of the EIU's five sub-indices directly, and two indirectly.

The Carnegie Endowment has made a parallel argument from the opposite direction: AI is as likely to expand representative participation as to constrict it. Translation interfaces, constituent-response systems, and synthetic deliberation tools are all being deployed by democratic governments this year. They are simply less visible than the deepfake ads.

Where this lands

A country where 77% of voters want systemic change, where the election-security agency is unfunded, and where the incumbent party is running deepfake ads against senators — is still, per the EIU, thirty-fourth out of the world's democracies. Not first. Not bottom. Somewhere specific. Worth marking.
