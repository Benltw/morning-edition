Research Insights of the Day

Parasocial Politicians

Political Parasocial Relationships on Social Media: The Role of User Experiences and Emotions in Shaping eWOM and Voting Intention
Published in Telematics and Informatics Reports, 2025 · ScienceDirect
Politicians who share personal narratives, unscripted moments, and behind-the-scenes content on social media are not just building followings — they are building parasocial relationships, the psychological one-way bonds audiences form with media figures. A new study examining voter behavior during the 2024 Indonesian presidential election finds that these bonds drive political action more powerfully than policy agreement alone. Voters who perceived homophily — a sense that the candidate was "like them" — and who had repeated relationship experiences with a political figure's content reported higher emotional engagement, stronger electronic word-of-mouth activity, and ultimately firmer voting intention. Pleasure, not anger or anxiety, emerged as the strongest mediating emotion linking social media experience to the decision to vote for a candidate.
Practitioner Implication

Campaigns investing in parasocial content strategies — personal storytelling, direct-to-camera formats, and perceived authenticity — may see higher returns in voter mobilization than those focused solely on policy messaging or paid advertising.

Source: Telematics and Informatics Reports, ScienceDirect, 2025

AI in Politics

The Town That Voted Against the Algorithm

In Festus, Missouri, voters ousted every incumbent council member who had approved a $6 billion hyperscale data center on 360 acres of community land. In nearby Independence, two more council members who voted for tax breaks exceeding $6 billion for a Nebius AI data center were similarly removed. The backlash was organized through a grassroots campaign called Stop The AI Data Center, which coordinated both online and in-person efforts to mobilize voters concerned about eminent domain, skyrocketing electricity costs, and a lack of transparency in the approval process. The results represent a new phenomenon in American politics: communities using democratic power to push back against the physical infrastructure of artificial intelligence, turning local elections into referendums on the tech industry's expansion.

Election Security

Twenty-Nine States, Twenty-Nine Lawsuits

The Justice Department has sued twenty-nine states and the District of Columbia for refusing to hand over unredacted voter rolls — including driver's license and partial Social Security numbers. At the center of the fight is SAVE, a DHS database that has flagged 21,000 registered voters as potential non-citizens out of 60 million names submitted — a rate of 0.035 percent. The DOJ's key privacy officer resigned in April as plans were finalized to share state voter data with DHS. Only twelve states have fully complied. Thirty-one states and D.C. have refused outright. Legal scholars note the Constitution is clear: states, not the federal government, hold primary authority over election administration. Three early federal court rulings have gone against the DOJ.

The Proxy War in New York's Twelfth

The contest to replace retiring Representative Jerry Nadler in New York's 12th Congressional District has become the first true proxy fight between rival AI factions. New York Assembly member Alex Bores, the chief architect of a suite of state AI regulatory bills, is the target: Leading the Future, backed by OpenAI co-founder Greg Brockman and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale, has spent $1.1 million in TV ads and mailers to defeat him. On the other side, Anthropic's $20 million contribution to Public First Action is backing candidates like Bores who support guardrails. The total AI-linked spending in this single House primary has already exceeded $2.4 million — in a district where the eventual winner will help write federal AI law for a decade. Both OpenAI and Anthropic are reportedly planning IPOs, making the regulatory stakes existential.

Ongoing: Hungary Transition
Global Elections

Hungary's First Hundred Hours

Six days after Peter Magyar's Tisza party won a two-thirds supermajority with 52.1 percent of the party-list vote and 136 of 199 parliamentary seats, Hungary's incoming government is moving at a pace its predecessor never permitted. Magyar has publicly demanded the resignation of President Tamas Sulyok, calling him "unfit to serve as the guardian of legality" and "unworthy of representing the unity of the Hungarian nation." He has announced a target date of May 5 for the new government to assume office.

The scale of Fidesz's collapse is historically unusual. Viktor Orban's party fell from 135 seats to 57 — a loss of 78 — on turnout of 79.6 percent, the highest since Hungary's democratic transition in 1990. Magyar's constitutional supermajority gives him the power to amend the constitution and reshape the institutional architecture that Orban spent sixteen years constructing: the courts, state media, the prosecution service, and the election commission.

In a press conference covered by Euronews on April 15, Magyar signaled that media reform would come first. "Every Hungarian deserves a public service media that broadcasts the truth," he said. European allies, cautious after years of dealing with Orban's blocking tactics in Brussels, are watching to see whether Magyar's rhetoric translates into structural change — or whether the gravitational pull of Hungary's captured institutions proves stronger than a single election.

09 / Political Technology Hero Gradient
Political Technology

Twenty-Eight States, One Constitutional Question

Since 2022, 170 state laws have been enacted targeting AI deepfake technology, with 146 bills introduced in 2025 alone. Twenty-eight states now require political advertisements containing AI-generated content to carry disclosure labels. But the legal ground is shifting beneath these laws: a federal judge struck down portions of California's AB 2839 in August 2025, finding that key provisions conflicted with Section 230 and the First Amendment. The tension is structural: deepfake disclosure mandates are content-based speech regulations, which face strict scrutiny under the First Amendment. Lawmakers are now looking beyond punishing individual deepfake creators toward regulating the platforms that enable production — generative AI services, hosting platforms, and cloud providers. The question is whether any regulatory architecture can survive the constitutional tests that are already underway.

10 / GovTech Dark / Midnight
GovTech

The White House Says No New Regulator

On March 20, the White House released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence — and made a definitive choice: no new federal rulemaking body. Instead, AI governance will be distributed across existing regulatory agencies with subject-matter expertise, supplemented by industry-led standards. The framework also recommends that Congress preempt state AI laws that "impose undue burdens," aiming for a single national standard rather than fifty competing ones. For the AI industry, the framework validates the deregulatory bet that companies like OpenAI and its investors have made through Leading the Future. For state legislators who have spent two years building deepfake disclosure laws, voting-AI guardrails, and algorithmic accountability requirements, it is a direct threat. The framework sets the terms for what Congress will debate through the rest of the year.
Sources: Consumer Finance Monitor, Baker Botts, CFR · April 2026

Republican Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina and Democratic Senator Jeanne Shaheen of New Hampshire, co-chairs of the Senate NATO Observer Group, issued a joint statement in April directly challenging President Trump's threats to withdraw from NATO. The statement came as Trump called NATO members "cowards" amid pressure to support the U.S. engagement in Iran. The bipartisan rebuke represents a rare instance of Republican foreign-policy leadership publicly breaking with a sitting president of their own party.

The Question: Who Should Regulate Artificial Intelligence?

The Case for Aggressive State and Federal Regulation

Illinois State Senator Mary Edly-Allen (D) warned that the consequences of inaction are already visible: "If we got social media wrong, and we did, we cannot afford to get AI wrong." Proponents of strong regulation argue that the White House framework's reliance on industry self-governance and existing agencies is inadequate for a technology moving faster than any regulator can follow. Anthropic's $20 million contribution to Public First Action backs this position, supporting candidates who favor enforceable guardrails on AI deployment, algorithmic transparency, and worker protections.

Sources: Capitol News Illinois, CNBC, The Fulcrum · April 2026

The Case for Light-Touch Federal Preemption

The White House framework explicitly recommends against creating any new federal rulemaking body, calling instead for AI to be governed through existing agencies with subject-matter expertise. Backers of this approach — including Leading the Future's investors, such as Andreessen Horowitz and Palantir co-founder Joe Lonsdale — argue that a patchwork of fifty state laws would strangle innovation and drive AI development overseas. Their position: establish a single, minimally burdensome national standard, let industry lead on safety standards, and preserve America's competitive advantage in the global AI race.

Political Tech Application

Quiller AI / Grassroots Analytics

What it does: Quiller is a generative-AI platform built for progressive campaigns and nonprofits. It produces first-draft fundraising emails, SMS blasts, press releases, op-eds, and digital ad copy in under 20 seconds. Organizations train a private workspace on their own talking points, platform positions, and writing samples, then generate unlimited drafts calibrated to their voice.

Who is using it: Grassroots Analytics, a data-segmentation firm serving Democratic campaigns, acquired Quiller in early 2026. The combined platform merges AI-generated content with donor-targeting data, creating an end-to-end fundraising pipeline. Quiller 2.0 is now operational as a standalone tool with a human support hotline, and deeper data integrations are planned for later this cycle.

Reported outcome: Quiller produces copy that users describe as "70 percent of the way done" — a first draft requiring human editing for tone and strategy but eliminating the blank-page problem that consumes hours of campaign staff time. The Grassroots Analytics merger aims to close the loop between who to ask and what to say.
The acquisition signals that the next wave of campaign AI isn't about generating content or targeting donors — it's about merging both into a single workflow. Expect the right-leaning equivalent within the cycle.
