The AI money war reaches the midterm ad market.

Leading the Future now sits on more than $51M. A parallel Trump-aligned vehicle promises $100M more. AI has become the best-funded sleeper issue of the 2026 cycle.

Bloomberg CNBC Quartz
By the desk · 18.04.2026

Who is writing the cheques. Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz added $25M to Leading the Future this week. The pro-AI super PAC's total haul across entities is now north of $140M for the cycle, per filings reported by Bloomberg and Quartz.

What it is buying. The committee is building air cover for House and Senate members who oppose state-level AI rules and support a pre-emptive federal framework — the same blueprint Trump released as a policy roadmap in March.

The other side. Anthropic sent $20M to Public First Action in February, arguing — unusually for a frontier lab — that regulation is lagging the risk. The AI industry is not one voter.

GovTech · Election Security

The election-security office is being quietly unfunded — one internship at a time.

CISA has now been operating without appropriations for two months. It cannot legally spend on anything beyond salaries.

Nextgov Votebeat Brennan
DHS dark since 14 Feb

Non-payroll costs require an Antideficiency Act exception. The agency has cancelled its CyberCorps Scholarship-for-Service summer class for the second consecutive year — up to one hundred undergraduate and postgraduate cyber interns who would have rotated through federal networks will not.

Town-hall sessions for the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act have been postponed indefinitely. Staff travel is frozen. The president's FY27 request, released last week, goes further: it explicitly reduces the budget for the election-security mission, stakeholder engagement, and infrastructure-protection partnerships.

Votebeat and the Brennan Center report that state election officials describe the working relationship with CISA as "broken" — and that some now see federal engagement as a threat to their independence rather than a resource. This is happening seven months before the first Tuesday in November.

AI in Politics · Synthetic Media

The deepfake ad is no longer an outlier. It is a line item.

At least fifteen campaign ads featuring AI-generated content have aired since November, per NBC News and Bloomberg tallies. Two dropped this month alone.

CNN Bangor Daily NCSL
Synthetic media beat
Alert · Synthetic Media

The 2026 cycle is the first US midterm in which AI-generated ads have moved from rumour to standard operating procedure inside a national party committee.

Maine · US Senate

A minute of a candidate who never spoke.

The NRSC's spot against Democrat Graham Platner used an AI-generated version of him to deliver attacks the real candidate never gave. It ran full length — unusual for synthetic content, which normally surfaces in fragments.

Texas · US Senate

Talarico, in words that were never his.

Last month's NRSC deepfake of James Talarico has drawn a complaint to the FEC. CNN reported the spot as "the latest in a series" from the national GOP committee in the past year.

Regulation

A patchwork, growing by the month.

Twenty-six states now regulate AI-generated political speech in some form — up from five in 2023. There is still no federal framework. The Trump AI roadmap would pre-empt the state laws entirely.

Republicans now poll imagined voters.

The American Association of Political Consultants reports 64% of Republican operatives use AI daily; 49% of Democrats do. Aaru and MiroFish sell synthetic panels as panel-replacements.

Global Elections · Budapest dispatch

After sixteen years: Orbán concedes.

Péter Magyar's Tisza party is projected to take a two-thirds parliamentary majority. The victory speech did not mention Vladimir Putin, Ursula von der Leyen, or Donald Trump.

CNN Al Jazeera
Budapest · 12.04.2026
Budapest · 12 April

The campaign ran on healthcare, not geopolitics.

"He has, from the minute he hit the trail, campaigned on kitchen-table issues — Hungary's stagnating economy and its poor health care — and almost nothing else." — CNN, 12 April 2026

Magyar, a former Fidesz insider turned insurgent, built a coalition out of small-town dissatisfaction with stagnating wages, deteriorating hospitals, and corruption fatigue. CNN and Al Jazeera both note how little he said, during the campaign, about the EU or the war in Ukraine.

For European capitals, the result removes the most consistent obstacle inside the European Council to coordinated policy on Ukraine, migration, and sanctions. For Washington, it is a setback: President Trump endorsed Orbán during the campaign.

For political-tech watchers, the more interesting finding is that Magyar's insurgent apparatus ran a largely unglamorous, door-to-door, data-led ground game with only modest digital spend — the opposite of the playbook being sold in US consultant decks.

The Weekend Read · Democracy

The world's democracy scoreboard stabilises. America keeps sliding down it.

The Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index 2025, released this month, shows the first broad global improvement since 2017. The United States is the largest exception in the dataset.

EIU Pew Atlantic Council
Long read · 5 min

After eight consecutive years of worsening scores, the EIU Democracy Index 2025 registered "one of the biggest increases" in the global index since 2012. The movement is modest — a handful of countries nudging from "flawed" to "full" — but the direction is new. Democracy, measured globally, is not currently getting worse.

The United States is the most-cited exception. Its overall score fell 0.2 points to 7.65, dropping it six places to thirty-fourth in the ranking. It remains where it has sat since 2016: in the "flawed democracies" category, below Chile, Mauritius, and the Czech Republic.

What drove the fall

The EIU attributes the decline to two specific sub-indices: "functioning of government" and "civil liberties." Staff reductions across the federal civil service, politically inflected dismissals, and the stalled appropriations cycle all depress the first. The civil-liberties sub-index picked up reductions in press access, restrictions on federal research data release, and the legal status of certain protest activity. None of these is a single dramatic event. That is, in the EIU's framing, the point: the index is picking up cumulative wear.

What the public thinks

The EIU's quantitative findings align with Pew's public opinion data from this week. Sixty-nine percent of Americans say they are dissatisfied with how democracy is working. Seventy-seven percent say the political system needs major changes or complete reform — the highest share among any advanced democracy Pew surveyed.

The technology layer

Neither the EIU nor Pew treats artificial intelligence as an index component — yet. The Atlantic Council's geopolitics brief this month argues that the next edition will have to: synthetic media, AI-driven voter simulation, and automated content pipelines affect at least three of the EIU's five sub-indices directly, and two indirectly. Carnegie has made the parallel argument from the opposite direction: AI is as likely to expand representative participation as to constrict it.

Where this lands

A country where 77% of voters want systemic change, where the election-security agency is unfunded, and where the incumbent party is running deepfake ads against senators — is still, per the EIU, thirty-fourth out of the world's democracies. Not first. Not bottom. Somewhere specific. Worth marking.
