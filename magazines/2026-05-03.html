Research Insights of the Day

Political Parasocial Relationships on Social Media: The Role of User Experiences and Emotions in Shaping eWOM and Voting Intention

Hidayanto, A. N., Fitriani, H., Khairunnisa, A. A., Aurelya, K., Kusumo, M. D. & Steven · Telematics and Informatics Reports · 2025

Politicians who cultivate one-sided but emotionally charged bonds with followers on social media can measurably shift voting intention, according to a study of Indonesian voters published in Telematics and Informatics Reports. The researchers modelled how three types of user experience — informational value, perceived homophily, and relationship closeness — trigger emotional responses that cascade into electronic word-of-mouth and, ultimately, ballot-box behaviour. Pleasure emerged as the strongest emotional mediator: voters who felt genuine enjoyment interacting with a politician's posts were significantly more likely to share that content and to support the candidate. The finding cuts against the common assumption that anger and outrage are the dominant political fuels on social media.

Practitioner implication: Campaign social teams should prioritise content that generates pleasure and perceived closeness — behind-the-scenes moments, candid humour, direct replies — over pure outrage bait. The parasocial bond, not the hot take, is what converts scrollers into advocates.

Source: ScienceDirect · Telematics and Informatics Reports · 2025

Trump Declares Iran Hostilities “Terminated” at the War Powers Deadline

In a letter to congressional leaders on May 1, President Trump asserted that "the hostilities that began on February 28, 2026, have terminated," citing the absence of direct fire between U.S. and Iranian forces since April 7. The declaration landed on the exact day the War Powers Resolution would have required him to seek congressional authorisation or withdraw. Democrats immediately challenged the claim, arguing that the ongoing naval blockade in the Strait of Hormuz still constitutes hostilities and that the legal clock has not reset. The Senate voted 47–50 the same day to reject a bipartisan war powers resolution. Pentagon officials put the conflict's price tag at $25 billion, and with crude hovering near $105 a barrel, the political fallout is already baked into midterm messaging on both sides. Trump separately told reporters he was "not satisfied" with a new Iranian peace proposal, leaving the diplomatic path uncertain even as he insists the war is over.

Sources: Axios · Washington Post · PBS News

Louisiana Suspends House Primaries After Supreme Court Redistricting Ruling

Republican Governor Jeff Landry issued an executive order Thursday suspending Louisiana's May 16 closed-party primaries for all six U.S. House seats "until July 15, 2026 or until such time as determined by the Legislature." The move came one day after the Supreme Court declared the state's existing congressional map an unconstitutional racial gerrymander in Callais v. Landry, giving the legislature room to redraw boundaries that could eliminate a majority-Black district created under the Voting Rights Act. Absentee voting was already underway and early in-person voting had been scheduled to start this weekend. Democrats have filed legal challenges to restore the original timeline, arguing the governor lacks the authority to unilaterally cancel federal elections. Louisiana Secretary of State Nancy Landry confirmed that all other races, including the state's hotly contested Senate primary, will proceed on schedule.

Sources: NBC News · NPR · CNN

Political Campaigning · Ongoing: Redistricting

Democrats Plot Sweeping 2028 Redistricting Counteroffensive

Emboldened — and alarmed — by the Supreme Court's gutting of the Voting Rights Act, House Democrats are mapping an aggressive mid-decade redistricting campaign targeting blue and purple states ahead of 2028. House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries told Axios that "all options are on the table" in New York, Illinois, Maryland, Colorado, and California. Pete Aguilar, the House Democratic caucus chair, said his home state of California "will respond just like we responded to Texas." The strategy is an explicit tit-for-tat: if Republican-controlled legislatures in the South and Sun Belt exploit the Callais ruling to eliminate minority-opportunity districts, Democrats will draw maximally favourable maps in the states they control. Brookings analysts estimate the net effect could shift 12 to 18 House seats across both parties before a single vote is cast in 2028, turning redistricting into the dominant structural battleground of the next cycle.

Sources: Axios · Brookings · Democracy Docket

India Purges 9 Million Voters From Rolls Before State Elections

More than nine million names have been culled from West Bengal's electoral roll alone, with millions more deleted nationwide, just weeks before state elections that will determine whether the ruling BJP can expand into India's south and east. The Election Commission of India says the clean-up targets duplications, deceased registrants, and data errors, but researchers and opposition leaders point to a starkly disproportionate impact on Muslim-majority districts in West Bengal, where nearly a third of the 90-million-strong population is Muslim. Al Jazeera, CNN, and the Christian Science Monitor have all reported that religion appears to be a significant factor in the deletions. Critics accuse the ECI of acting at the behest of the BJP to weaken minority representation. Election results are expected in early May, but millions of voters remain in limbo, uncertain whether they will be allowed to cast a ballot.

Sources: CNN · Al Jazeera · CS Monitor

Political Campaigning · Ongoing: Redistricting

Colorado’s Dueling Ballot Measures Ignite a Redistricting Arms Race

Colorado has become the sharpest test case for mid-decade redistricting after the progressive group Coloradans for a Level Playing Field filed three linked ballot initiatives — 240, 241, and 242 — that would temporarily bypass the state's independent redistricting commission to draw a new congressional map for the 2028 and 2030 elections. Their explicit goal: convert the current 4–4 partisan split into seven Democratic-leaning districts and one safe Republican seat, then restore the commission after the 2030 census. The conservative dark-money group Advance Colorado fired back with counter-measures 252 and 256, both constitutional amendments that would require any mid-decade map to be approved by both the redistricting commission and the state Supreme Court, and would bar maps "drawn purposefully to favour one political party." The duelling proposals set up a November ballot clash that could define whether voter-created independent commissions survive the post-Callais landscape — or become collateral damage in a national gerrymandering escalation. Initiative 240, as a constitutional amendment, would need 55% of the vote; the paired statutory measures only need a simple majority, but both must pass to take effect.

Sources: Colorado Newsline · Colorado Politics

Political Technology

Higher Ground Labs Bets on “Protected Civic Tech” for the Midterms

As federal pressure on state-run election infrastructure intensifies, the progressive political-tech incubator Higher Ground Labs is steering its new Fund V toward what it calls "protected civic tech" — voter registration tools, GOTV platforms, and election-protection systems designed to remain operational even under hostile federal oversight. Fund V also backs data-sovereignty solutions that give campaigns genuine control over their own information, resisting federal demands to hand over voter contact records. Alongside the investment vehicle, HGL's institute arm is running a Campaign Manager Innovation Cohort and a series of political simulations, stress-testing how campaigns would function if key digital infrastructure were disrupted. The organisation's Progressive CRM Guide, released earlier this year, has already been used by roughly 1,000 campaign and advocacy teams to audit their tech stacks ahead of November. The bet: in an era of unprecedented federal interference, resilient infrastructure matters more than the flashiest AI feature.

Source: Higher Ground Labs · HGL Election Disruption Series

Jeffries Declares “Maximum Warfare” on Republican Maps

House Minority Leader Hakeem Jeffries escalated the redistricting fight this week with language rarely heard from a caucus leader, telling supporters: "We are in an era of maximum warfare, everywhere, all the time." The phrase, pulled from a redistricting strategy call, was quickly stripped of context by conservative media and repackaged as a call to political violence — a charge PolitiFact rated false. Jeffries's actual target was Florida, where Governor Ron DeSantis has proposed a new congressional map that Democrats have derided as a "dummymander." Jeffries named eight Republican incumbents he said Democrats would "aggressively target for defeat" if the Florida maps go through, including Maria Elvira Salazar, Anna Paulina Luna, and Brian Mast. The rhetoric underscores a fundamental strategic shift: Democrats are no longer defending the Voting Rights Act framework as their primary redistricting tool but are instead preparing to play the same raw-power map-drawing game that Republicans have dominated for a decade.

Sources: PolitiFact · Florida Politics · Axios

Rise of AI Berlin: Europe’s Sovereignty Summit Opens Tuesday

On May 6, roughly 300 hand-picked decision-makers from business, policy, research, and investment will gather in Berlin for the tenth edition of the Rise of AI Conference, this year themed around Europe's technological sovereignty. The programme spans two stages — Meta Stage and Applied AI Stage — with keynotes from Dr. Volker Wissing, Germany's Federal Minister for Digital, Transport, and Justice, and Professor Jurgen Schmidhuber, the LSTM pioneer and NNAISENSE co-founder. The conference's significance for campaign professionals lies in the policy pipeline: decisions made at events like Rise of AI shape the regulatory environment that will govern AI use in European elections for years to come. A hybrid livestream will make the main-stage sessions available globally.

TikTok’s New American Owners Face Their First Midterm Stress Test

Since signing a deal on January 22 to sell a controlling stake in its U.S. operations to an American-led joint venture, TikTok has operated under new ownership — but old political pressures. With close to 40% of Americans aged 18 to 29 getting news regularly from the platform, the 2026 midterms are the first major election cycle in which U.S. investors hold sway over the algorithm that decides what political content surfaces. Campaigns on both sides are increasing spending on creator partnerships, but disclosure rules remain virtually nonexistent: following the money between a political committee and a content creator is, as Harvard's Berkman Klein Center put it, "nearly impossible." Meanwhile, a February 2026 Westminster Foundation for Democracy briefing warned that TikTok's recommendation-driven architecture makes it uniquely susceptible to coordinated inauthentic behaviour during election periods. The platform's new American board has yet to publish an election-integrity policy for the midterms.

Sources: Pew Research · WFD Briefing · HKS Misinformation Review

GOP Consultants Now Use AI Daily — Democrats Trail at 49%

A survey by the American Association of Political Consultants found that 64% of Republican political consultants report using artificial intelligence tools daily in their campaign work, compared to 49% of their Democratic counterparts. The gap reflects a broader cultural divergence: while the GOP has embraced AI-generated content for attack ads, fundraising personalisation, and synthetic opposition research, the DNC moved in the opposite direction earlier this year by restricting ChatGPT use on its internal systems. The disparity is most visible in the Super PAC ecosystem, where AI-driven video production — including deepfake-style ads that depict opponents in fabricated scenarios — has become a standard line item. Senator John Cornyn's campaign made headlines with an AI-generated music video depicting his opponent Ken Paxton in satirical vignettes. As AI spending in the midterms passes $70 million, the question is no longer whether campaigns will use AI but whether the party that uses it more aggressively gains a durable structural advantage.

Sources: Axios · OpenTools · Daily Signal

Should States Pursue Mid-Decade Redistricting?

The Progressive Case: Fight Fire With Fire

With the Supreme Court dismantling the Voting Rights Act's redistricting protections, Democrats argue they have no choice but to redraw maps in states they control. The alternative — unilateral Republican gerrymandering in the South — would lock in structural minority-rule for a decade. Proponents point to New York, Illinois, Maryland, and Colorado as states where new maps could offset Republican gains in Louisiana, Texas, and Florida, preserving competitive national elections until the 2030 census resets the board.

Sources: Democracy Docket · Axios

The Conservative Case: Commissions Exist for a Reason

Republicans and good-government groups counter that mid-decade redistricting — by either party — undermines the independent commissions voters created to depoliticise map-drawing. Advance Colorado's counter-ballot measures would require any new map to pass both the state's independent commission and the Supreme Court, preserving institutional guardrails. Conservatives argue that Democrats' plan to convert Colorado's 4–4 split into 7–1 is nakedly partisan and would erode public trust in the commission model nationwide.

Sources: Colorado Newsline · RedState

Political Tech Application

Solidarity Tech

What it does: An all-in-one CRM built for unions, nonprofits, and grassroots campaigns, combining calling, texting, email, websites, events, and volunteer coordination in a single platform. Founded by labour organiser and software developer Ivan Pardo, it launched in 2025 from efforts to organise rideshare drivers in California.

Who is using it: Zohran Mamdani's New York City mayoral campaign — with 95,000 volunteers, the first major U.S. race to deploy the platform. In Ireland, President-elect Catherine Connolly's campaign used Solidarity Tech via a custom WhatsApp integration to coordinate field operations.

Reported outcome: Mamdani's volunteer programme, powered by Solidarity Tech's CRM, has been credited by Campaigns & Elections as one of the largest grassroots volunteer operations in modern New York City politics.

> takeaway: The platform's labour-movement DNA — built for high-volume, low-budget organising — gives it a structural advantage in races where volunteer depth matters more than ad spend. Watch for adoption in down-ballot and state legislative races through November.

Sources: Campaigns & Elections · Solidarity Tech
