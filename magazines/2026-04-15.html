Essay · AI in Politics · 05

Twenty million
dollars to choose the
next AI regulator.

Anthropic pledged twenty million dollars to Public First Action, a super-PAC supporting candidates friendly to AI guardrails, in what may be the clearest sign yet that model labs have become first-rank political actors. The money arrives in a cycle where rival AI-industry PACs are already outspending issue groups across the Texas and North Carolina primaries. Whatever one thinks of the lab's stated mission, the precedent is plain: the producers of the technology now fund the lawmakers who will govern it, and the ads those candidates run will be shaped by tools their own benefactors build. The frame is no longer public-interest philanthropy. It is industrial policy, fought on cable and on TikTok, with the same logic as energy, banking, or defense before it. The rest of the cycle will test whether "guardrail" is a policy or a brand.

Global Elections · São Paulo · Analysis

Brazil braces for
a laboratory of lies.

Lula, Flavio Bolsonaro, WhatsApp, and a decade of muscle-memory disinformation — six months out, the October vote is already a case study.

President Lula is reportedly recalibrating his 2026 message toward an anti-elite register, a tonal shift aimed squarely at voters who carried the Bolsonaro project. Against him: Flavio Bolsonaro, an heir playing his father's hand without his father's distraction, and a movement whose ground game was always digital.

The playbook is familiar. In past cycles, pastors and politicians laundered claims on Facebook that Lula planned to close churches. False allegations that he was an anti-Christian crusader reached 142 million Twitter accounts. WhatsApp delivered a daily, forwardable drip — industrial in scale, intimate in delivery — into the phones of tens of millions of Brazilians.

Lula's response — an Office for the Defense of Democracy — has institutionalized counter-disinformation inside the executive. The judiciary has widened its jurisdiction over political speech online. Both are unpopular with a platform sector that prefers friction over rule.

What makes 2026 different is the tooling. Generative models produce campaign-grade video at negligible cost. The same WhatsApp that carried the rumors now carries AI-generated "news anchors" reading fabricated scripts. The Commission's Brussels standard will not apply. The FEC's non-intervention offers no guide. Brazil will decide its next president in the first election where synthetic media is the default medium of political speech.
